Locked out: what that actually means

Hardware wallets such as Trezor (made by SatoshiLabs) and Ledger (made by Ledger SAS) are small, dedicated devices designed to store the private keys that control cryptocurrency funds. They are protected by a PIN that you enter on the device, and optionally by a BIP39 passphrase that you remember separately from the device itself. When everything works correctly, the device is one of the most secure ways to hold cryptocurrency. When something goes wrong — a forgotten PIN, a forgotten passphrase, a misplaced seed phrase backup, a damaged device — the same security features that protect your funds from attackers are equally effective at preventing you from accessing them yourself.

"Locked out" can mean several different situations, and the implications of each are very different:

  • Forgotten PIN, seed phrase available: The device's PIN is the on-device lock. If you have your written 12, 18, or 24-word seed phrase backup, the device PIN is not actually a recovery problem — you can wipe and restore the wallet onto the same device or another compatible one using the seed.
  • Forgotten PIN, seed phrase lost: This is a genuinely critical situation. Without the seed phrase, the PIN is the only thing holding the wallet together, and after a finite number of wrong attempts the device will refuse further attempts or self-wipe. There is no legitimate brute-force or "unlock" service.
  • Forgotten BIP39 passphrase: If you used a passphrase (sometimes called a "25th word" or "hidden wallet"), the seed phrase alone restores only the empty default wallet. Without the exact passphrase, the wallet that contained your funds cannot be reconstructed. Brute-forcing a passphrase of any meaningful length is computationally infeasible.
  • Damaged or destroyed device: A physically broken Trezor or Ledger is not a recovery problem if you have your seed phrase — buy a new compatible device and restore. The funds live on the blockchain, not on the device.
  • Inherited or unknown device: A device received from a deceased relative or purchased second-hand without seed information is functionally identical to a forgotten-PIN scenario without seed access.

The most important thing to understand at the outset is this: the cryptocurrency itself is not stored on a hardware wallet. It is recorded on the blockchain at addresses that are mathematically derived from the seed phrase (and, if used, the passphrase). The hardware wallet is simply a device that stores the seed and signs transactions when you ask it to. If you can reconstruct the seed and passphrase by other means — typically from your written backup — you do not need the device at all. If you cannot reconstruct the seed and passphrase, no firm in the world can legitimately do it for you.

The PIN attempts limit

Both Trezor and Ledger limit the number of PIN attempts before the device protects itself. On Trezor devices, after each wrong PIN entry the device imposes an exponentially increasing time delay before the next attempt is permitted, and after 16 consecutive incorrect entries the device performs a factory reset, wiping the encrypted seed from the secure element. On Ledger devices (Nano S, Nano X, Nano S Plus, and Stax), the device factory-resets after three consecutive incorrect PIN entries. In both cases the funds are not destroyed — they remain at their blockchain addresses — but the device is no longer a route to access them. The seed phrase, if you have it, becomes the only route.

The passphrase gap

The BIP39 passphrase is an optional but powerful addition to the seed phrase. When set, it is combined with the seed phrase using a key derivation function to produce an entirely separate set of wallet addresses. A different passphrase produces a different wallet — meaning that the same seed phrase can be used with hundreds of different passphrases to access hundreds of different wallets, none of which are related from an outside observer's view. This is sometimes called "plausible deniability" because an attacker who finds your seed phrase only sees the empty default wallet unless they also know your passphrase. This same property means that if you forget the passphrase, you have lost the wallet — there is no fallback, no recovery question, and no support number that can help.

How Trezor and Ledger security works

Understanding how the security of these devices is constructed is essential before considering what is and is not possible after a lockout. The following description is high-level but technically accurate, and it explains why certain things people hope for — such as "Trezor support unlocking the device for you" — are not actually possible.

The seed phrase is the wallet

When you initialise a new Trezor or Ledger, the device generates a random seed and presents you with a list of 12, 18, or 24 words drawn from the standard BIP39 wordlist of 2,048 words. Those words encode the seed in human-readable form. Cryptocurrency wallet addresses are then deterministically derived from this seed using BIP32 and BIP44 derivation paths. Every Bitcoin address, every Ethereum address, every wallet for every supported coin is a mathematical function of the seed. Anyone with the seed can recreate the wallet on any compatible device or software. Anyone without the seed cannot.

The PIN is a device lock, not an encryption key

The PIN you enter on a Trezor or Ledger is what unlocks the device for use. On Trezor, the seed is stored encrypted on the device, and the PIN is part of the decryption process; on Ledger, the seed is stored within a secure element chip that requires PIN authentication to use. In both cases, the PIN is a barrier to using the device — but the seed phrase itself is unaffected by the PIN. A correct seed phrase, entered on a freshly initialised device, produces the same wallet regardless of what PIN was previously used.

Trezor PIN wipe after 16 attempts

Trezor devices use a doubling time delay between PIN attempts: the first wrong attempt has no delay, the second imposes a roughly 2-second wait, then 4, 8, 16, 32 seconds and so on, doubling each time. After about a dozen attempts the wait between further attempts becomes hours, then days. After 16 wrong attempts in total, the device wipes itself. This protects against brute-force attacks by anyone who has stolen your device. It does not provide any back door for the legitimate owner who has forgotten the PIN.

Ledger PIN lockout after 3 attempts

Ledger devices use a stricter lockout: three consecutive incorrect PIN entries trigger an immediate factory reset of the secure element, wiping the on-device copy of the seed. Because the secure element is a tamper-resistant chip, there is no separate "extract the seed" pathway available to the user, to Ledger, or to any third party. The same security property that prevents an attacker who steals your Ledger from extracting your seed prevents you, the legitimate owner, from doing so.

The BIP39 passphrase: a different kind of protection

Where the PIN is a device lock, the BIP39 passphrase is a cryptographic input to the wallet derivation itself. It is never stored on the device — every time you connect your device and want to access a passphrase-protected wallet, you must enter the passphrase again. Common implementations allow passphrases up to 50 characters of arbitrary text (including spaces, punctuation, and capitalisation, all of which matter exactly). A 20-character passphrase using mixed case, numbers, and symbols has more entropy than the underlying seed phrase. Brute-forcing such a passphrase is computationally infeasible — it would take longer than the age of the universe with current hardware — and any service offering to do so should be treated with extreme suspicion.

What you should never do

Acute distress over a locked hardware wallet leads many people into actions that make their situation worse. The following are common mistakes that we see repeatedly in case enquiries — please pause before doing any of them.

Do not attempt to brute-force the PIN. On Ledger you have only three attempts before factory reset; on Trezor the time delays grow exponentially. Random guessing destroys the device's chance of unlocking with a remembered PIN later. If you genuinely think you can recall the PIN given more time, stop attempting and write down what you do remember — partial digits, length, patterns — before continuing.
Do not engage any "wallet unlocker" or "PIN recovery" service that asks for your seed phrase or device. No legitimate service can extract a seed from a locked hardware wallet, and the moment you give your seed phrase to anyone, the funds are theirs. CountWize will never ask for your seed phrase, your private keys, your passphrase, or remote access to your device. Any third party making such requests is a fraudster. Report them to Action Fraud and the FCA ScamSmart register.
Do not photograph or upload your seed phrase. If you find your written seed backup, do not type it into a website, send it via email or messaging app, store it in a cloud notes app, or photograph it for safe-keeping. Cloud-stored seed phrases are routinely stolen by malware and account compromise. Store the seed only in physical, offline form.
Do not buy a "decryption tool" from a marketplace. Online listings for tools that claim to decrypt or unlock Trezor or Ledger devices are scams. The genuine Trezor firmware is open source, and security researchers have publicly documented every legitimate side-channel issue. There is no shortcut available for purchase.

If your situation involved a fraudster who tricked you into entering your seed phrase or passphrase on a fake interface, that is a separate type of incident — the funds may already have been moved and tracing analysis may be relevant. See our guide on recovery scam warnings if you have already paid an unauthorised "recovery" service.

On-chain tracing methodology

It is important to be precise about what role on-chain tracing plays in a hardware-wallet lockout situation. Tracing is not a method for unlocking a device, recovering a seed phrase, or guessing a passphrase. None of those things is possible. What tracing can do is establish a documented record of where the funds in question are located, whether they remain at their original addresses, whether they have moved, and what attribution information (if any) is available. This factual record is sometimes useful — and sometimes not — depending on the specific situation.

What on-chain tracing can establish in a hardware-wallet case

  • Confirmation of address ownership: If you know one or more of the receiving addresses in the wallet (for example, from past transaction history or exchange withdrawal records), tracing can confirm the current balance and full transaction history of those addresses.
  • Movement detection: If the funds have been moved from those addresses since you lost access, tracing can document when, in what amounts, and to which subsequent addresses — a significant finding because it means someone other than you has access.
  • Static balance confirmation: If the funds remain unmoved at the original addresses, tracing confirms that they are still there. This is often reassuring information, even though the funds remain inaccessible without the seed.
  • Attribution of recipient addresses: If funds have moved, tracing can identify whether they ended up at known exchanges, custodians, mixers, or other entities — relevant if law enforcement or civil legal action is being considered.
  • Documented intelligence report: A written report suitable for a solicitor, an exchange, or law enforcement, summarising findings in plain language with blockchain citations.

In the most common hardware-wallet case — funds untouched at the original addresses, owner without seed phrase or passphrase — the tracing report confirms the funds are still there but inaccessible. This is not a happy outcome, and it is one we explain at preliminary triage rather than after we have done the full work. We want clients to commission a tracing analysis only when it is genuinely likely to help.

In a smaller subset of cases — typically where the wallet has been accessed by someone else (a hacker, a family member, a former associate) — tracing can be highly useful, providing documented evidence of the unauthorised access and the path of the funds. This documentation is the kind of evidence that can support a police report, an exchange compliance request, or a civil action.

What on-chain tracing cannot do

Honest limits in hardware-wallet cases

  • It cannot unlock your device. No firm of any kind can bypass a Trezor or Ledger PIN protection. Anyone claiming otherwise is misrepresenting their capability.
  • It cannot reconstruct your seed phrase. The seed is generated from a cryptographic random source on the device. There is no algorithm that can derive it from your wallet addresses or any on-chain data.
  • It cannot guess your passphrase. A passphrase of meaningful length cannot be brute-forced with current or foreseeable hardware. Services claiming passphrase recovery are scams.
  • It cannot compel an exchange to act. If tracing identifies that funds have moved to an exchange, only law enforcement or formal legal process can compel that exchange to investigate or freeze the account.
  • It does not guarantee any outcome. Tracing is investigative intelligence, not asset recovery. We provide a documented report; what happens after the report is in the hands of police, courts, exchanges, and other parties.

Frequently asked questions

Can a locked Trezor be recovered?

If you have the 12, 18, or 24-word recovery seed (BIP39 seed phrase), the wallet itself can be restored on a new or wiped device — the seed, not the device, is what controls the funds. If you do not have the seed, and you have lost the PIN, the device will eventually wipe itself after 16 incorrect PIN attempts on a Trezor and the funds will not be accessible from that device. There is no legitimate way to bypass PIN protection on a Trezor without the seed phrase.

What happens after 16 wrong PINs on Trezor?

After 16 consecutive incorrect PIN attempts, a Trezor device performs a factory reset, wiping the encrypted seed from the device. The funds themselves are not destroyed — they remain at the same blockchain addresses — but they can only be accessed if you can restore the seed phrase onto another compatible wallet. The exponential time delay between attempts (the wait roughly doubles each time) makes systematic guessing impractical.

Is my seed phrase enough to restore a hardware wallet?

The seed phrase is enough to restore the standard wallet on a new device of the same or a compatible brand. However, if you originally used a BIP39 passphrase (the so-called 25th word) on top of your seed, the seed alone will only restore the empty default wallet — to access the hidden wallet that contained your funds, you need both the seed and the exact passphrase. There is no way to derive the passphrase from the seed.

What is a BIP39 passphrase and how is it different from the PIN?

The PIN is a device-level lock; it stops anyone with physical access to the device from using it. The BIP39 passphrase is a cryptographic input that combines with your seed to derive an entirely separate set of wallet addresses — a different passphrase produces a different wallet. There is no recovery for a forgotten passphrase: the wallet that depended on it cannot be reconstructed without the exact characters, including spelling and capitalisation. This is by design.
