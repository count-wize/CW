Lost crypto tax relief UK: what HMRC says and how to document your claim

This page covers how HMRC treats lost, stolen, or inaccessible cryptocurrency for capital gains tax (CGT) purposes under UK law — including negligible value claims, the documentation HMRC expects, and how on-chain tracing evidence can support a tax relief claim on your Self Assessment return. It does not cover income tax on crypto, does not constitute tax advice, and does not apply to non-UK taxpayers.

How HMRC classifies cryptocurrency for tax purposes

HMRC does not treat cryptocurrency as currency or money. Instead, HMRC classifies most cryptocurrency held by individuals as a capital asset — specifically, as a "cryptoasset" that is subject to capital gains tax (CGT) on disposal. This classification is set out in HMRC's Cryptoassets Manual, which has been updated several times since 2019 and represents the current authoritative HMRC position.

For most individuals — as opposed to businesses operating as crypto traders — the relevant tax is CGT under the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 (TCGA 1992). This means:

  • When you dispose of crypto (sell it, exchange it, use it to pay for goods or services, or gift it), a chargeable gain or allowable loss arises based on the difference between your disposal proceeds and your allowable acquisition cost.
  • CGT losses can be set against CGT gains in the same tax year, reducing the amount of tax owed. Unused losses can be carried forward to future tax years indefinitely.
  • The annual CGT exempt amount (which has been reduced significantly in recent years — from £12,300 in 2022–23 to £3,000 in 2024–25) applies to your net gains, not individual transactions.
  • You must report disposals and gains (and claim losses) on a Self Assessment tax return. If you do not normally complete a Self Assessment return, you may need to register.

For most people reading this page, the key question is: if I have lost access to my crypto, or it was stolen from me, does that count as a disposal that creates a loss I can use? The answer is nuanced and depends on the specific circumstances — which the following sections explain.

What "disposal" means under TCGA 1992

A disposal is broadly any transfer of ownership. This includes a sale, an exchange, a gift, or any other event where you cease to own the asset. Critically, a theft is not a disposal in the conventional CGT sense — the owner has not chosen to transfer the asset. This distinction matters significantly for how you can claim a CGT loss on stolen crypto, as HMRC's treatment of theft versus voluntary disposal differs. Section 2 below explains this in detail.

For further reading on the basic CGT framework for crypto, see HMRC's published guidance: Individuals, cryptoassets and capital gains tax (gov.uk).

Lost, stolen, or inaccessible: what the distinction means for tax

HMRC's Cryptoassets Manual draws meaningful distinctions between three different scenarios involving crypto that is no longer accessible to the owner. Understanding which category your situation falls into is the first step in determining what tax relief — if any — is available.

Scenario HMRC position Relief mechanism
Theft or fraud — crypto taken from your wallet or exchange account without your consent No disposal has occurred (CRYPTO22200). The asset still technically exists and you remain the legal owner. A loss does not arise at the point of theft. Negligible value claim under s.24 TCGA 1992 once the asset is confirmed unrecoverable. Requires contemporaneous evidence.
Lost private key or seed phrase — you still legally own the crypto but cannot access it Again, no disposal. The asset exists on-chain; you simply cannot access it. Loss arises when you can demonstrate the key is unrecoverable. Negligible value claim. You must demonstrate the asset has become worthless from your perspective — i.e., access is permanently and irretrievably lost.
Exchange collapse — e.g. FTX, Celsius, Voyager More complex. If there is a creditor process underway, HMRC may consider the asset to have some residual value while distributions remain possible. HMRC has issued specific guidance on FTX (CG69705). Negligible value claim or capital loss on any actual distribution received that is less than acquisition cost. Timing of the claim matters significantly — see Section 3.
Scam investment platform — you transferred funds to a fraudulent scheme The transfer was likely a disposal (you chose to transfer the asset to a third party). The counterparty's fraudulent intent does not retroactively make this a non-disposal. Capital loss on the disposal, with disposal proceeds equal to market value at time of transfer (likely near-zero given the fraudulent nature of the "investment").

The significance of HMRC's "no disposal on theft" position

For victims of crypto theft and fraud, HMRC's position that a theft is not a disposal creates a specific challenge: you cannot simply claim a capital loss in the year of the theft. Instead, you must make a formal negligible value claim, and that claim must be supported by evidence that the asset has become, in HMRC's phrase, "of negligible value" — meaning that the prospect of recovering it is so remote as to be effectively nil.

This is both a timing question (when can you make the claim?) and an evidential question (what can you provide to satisfy HMRC?). Both questions are addressed in the sections below.

HMRC's specific guidance on exchange collapses

HMRC published specific guidance on the tax treatment of assets held on collapsed exchanges following the FTX insolvency in 2022. The guidance (CG69705, available in the Capital Gains Manual) sets out that where a creditor process is underway, HMRC does not accept that the asset has automatically become worthless merely because the exchange has halted withdrawals. The timing of any negligible value claim must reflect the point at which recovery prospects are genuinely nil — which may not be until a final creditor distribution has been made or a formal insolvency conclusion has been reached.

This means that for exchange-collapse situations, claiming a tax loss too early — before the insolvency process has concluded — carries the risk that HMRC will challenge the timing of the claim. Taking specialist tax advice is particularly important in these cases. Further reading: how on-chain tracing works and what it can document.

Negligible value claims: the mechanism for unrecoverable crypto

A negligible value claim is the primary tax relief mechanism available for lost or stolen crypto that is not expected to be recovered. The statutory basis is section 24(1) TCGA 1992, which provides that where an asset has become of negligible value, the owner may elect to be treated as having disposed of the asset and immediately reacquired it at its negligible value — effectively crystallising a capital loss without an actual sale.

What "negligible value" means in practice

HMRC's general guidance is that an asset has negligible value when its value is so small as to be practically worthless — often interpreted as less than 1% of original cost. For stolen or lost crypto, the argument is straightforward: if you cannot access the asset and have no realistic prospect of recovering it, it has no value to you. The challenge is demonstrating this to HMRC's satisfaction.

HMRC maintains a published list of assets accepted as being of negligible value (primarily shares in dissolved companies). Cryptocurrency is not on this list, which means every crypto negligible value claim must be individually evidenced and assessed.

When to make the claim: timing rules

A negligible value claim can be backdated by up to two tax years. This is significant: if your crypto became worthless in 2023–24 but you did not make the claim at the time, you can make the claim in your 2025–26 Self Assessment return and elect for the loss to be treated as arising in 2023–24, provided the asset was indeed of negligible value in that earlier year.

This backdating provision is useful for victims who were not aware of the tax implications at the time of their loss. However, the two-year window means that for losses arising in 2021–22 or earlier, the backdating opportunity may now have passed. Early professional advice is important to preserve options.

Capital losses versus the annual CGT exemption

Capital losses — including those arising from a negligible value claim — are set against capital gains in the same tax year first. Only net losses (where losses exceed gains) are carried forward. Losses cannot be set against income; they are specific to CGT. For most individuals with no other significant capital gains, a crypto loss claim in isolation will have no immediate tax effect other than creating a loss pool for future years.

Important: Capital losses must be formally claimed on a Self Assessment return. They are not automatically applied. The four-year notification window (s.16 TCGA 1992) means that a loss arising in the 2021–22 tax year must be claimed by 31 January 2027. After this date, the loss is permanently forfeited. If you have unclaimed losses from prior years, act promptly.

What documentation HMRC expects: the checklist

HMRC's Cryptoassets Manual (CRYPTO22200 and CRYPTO22300) makes clear that a negligible value claim for lost or stolen crypto must be supported by contemporaneous documentary evidence. HMRC officers are specifically directed to request evidence when reviewing these claims. The checklist below reflects what HMRC guidance and professional practice indicate is expected.

  1. Proof of original acquisition. Exchange purchase confirmation emails, transaction history exports from the exchange, or on-chain transaction records showing the original purchase. This establishes your acquisition date and cost — both required for the CGT computation.
  2. Proof of the loss event. For theft: screenshots of wallet activity showing the unauthorised transfer, any platform notifications of suspicious activity, email communications from the exchange (if relevant). For lost access: records showing your attempts to regain access (support tickets, correspondence). For exchange collapse: official communications from the administrator, administrator reports, or press coverage from verified sources.
  3. Transaction ID (TXID) of the relevant transfer. For theft cases, the on-chain transaction ID of the transfer out of your wallet is the most powerful single piece of evidence — it is an immutable, publicly verifiable record of exactly when and what was transferred. If you do not have the TXID, it can often be retrieved from the blockchain using your wallet address.
  4. Wallet addresses involved. Your wallet address and, where known, the destination wallet address. These allow HMRC (or a reviewer) to independently verify the on-chain record. They are also the starting point for any on-chain tracing exercise.
  5. Market value at the time of the loss event. The CGT computation requires you to establish the value of the crypto at the time it was lost or stolen. Historic price data is available from sources such as CoinGecko, CoinMarketCap, and major exchange APIs. Retain a screenshot or export of the historic price data and the methodology used to calculate the sterling value.
  6. Crime reference number (if a theft). An Action Fraud crime reference number is not strictly required by HMRC to make a negligible value claim, but its absence in a theft case significantly weakens the claim. It demonstrates that the theft was reported contemporaneously to law enforcement. If you have not reported to Action Fraud, do so now at actionfraud.police.uk.
  7. Evidence that recovery is not realistically possible. For theft cases, this might be a statement from an exchange that the destination account has been investigated and funds cannot be returned, or an on-chain tracing report showing the funds have been dispersed through multiple wallets and mixed. For lost-access cases, documented attempts to recover the key or seed phrase. For exchange collapses, administrator reports indicating the likely recovery rate for unsecured creditors.
  8. Your original cost basis records. Records of how much you paid for the crypto in sterling, including exchange fees. This is required for the CGT loss calculation. If you acquired crypto in multiple tranches, HMRC's section 104 pooling rules apply to determine the cost base.

HMRC record-keeping requirement: UK taxpayers are required by law to retain records relevant to their Self Assessment returns for at least 22 months after the end of the relevant tax year (or five years after the filing deadline for later-filed returns). For crypto transactions, this means retaining exchange statements, wallet records, and transaction history. HMRC can open an enquiry into any return up to four years after filing (or 20 years in cases of deliberate understatement).

How on-chain tracing evidence supports your claim

On-chain tracing is the analysis of public blockchain data to follow the movement of cryptocurrency from a specific point — in this context, from your wallet or exchange account at the time of the loss event. Every transaction on a public blockchain (Bitcoin, Ethereum, and most major chains) is permanently recorded and publicly verifiable. This creates a uniquely strong form of contemporaneous documentary evidence.

What an on-chain tracing report documents

A professional on-chain tracing report for a tax relief purpose typically documents:

  • The specific transaction(s) on the blockchain that correspond to the loss event — identified by transaction ID, timestamp, sending and receiving addresses, and amount.
  • The subsequent movement of funds through the blockchain — demonstrating that the funds left your control and moved to one or more third-party addresses.
  • Whether the destination addresses are linked to known centralised exchanges, mixing services, or other identifiable entities.
  • The current status of the funds — still held in a wallet, dispersed further, or converted.
  • A written methodology explaining how the analysis was conducted and what conclusions can and cannot be drawn from the blockchain data.

For HMRC purposes, the most important elements are the first two: establishing that a specific transfer occurred on a specific date, and demonstrating that it moved funds out of your control. This is the on-chain equivalent of a bank statement showing a fraudulent outgoing payment — except that, unlike a bank statement, it is independently verifiable by anyone with access to a blockchain explorer.

Why on-chain evidence is particularly strong for HMRC

HMRC is familiar with the evidential value of blockchain data and its immutability. Unlike screenshots of exchanges or wallet apps (which could theoretically be manipulated), on-chain transaction records exist on a public, distributed ledger that cannot be altered. Any HMRC officer, tax adviser, or court can independently verify the transaction using only the transaction ID and a publicly available blockchain explorer.

This makes on-chain evidence particularly persuasive as part of a negligible value claim. It provides an objective, verifiable record of the loss event that does not rely solely on the taxpayer's own account.

What on-chain tracing cannot do for your tax claim

On-chain tracing produces an intelligence report — evidence of what happened on the blockchain. It is not a guarantee that HMRC will accept your claim, and it does not constitute tax advice or a professional tax opinion. The tracing report should be used alongside, not as a substitute for, professional tax guidance from a qualified accountant or tax adviser.

Reporting a crypto loss on Self Assessment

Capital losses on cryptocurrency — including those arising from a negligible value claim — must be reported on your Self Assessment tax return. The process is as follows.

Registering for Self Assessment

If you do not already complete a Self Assessment return, you may need to register with HMRC. HMRC requires individuals to complete Self Assessment returns if, among other reasons, they need to report capital gains or losses from asset disposals. Registration deadlines apply: for the 2024–25 tax year, the deadline to register as a new Self Assessment taxpayer is 5 October 2025. Late registration may result in penalties. See gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment.

Where on the return to report crypto losses

Crypto losses are reported in the Capital Gains section of the Self Assessment return (SA108 form for paper returns, or the equivalent online section). You will need to provide:

  • The description of the asset disposed of (e.g. "Bitcoin — negligible value claim").
  • The date of the disposal (for a negligible value claim, this is the date you elect for the loss to be treated as arising — which can be backdated up to two tax years).
  • Disposal proceeds (for a negligible value claim, this is the negligible value — typically nil or near-nil).
  • Allowable costs (your original acquisition cost, including exchange fees).
  • The resulting loss.

The self-assessment filing window for the 2024–25 tax year

The UK self-assessment filing window runs from 6 April each year (when the previous tax year closes) to 31 January the following year for online returns. For the 2024–25 tax year (ending 5 April 2025), the online filing deadline is 31 January 2026. The paper filing deadline is 31 October 2025. If you are claiming a crypto loss for the 2024–25 year, your window to file online runs through January 2026.

For the self-assessment window that runs November–January each year — the period when search interest in "lost crypto tax relief uk" peaks — the relevant question is usually whether to include a crypto loss on the current year's return (2024–25) or whether a backdated negligible value claim for an earlier year is more appropriate. A tax adviser can help determine the optimal timing.

Amended returns for prior years

If you have already filed a Self Assessment return for a year in which you had a crypto loss, you can amend the return to add the loss claim. Returns can be amended within 12 months of the online filing deadline for the relevant tax year. For earlier years, you would need to make an overpayment relief claim (which has different procedural requirements) or write to HMRC explaining the circumstances. In either case, the four-year notification window for losses under s.16 TCGA 1992 applies.

HMRC's crypto reporting tool: HMRC has published a dedicated online resource for individuals reporting cryptoasset transactions: gov.uk — check if you need to pay tax when you receive cryptoassets. HMRC has also published a cryptoassets toolbox for those with complex multi-year crypto histories.

Common mistakes that lead HMRC to reject a claim

Based on published HMRC guidance, tax tribunal decisions, and professional practice, the following errors most commonly undermine crypto loss claims — including negligible value claims for lost or stolen crypto.

Claiming a loss at the point of theft rather than at negligible value

As explained in Section 2, HMRC's position is that a theft is not a disposal. A claim that treats the theft date itself as the date of a capital loss disposal is technically incorrect and will likely be challenged by HMRC. The correct mechanism is a negligible value claim, which requires the asset to have become worth nothing — not merely that it has been stolen. Timing the claim correctly is important.

Providing insufficient contemporaneous documentation

HMRC officers reviewing crypto negligible value claims look for evidence that was created at the time of the loss — not reconstructed later. Screenshots taken months or years after the event, or records that cannot be independently verified, are significantly less persuasive than on-chain transaction records retrieved from a blockchain explorer or an Action Fraud report with a contemporaneous reference number.

Incorrect cost basis calculation

Crypto CGT calculations must use HMRC's specific pooling rules (s.104 TCGA 1992), which require you to maintain a running average cost per token across all purchases. If you have bought the same token multiple times, the cost basis for the disposal is not simply the price you paid on the most recent occasion. Many crypto tax software tools calculate this automatically, but errors in cost basis are a common source of HMRC enquiries.

Missing the four-year notification window

Capital losses must be notified to HMRC within four years of the end of the tax year in which the loss arose. For a loss arising in 2019–20, the notification deadline was 5 April 2025. Many crypto fraud victims from the 2020–21 bear market are approaching this window for that tax year now. If you have unclaimed losses from 2021 or earlier, professional advice should be sought promptly.

Confusing tax loss claims with scam "tax recovery" services

There are a significant number of fraudulent firms that target crypto fraud victims by claiming to offer "tax recovery" services — promising to recover your funds through a "tax rebate" or similar mechanism, usually in exchange for an upfront fee. These firms are not registered with HMRC and the services they offer do not exist. HMRC does not issue "tax rebates" on crypto losses outside the normal Self Assessment process. Any firm promising to recover your crypto through a tax mechanism and charging an upfront fee is almost certainly a scam. See our recovery scam warning page for more detail on how these schemes operate.

Frequently asked questions

Can I claim tax relief on lost or stolen cryptocurrency in the UK?

HMRC treats lost or stolen cryptocurrency as a capital loss in most circumstances. You may be able to deduct the loss against capital gains in the same or future tax years, subject to meeting HMRC's evidential requirements. The loss must be reported on a Self Assessment return. HMRC does not accept a claim based solely on your word — contemporaneous documentary evidence is required. This page explains what HMRC expects and how on-chain tracing evidence can help.

Does HMRC treat stolen crypto differently from lost crypto?

HMRC's Cryptoassets Manual (CRYPTO22200) draws a distinction. For a theft, HMRC's position is that no disposal has occurred because the owner has not chosen to transfer the asset — so technically the capital loss arises when the asset is proven to be unrecoverable, not at the moment of theft. For lost access (forgotten password, lost seed phrase, hardware failure), HMRC again requires a formal negligible value claim. In both cases, the practical requirement is the same: contemporaneous documentation demonstrating the loss and its value at the time.

What is a negligible value claim and when does it apply to crypto?

A negligible value claim under s.24 TCGA 1992 allows a taxpayer to treat an asset as having been disposed of and immediately reacquired at a negligible (near-zero) value, crystallising a capital loss without an actual sale. For cryptocurrency, HMRC accepts these claims where the owner can demonstrate the asset has become genuinely worthless or unrecoverable — for example, a collapsed exchange with no prospect of distributions, or a wallet to which all access has been permanently lost. The claim must state the date at which the asset became negligible value and can be backdated by up to two tax years.

How does on-chain tracing evidence help a tax relief claim?

On-chain tracing produces a documented record of the movement of funds on the blockchain — transaction IDs, wallet addresses, timestamps, and the destination of funds. For a tax relief claim, this evidence can demonstrate: that a transfer to a specific address occurred on a specific date (establishing the disposal or theft date); that the destination address is controlled by a third party (supporting the case that ownership was lost); and whether the funds have moved further, been mixed, or reached an exchange account. This evidence is contemporaneous, immutable, and independently verifiable — exactly what HMRC requires.

Do I need to report a crypto loss on Self Assessment even if I have no gains?

You are not required to report a capital loss to HMRC, but you should if you want to carry it forward against future gains. Losses are only available for offset if they have been formally notified to HMRC within four years of the end of the tax year in which the loss arose (s.16 TCGA 1992). If you do not report the loss, you cannot use it to reduce a future capital gains tax liability. Consult a tax adviser to determine whether and when to file.
