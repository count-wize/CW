Annual Transparency Report — CountWize 2025

Our annual transparency report sets out — in honest, audit-ready terms — how many on-chain tracing enquiries CountWize received in our reporting year, how many of those cases we accepted, and how many we declined. It explains the reasons cases are refused and the principles behind every editorial and operational decision. We publish this report because UK fraud victims are entitled to see how a tracing firm actually behaves before they engage one.

Cases seen — total enquiries received

"Cases seen" is the total count of distinct first-contact enquiries CountWize received during the reporting year, regardless of whether the enquiry was ultimately accepted for paid work, declined at intake, or referred onward. Every form submission, telephone call logged through our published UK number, and direct email to our listed contact addresses is counted exactly once at the point of first contact.

What the figure includes

The "cases seen" figure includes every enquiry where the contact described a potential cryptocurrency loss, hardware-wallet difficulty, exchange dispute, scam concern, or related on-chain matter. It includes enquiries that turned out, on closer review, to be outside our scope (for example, traditional banking fraud with no crypto element). Including out-of-scope contacts in the headline figure is deliberate — we want the report to reflect total demand on our intake function, not a curated subset.

Breakdown by enquiry type

The full report will publish a non-identifying breakdown of enquiries by category, including (for example) suspected investment fraud, pig-butchering, recovery-scam concerns, exchange insolvency questions, hardware-wallet PIN/passphrase issues, and wrong-address transfers. Category totals are aggregated; no individual case is identifiable in the published figures.

Cases taken — accepted for on-chain tracing work

"Cases taken" is the count of enquiries that progressed past the free initial review and were accepted for paid on-chain tracing work during the reporting year. We accept a case only when the facts disclose a real prospect that on-chain tracing will produce actionable intelligence — meaning a documented fund-flow trace, exchange identification, or attribution evidence that can be used by a solicitor, regulator, or law-enforcement contact in a subsequent step the client controls.

Why "accepted" does not mean "recovered"

Acceptance reflects only that we believe on-chain tracing can produce useful intelligence — not that the underlying funds will be recovered, returned, or otherwise made good. Recovery depends on factors outside our control, including the willingness of regulated exchanges to act on disclosure orders, the cooperation of UK or overseas law enforcement, the availability of solicitors to bring civil proceedings, and the residual location of the funds. CountWize publishes intelligence reports; we do not guarantee outcomes and we do not act as a claims management company or as a solicitor.

What an accepted engagement involves

An accepted engagement begins with a written engagement letter setting out the scope, the fixed or capped fee, the deliverables, and the limitations. The engagement produces an intelligence report that the client owns and can hand to their solicitor, to law enforcement, or to any regulator they choose to engage. The full transparency report will publish — in aggregate — average engagement durations, typical deliverable structures, and the proportion of accepted cases that resulted in a Norwich Pharmacal Order application, an Action Fraud intelligence packet, or a referral to a solicitor.

Cases refused — declined and why

"Cases refused" is the count of enquiries that did not progress to an accepted engagement during the reporting year. We publish this figure — and the breakdown of reasons — because honest decline data is one of the strongest indicators that a tracing firm is not simply taking everyone's money. A firm that accepts every case is a firm that is not assessing case viability, and a firm that does not assess case viability is one that is unlikely to produce useful results for the cases it does take.

Why we decline cases

The reasons we decline cases are predictable and consistent. The full report will publish the proportion of declines attributable to each of the following categories, and an honest narrative comment on the year's distribution.

  • Out of scope: the matter is not a cryptocurrency or on-chain issue. For example, a traditional bank-transfer fraud with no crypto component, a generic legal dispute, or a tax question. We refer these enquirers to Citizens Advice, Action Fraud, or — where appropriate — the relevant regulator.
  • Insufficient evidence: the enquirer cannot provide a transaction ID, a wallet address, an exchange account reference, or other verifiable on-chain starting point. Without a verifiable starting point, on-chain tracing is impossible. We help the enquirer understand what evidence to gather and invite them to come back if they later assemble it.
  • Likely fraud target: the surrounding facts indicate that the enquirer has been targeted by, or is being actively defrauded by, a recovery-scam impersonator. In these cases we decline the engagement, warn the enquirer in writing, and direct them to FCA ScamSmart and to our own recovery-scam warning guide.
  • Tracing prospects too limited: the funds passed through privacy coins (Monero), through one or more mixing services, or through cross-chain bridges in a configuration that makes meaningful attribution unlikely. We explain this clearly before the enquirer pays for any work.
  • Transaction too old: on-chain data does not expire, but the practical prospects of a downstream recovery action drop sharply for transactions more than five years old. We tell enquirers honestly when the trace is technically possible but unlikely to support a useful next step.
  • Conflict or capacity: a small number of cases are declined because of a conflict of interest with an existing client or because we lack the immediate capacity to handle the matter to the standard we require. We refer affected enquirers to other competent tracing firms or to a UK-qualified solicitor.
  • Already paid a recovery firm: where the enquirer has already paid an upfront-fee recovery firm, we explain the FCA warning on recovery scams and the difficulty of building a clean evidence trail. We may still accept the case for a tracing engagement, but we will be explicit about the complications.

Why we publish this report

The UK on-chain tracing market is unregulated. There is no statutory requirement for any tracing firm to publish how many cases it sees, how many it takes, or how many it refuses. This regulatory gap is itself the reason we publish. A market without disclosure is a market in which fraud victims cannot tell a competent tracing firm from a fraudulent one. Transparency reporting is the simplest mechanism by which a firm can demonstrate, over time, that its public claims about its work match its actual operational record.

Three reasons we publish

1. Accountability to the people we serve. Cryptocurrency victims have, in many cases, already been deceived once. They are entitled to know — before they engage a tracing firm — what proportion of cases that firm declines, on what grounds, and how its acceptance rates compare across categories. Transparency reporting puts a verifiable record in their hands so that the choice to engage CountWize is an informed one.

2. Discipline on our own decision-making. Knowing that our acceptance and decline figures will be published every year imposes its own discipline. It makes it harder to drift toward accepting cases for revenue reasons rather than viability reasons. It makes it harder to under-document declines when the reason was inconvenient. It builds operational maturity by exposing operational reality.

3. Contribution to the wider UK fraud-prevention community. The aggregate picture of cryptocurrency-fraud enquiries received by an independent UK tracing firm is itself useful information for regulators, law enforcement, journalists, and academics. We publish category breakdowns and decline proportions in a format that other firms, researchers, and policymakers can compare against — encouraging the wider market to adopt similar transparency.

What this report does not claim

We do not claim that our figures are independently audited in their first year. We do claim that they are produced from CountWize's case-management records, that the methodology used to derive them is the methodology described below, and that — beginning with the second annual report — we intend to engage independent assurance over the figures. We do not claim that publishing this report makes CountWize FCA-authorised. Methodology and definitions

This section sets out the operational definitions used to produce the figures in this report. We publish the methodology in advance of the figures so that the data, when published, can be evaluated against a clearly stated framework rather than an after-the-fact reconciliation.

Reporting period

The reporting period for each annual report is the calendar year, 1 January to 31 December. The first published annual report covers the 2025 calendar year and will be released in the second half of 2026 to allow time for end-of-year data closure, internal review, and editorial preparation.

How an "enquiry" is counted

An enquiry is counted once at the point of first contact. Repeat contacts from the same person about the same underlying matter are merged into a single case for counting purposes. Enquiries from solicitors, regulators, journalists, or law enforcement on behalf of victims are counted only where they progress to a victim-led engagement; pure information requests are excluded.

How an "acceptance" is counted

A case is counted as accepted at the point a written engagement letter is signed by both parties and the agreed initial fee is paid. Free initial reviews are not counted as acceptances regardless of how detailed the review is.

How a "decline" is counted

A decline is counted at the point CountWize sends the enquirer a written communication stating that we will not be progressing the matter to a paid engagement. The decline reason is recorded against the categories listed in the cases-refused section above. Each declined case is assigned exactly one primary decline reason, even where multiple factors contributed.

What we do not publish

We do not publish anything that would identify an individual client, an individual case, or any specific counterparty exchange or wallet address. Aggregate proportions and totals are the only figures published. Where the count in any single category is below five cases for a year, we publish a "fewer than 5" indicator rather than the exact count to protect against re-identification.

Future independent assurance

From the 2026 reporting year onward, we intend to commission limited assurance over these figures from an independent UK chartered accountancy firm. The first assured report will be published in 2027. The 2025 report — the figures of which will populate the placeholders in this page — is published as an unassured firm-produced report and is labelled as such.

Who we cannot help

  • If you have already paid a fraudulent recovery firm — see our recovery-scam warning guide and the FCA warning at fca.org.uk/scamsmart.
  • If the transaction is more than 5 years old — on-chain traceability does not expire, but the practical prospects of any further action are substantially reduced.
  • If the counterparty is an unidentified peer-to-peer wallet not attributable to any exchange — without exchange attribution, civil legal routes are limited.
  • We cannot guarantee that tracing results will lead to any outcome — we provide intelligence, not guarantees.
  • We do not act as a solicitor, authorised claims management company (CMC), or financial adviser.
