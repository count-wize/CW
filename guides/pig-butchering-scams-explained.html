Pig-butchering scams explained: what UK victims need to know

What is a pig-butchering scam?

Pig-butchering is a specific type of investment fraud that combines prolonged social engineering with a fake cryptocurrency trading platform. The name comes from the Chinese term 杀猪盘 (shā zhū pán), which translates roughly as "fattening the pig before slaughter" — a reference to the fraudster's method of building trust and encouraging larger and larger deposits before disappearing with the victim's funds.

The fraud is not new — variants existed in East Asia before cryptocurrency became the vehicle of choice — but the combination of anonymous messaging apps, convincing fake trading platforms, and the irreversibility of on-chain cryptocurrency transactions has made it far more scalable and far more destructive than its predecessors. Pig-butchering operations are now among the highest-revenue fraud types in the world, and the UK has seen consistent growth in reported cases since 2022.

Where pig-butchering operations come from

INTERPOL and multiple national law enforcement agencies have documented that the large majority of pig-butchering operations are run by organised criminal networks based in Southeast Asia, primarily in Myanmar, Cambodia, and Laos — often operating from so-called "scam compounds" where workers (many of whom are themselves trafficking victims) run the fraud operations under coercion. The UN Office on Drugs and Crime has estimated that these compounds generate billions of dollars annually from victims worldwide. In many cases, the individual "romantic" or "professional" contact a UK victim received was handled by a person working against their will in such a facility.

This context does not change what the victim has experienced or what practical steps they should take — but it is relevant to understanding why the fraud is so well-organised, why scripts and personas are so consistent across different cases, and why law enforcement coordination across multiple jurisdictions is required to address it at source.

How pig-butchering differs from classic romance scams

Traditional romance fraud involves building a relationship and then requesting money transfers — often framed as emergencies, medical costs, or travel expenses. Pig-butchering is structurally different: the money is not taken directly but is invested into a fake platform that shows fabricated returns. The victim believes they are making profit. This distinction matters because it means pig-butchering victims often have significant on-chain transaction records: real cryptocurrency was sent to real blockchain addresses, even if the investment platform itself was entirely fictitious.

UK-specific context

Action Fraud and the FCA have both published warnings about pig-butchering targeting UK consumers. UK victim losses to pig-butchering and closely related crypto investment scams have increased year-on-year between 2023 and 2025, according to data cited in FCA ScamSmart warnings and Action Fraud annual reporting. Note that CountWize cannot verify that any cited figure is exhaustive — underreporting of pig-butchering fraud is believed to be significant because of the shame and stigma many victims feel.

If you believe you have been targeted by a pig-butchering operation and have made one or more cryptocurrency transfers to a platform that you now cannot access, or that is requesting further payment before "releasing" your funds, the steps in sections 4 and 5 of this guide are relevant to your situation.

How pig-butchering works — the five stages

Pig-butchering follows a consistent operational pattern that has been documented across thousands of cases worldwide. Understanding the stages helps both potential victims identify a developing fraud and past victims understand what happened to them — which can be useful context when making a report or requesting a tracing exercise.

  1. Contact. The fraud begins with an unsolicited first message to the victim via WhatsApp, Telegram, Instagram, LinkedIn, a dating app, or an SMS. Common entry scenarios include a "wrong number" text (the fraudster apologises, the conversation continues), a connection request on a professional network, or a mutual-interest message on a social platform. The persona is almost always attractive, successful, and either local or plausibly international (often claiming to be based in London, Singapore, or the US). The fraudster does not mention cryptocurrency at this stage.
  2. Trust-building. Over a period of weeks — sometimes months — the fraudster invests substantial time in building an emotional connection. Conversations cover everyday life, family, interests, and ambitions. The fraudster will often share "personal" photographs, stories of a difficult childhood or past relationship, and expressions of growing affection. In professional variants, the relationship is framed as a valuable business connection. No cryptocurrency is mentioned at this stage. The purpose of this phase is to establish a level of trust that will later override the victim's scepticism when the investment opportunity is introduced. The fraudster's persona consistently claims to be a financially successful professional — frequently in finance, technology, or international trade.
  3. The introduction. After the trust-building phase, the fraudster casually mentions a trading or investment opportunity. It is typically framed as something they do on the side — a platform that a family member, friend, or colleague in the finance industry introduced them to. The opportunity is presented as low-risk, with consistent returns. The fraudster shows screenshots or screen shares of their own account showing impressive returns. They offer to guide the victim through opening an account. The platform itself is a fake exchange — often branded to look like a legitimate known exchange, but operating on a fraudulent back-end that allows the operators to manipulate displayed balances freely.
  4. Small wins. To build the victim's confidence, the platform initially allows small withdrawals. The victim deposits a modest amount, sees apparent profits on the fake dashboard, and is able to withdraw a portion. This "proof of concept" is a deliberate part of the operation — the fraudsters are investing in a larger return by allowing a small early withdrawal. Encouraged by this success, the victim is coached to deposit progressively larger amounts. Urgency tactics are commonly used: limited-time opportunities, signals from the fraudster's "uncle at the exchange," or the suggestion that a major market move is imminent and the victim should deposit more before it closes.
  5. The slaughter. When the fraudster judges that the victim's deposits have reached the optimal level, the operation concludes. The victim attempts to withdraw their funds and finds the account locked. They are presented with a demand — a "tax payment," a "compliance fee," a "withdrawal bond," or an "anti-money laundering clearance charge" — which must be paid before the funds can be released. No such fee exists; it is a final extraction attempt. Whether or not the victim pays this additional charge, the platform eventually becomes inaccessible, and the fraudster ceases contact. In some cases, a secondary fraud follows: a firm claiming to be a law enforcement agency or specialist recovery company contacts the victim offering to recover the funds for an upfront fee. This is known as a recovery scam.

The entire cycle may last anywhere from four weeks to over a year, depending on the fraudster's assessment of the victim's financial capacity and the rate at which trust is established. Cases involving losses above £50,000 typically involve grooming periods of several months or more.

Nine warning patterns to recognise

Pig-butchering scams are sophisticated and the grooming process is designed to suppress the victim's scepticism. However, there are consistent patterns that, when recognised early, can prevent losses. The following nine patterns are documented across a large number of reported cases in the UK and internationally. No single pattern on its own confirms fraud — but multiple patterns appearing together should trigger caution.

  1. Unsolicited first contact on an unexpected platform. The initial contact comes on a platform where you would not ordinarily expect to be approached by a stranger with romantic or professional interest — a "wrong number" text, a random WhatsApp message, an unexpected LinkedIn connection with no mutual contacts, or a dating app match that immediately moves off-platform. Legitimate professional contacts and genuine romantic matches do not typically begin with unsolicited approaches on multiple platforms simultaneously.
  2. The relationship develops unusually fast. Within days or a small number of weeks, the contact is expressing strong affection, calling you a close friend, describing you as a rare person they connect with, or suggesting a future together. Legitimate relationships — whether professional or romantic — develop at a more natural pace. Accelerated emotional bonding is a deliberate technique to bypass rational judgement before the investment hook is introduced.
  3. They avoid video calls, or video quality is suspiciously poor. The contact makes excuses to avoid live video calls — they are busy, their internet is poor, they are shy on camera. When video calls do happen, the image is blurred, filtered, or inconsistent with the photos they have shared. This is because the persona is typically operated by a different person to the photos used, and sometimes by multiple operators working in shifts. Deepfake video technology is increasingly used in higher-value operations.
  4. They mention a specific trading platform or exchange you have never heard of. The recommended platform has a professional-looking website and a name that sounds plausibly legitimate — often resembling a real exchange with minor variations (a different domain extension, an added word, or a slightly different spelling). It is not listed on any mainstream financial directory, is not registered with the FCA, and does not appear on the FCA's authorised firm register. Any app or website used for cryptocurrency investment that is not listed on a recognised regulatory register warrants independent verification before any deposit.
  5. They claim to have a contact at an exchange who provides early-access signals. The fraudster says their uncle, aunt, former colleague, or family friend works at a major exchange or trading firm and can provide trading signals that give them advance notice of market movements. This is a fabrication designed to explain the implausibly consistent returns shown on the fake platform. No such inside advantage is being provided; the platform's back-end simply displays whatever balance the operators choose.
  6. Profits appear suspiciously quickly and consistently. The fake platform shows returns that are both high and consistent — gains week after week, with no periods of loss. Real cryptocurrency markets are highly volatile. Any platform claiming consistent double-digit monthly returns with no significant drawdowns is either fabricating the numbers or operating illegally. If the displayed returns feel too good to be true, they are.
  7. You are encouraged to recruit friends or family members. The fraudster suggests, often with an attractive "referral bonus" incentive, that you introduce the investment opportunity to people you know. This is a secondary revenue extraction mechanism — and it is also a way to spread the fraud to additional victims. Asking victims to recruit others is a feature of both pig-butchering operations and multi-level investment fraud. Sharing the opportunity with others before independently verifying the platform exposes your contacts to the same losses.
  8. Withdrawal requires payment of a "tax," "clearing fee," or "compliance deposit." When you attempt to withdraw your funds, the platform generates a demand for additional payment before the withdrawal can be processed. This is framed as a government tax, a compliance requirement, an anti-money laundering clearance, a platform withdrawal bond, or a network fee. No legitimate exchange charges a fee to the recipient before processing a withdrawal. This demand is the final extraction stage of the fraud — paying it does not result in funds being released.
  9. The platform URL is a near-copy of a legitimate exchange name. The platform domain may differ from the genuine exchange by one letter, use a different top-level domain (.co, .io, .net instead of .com), or add a word ("pro," "global," "trade") to the legitimate name. Before depositing on any platform, search the exact domain name on the FCA's warning list at fca.org.uk/consumers/warning-list-unauthorised-firms and verify the platform's FCA authorisation status independently.

UK reporting: what to do immediately

If you have been targeted by a pig-butchering scam — whether or not you have made a deposit — there are four immediate steps you should take. Acting quickly improves the intelligence value of your report and may be relevant to bank dispute timelines.

Step 1: Report to Action Fraud

Report the fraud online at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. You will receive a crime reference number (CRN). Keep this number — it is required for all subsequent steps including bank disputes, exchange complaints, and any civil legal process. Action Fraud is operated by the City of London Police and reports are assessed by the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB) and disseminated to relevant policing units. When reporting, include: the wallet addresses you sent funds to, any transaction IDs (txIDs), screenshots of the platform, all conversation logs, and any website URLs associated with the platform or the contact.

Step 2: Report the platform to the FCA and check the Warning List

Report the unregulated firm to the FCA Consumer Helpline on 0800 111 6768 or through the FCA ScamSmart reporting tool at fca.org.uk/scamsmart. Also check whether the platform is already on the FCA Warning List of unauthorised firms at fca.org.uk/consumers/warning-list-unauthorised-firms. Reporting the platform contributes to the warning list, helping protect future victims.

Step 3: Report the social media profile

Report the fraudster's profile to the platform where contact was made — WhatsApp, Telegram, Instagram, LinkedIn, or the relevant dating app. Most platforms have a "report" or "block and report" function. This may help the platform remove the account before it targets additional victims, and your report may be used by the platform's fraud team to identify connected accounts.

Step 4: Preserve evidence before accounts are deleted

Take screenshots of everything immediately: the platform URL and login screen, your account dashboard showing the fabricated balance, all conversations with the fraudster (including timestamps), any profile photos or documents they shared, wallet addresses you sent funds to, and all transaction IDs. Save these to a location that does not rely on the same device or account used during the fraud. Transaction IDs are particularly important — they allow any tracing firm or law enforcement agency to locate the on-chain record of your transfers independently and permanently.

Can your crypto be traced?

One of the most common questions from pig-butchering victims is whether anything can be done once the platform has disappeared. The answer requires careful framing: on-chain tracing is often possible, but what tracing can practically achieve depends heavily on where the funds went after you sent them.

Why on-chain records exist even for fake platforms

The pig-butchering platform is fake — the investment dashboard, the displayed profits, and the trading activity shown are all fabricated. But the cryptocurrency you sent is real, and the on-chain transaction record is permanent. When you sent funds to the wallet address provided by the platform, that transfer was recorded on the public blockchain. The transaction ID is permanently inscribed in the blockchain ledger and is readable by anyone, regardless of what subsequently happened to the platform's website or the fraudster's social media accounts.

This means that even after the platform has disappeared and all contact has ceased, a professional on-chain tracing exercise can start from the transaction ID you have and follow the movement of the funds forward through the blockchain — from the address you sent to, through subsequent hops, to wherever the funds currently reside.

What on-chain tracing can identify

Depending on the specific transaction path, on-chain tracing may identify: a cluster of wallet addresses controlled by the same fraudulent operation; deposit addresses at centralised exchanges (exchanges that hold Know Your Customer identity records for the receiving account); the current wallet holding the funds or a downstream address; and patterns consistent with previously documented pig-butchering operations.

If funds can be traced to a deposit address at a regulated exchange — particularly a UK-regulated or EU-regulated exchange — that is a legally significant finding. The exchange holds identity records for the account that received the funds, and those records may be obtainable via a UK court order (a Norwich Pharmacal Order, or NPO) or via a law enforcement production order.

Realistic outcome scenarios

Any firm or individual who promises you will recover your cryptocurrency should be treated as a red flag, not a reassurance. Recovery is possible in some circumstances, but it is not guaranteed, not automatic, and not common for smaller-value cases. The following scenarios represent an honest breakdown of what on-chain tracing can produce in practice.

Outcome A: Transaction traced to a regulated exchange

This is the most favourable scenario. If on-chain tracing identifies that your funds arrived at a deposit address held at a regulated, KYC-compliant exchange — particularly a UK or EU-regulated exchange — there is a legal mechanism to pursue the account holder's identity. A Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) application to a UK court can compel the exchange to disclose account details. CountWize produces an intelligence report documenting the exchange attribution; you would then need to engage a solicitor to pursue the NPO. NPO applications involve legal costs and are generally most economical for losses above approximately £10,000. Even in this scenario, outcome depends on further steps outside CountWize's control — including whether funds have already been withdrawn to fiat currency and whether the account holder can be located and pursued.

Outcome B: Funds moved through multiple intermediate wallets with no exchange link yet identified

This is a common scenario in pig-butchering cases. Funds are moved rapidly through a chain of addresses before reaching their ultimate destination — or before reaching an exchange, if they do so at all. In this case, tracing can document the full transaction chain and identify the current holding addresses, but cannot yet produce exchange attribution. The intelligence report is still valuable as law enforcement evidence and may be combined with other intelligence (OSINT, reports from multiple victims to the same operation) to build a more actionable case. The chain is less complete, but the report still contributes meaningfully to any investigation.

Outcome C: Funds passed through a mixer or moved to a high-risk jurisdiction

This is the most challenging scenario. If funds were routed through a cryptocurrency mixer or tumbler — a service designed to obscure the transaction trail — or converted into a privacy coin such as Monero, the on-chain trail becomes substantially harder to follow. Tracing can still document the funds up to the point they entered the obfuscation mechanism, and OSINT analysis may provide supplementary intelligence, but the prospects of exchange attribution or further civil action are reduced. An honest tracing firm will tell you this upfront after reviewing the initial transaction data. Even in this scenario, an intelligence report documenting what can be established remains potentially useful to law enforcement as part of a broader operation.

We will never ask for: your seed phrase, private key, exchange password, two-factor authentication codes, remote access to your computer, or cryptocurrency payment to begin work. Who we cannot help

  • If you have already paid a second firm claiming to be a "recovery service" — this does not exclude you from a case review, but it complicates the evidence trail and may indicate an active secondary fraud.
  • If the transaction is more than 5 years old — the on-chain trail may still exist, but practically, the prospects of any further action are substantially reduced.
  • If no on-chain transaction ID or wallet address is available — without a starting point on the blockchain, a tracing exercise cannot be conducted.
