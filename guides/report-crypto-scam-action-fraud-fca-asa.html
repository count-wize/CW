How to Report a Crypto Scam in the UK: Action Fraud, FCA Warning List, and ASA

This page explains where and how to report a cryptocurrency scam in the UK — step by step. It covers reporting to Action Fraud, checking and using the FCA Warning List, submitting a complaint to the ASA about misleading crypto adverts, what information to gather before you report, and what realistically happens after. It does not promise fund recovery, provide legal advice, or constitute a financial promotion.

What information to gather before you report

The quality of your report determines how useful it is to investigators and to any subsequent on-chain tracing. Rushing to report without assembling your evidence can mean submitting an incomplete record that is harder for the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB) to act on. Before you open the Action Fraud reporting portal, spend time pulling together the following categories of information.

Transaction records

Every cryptocurrency transaction generates a unique transaction ID (also called a TXID or transaction hash). This is a long string of letters and numbers that serves as the permanent, public identifier for that transaction on the blockchain. You can find TXIDs in your crypto wallet's transaction history, on the exchange or platform you used to send funds, or by looking up your wallet address on a public blockchain explorer such as Etherscan (for Ethereum-based tokens) or Blockchain.com (for Bitcoin). Record every TXID associated with funds you sent to the scammers. These are the starting points for any on-chain tracing.

Wallet addresses

A blockchain wallet address is the public identifier to which cryptocurrency is sent — similar in concept to a bank account number. You should record: your own wallet address or addresses from which you sent funds, the scammer's wallet address or addresses to which you sent funds (this is typically visible in your transaction history and on the blockchain explorer for your TXID), and any additional addresses the scammer provided to you during the fraud. Even a partial address is useful — on-chain tracers can work backwards from a TXID to identify destination addresses if you do not have them independently.

Communications

Take screenshots of every communication you have had with the scammers. This includes: WhatsApp or Telegram messages, emails (including full message headers if possible, which show the originating server), social media messages (Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, LinkedIn), any voice call logs with phone numbers, website URLs and screenshots of websites the scammers used, and any documents or contracts they provided. Do not delete any of these communications. In cases involving authorised push payment fraud via crypto, preserved communications can be critical to any bank chargeback or Section 75 claim.

Payment records

Record every payment you made as part of the scam. This includes: bank transfer records (sort code, account number, amount, date, reference), card payment receipts if you purchased crypto via card, exchange deposit records if you converted fiat to crypto on a legitimate exchange before sending, receipts or invoices the scammers provided, and any upfront fees paid to a secondary firm claiming to help you recover funds (these may themselves constitute a separate fraud). Your bank's statements will show bank transfers; your crypto exchange will have a record of any fiat-to-crypto conversions you made through them.

Identifying information about the scammers

Write down every piece of identifying information the scammers provided, regardless of whether you believe it to be genuine. This includes: the name or names they used, any company name and purported registration number, phone numbers including country codes, email addresses and domains, website URLs (copy the full URL, not just the domain name), physical addresses if provided, and any FCA or other regulatory reference numbers they quoted. Fraudsters frequently impersonate legitimate firms — the name or registration number they gave you may help investigators identify the legitimate firm being cloned.

A written timeline

Before reporting, write a clear chronological account of what happened. Include: when you first made contact or were contacted, the sequence of events, when each payment was made, when you realised something was wrong, and any subsequent contact from the scammers or from third parties claiming to help. A clear timeline makes your report far easier for investigators to assess and significantly improves the quality of any on-chain tracing that may follow.

Do not contact the scammers again. Once you have recognised the fraud, cease all contact immediately. Further engagement rarely produces any useful information and may expose you to additional pressure, psychological manipulation, or a secondary scam targeting people who have already been defrauded.

How to report to Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk)

Action Fraud is the UK's national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime. It is a service of the City of London Police and operates in conjunction with the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB). Reporting to Action Fraud is the primary formal reporting step for UK victims of cryptocurrency fraud. Reports are assessed centrally by the NFIB and, where they meet certain thresholds or form part of a pattern, referred to the appropriate police force for investigation.

How to submit your report

  1. Go to actionfraud.police.uk. The reporting tool is available online 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. Alternatively, you can call 0300 123 2040, available Monday to Friday 8am–8pm. If you are deaf or hard of hearing, a textphone service is available on 0300 123 2050.
  2. Create an account or continue as a guest. Creating an account allows you to return and update your report later, which is useful if you obtain additional evidence after the initial submission. For a one-off report, the guest route is faster.
  3. Select the fraud type. For cryptocurrency investment scams, select "Investment fraud" or "Online shopping and auction fraud" depending on the nature of the scam. For pig-butchering or romance-investment hybrids, "Investment fraud" is the most appropriate category. For cases involving a fake recovery firm that charged you upfront fees, select "Recovery fraud."
  4. Complete the incident report. Provide your timeline, the scammer's contact details, the amounts involved, and the transaction IDs and wallet addresses you gathered before reporting. The more detail you provide, the more useful the report is to the NFIB's pattern-matching analysis.
  5. Note your crime reference number (CRN). At the end of the process, Action Fraud will issue a CRN. Keep this safe. You will need it if you subsequently engage a solicitor, make a bank chargeback claim, instruct CountWize for on-chain tracing, or wish to follow up on your report.
  6. Report to your bank immediately. Call your bank's fraud line as soon as possible after reporting to Action Fraud. If you made payments via bank transfer, your bank may be able to recall or freeze the transaction if it is recent enough. For credit card payments, you may have a Section 75 claim for amounts over £100.

Reporting bitcoin fraud specifically

If your loss specifically involved Bitcoin or other cryptocurrency sent to a wallet address (rather than a payment made through an exchange account), the same Action Fraud process applies. The key difference is the evidence you provide: your TXIDs and wallet addresses are the digital equivalents of a cheque number and bank account number. These are the items the NFIB will pass to law enforcement and to specialist blockchain intelligence units such as the National Cyber Crime Unit (NCCU) if a referral is made. Include them explicitly in the free-text sections of your report, even if the form does not specifically request them.

How to check and use the FCA Warning List

The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) maintains a Warning List of firms it knows are targeting UK consumers without authorisation. The list is publicly searchable and is one of the most accessible tools available to individuals who suspect they are dealing with — or have been defrauded by — an unauthorised firm. It is also an important step before engaging with any new firm offering investment or on-chain tracing.

Searching the Warning List

Go to register.fca.org.uk/s/search. In the search bar, enter the firm's name, website address, or phone number. The search is not case-sensitive. If the firm appears on the Warning List, the FCA has identified it as operating without authorisation or targeting UK consumers in a way the FCA considers unlawful. You should not send any further funds to a firm on the Warning List.

Checking the Financial Services Register

The Warning List covers firms the FCA has specifically flagged, but it is not exhaustive — not every unauthorised firm will have been identified yet. For a more complete check, also search the FCA's Financial Services Register at register.fca.org.uk. This is the official list of firms that are authorised or registered with the FCA. If a firm claims to be FCA-authorised and does not appear on the Register — or appears with different contact details than those the firm gave you — it is almost certainly a clone firm impersonating a legitimate business.

Clone firm fraud

Clone fraud is a specific form of authorised push payment fraud in which a scammer copies the name, FCA registration number, and branding of a legitimate, FCA-authorised firm and uses those details to appear credible to victims. The legitimate firm's details will appear on the FCA Register — but the phone number, email address, and website the scammer gave you will be different. Always independently verify contact details by looking up the firm directly on the FCA Register, rather than using the contact details the firm itself provided. The FCA's ScamSmart pages at fca.org.uk/scamsmart explain clone fraud in detail and include guidance on how to verify contact details.

Reporting an unauthorised firm to the FCA

If the firm that defrauded you is not on the Warning List, you can report it to the FCA directly at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-us. The FCA reviews these reports and adds firms to the Warning List where appropriate. Reporting also contributes to the FCA's enforcement intelligence. Note that the FCA is a regulator, not a compensation scheme — it will not directly recover your funds, but it can take action to prevent others from being defrauded by the same firm.

How to report misleading crypto adverts to the ASA

The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) is the UK's independent advertising regulator. It has jurisdiction over marketing communications published in the UK, including adverts on social media platforms, websites, search results, video-sharing platforms, and email. Since October 2023, financial promotions for qualifying cryptoassets that are not approved by an FCA-authorised person are illegal under the UK Financial Services and Markets Act 2000, and the FCA has expanded ASA's remit to include cryptoasset adverts.

What the ASA can act on

The ASA can investigate complaints about adverts that are misleading, harmful, or irresponsible. In the context of crypto, this includes: adverts that present crypto investment as low-risk or guaranteed-return; adverts that fail to display prominent risk warnings (required by FCA rules for cryptoasset promotions); adverts that use celebrities or endorsements in a misleading way; adverts promoting unlicensed firms as if they were FCA-authorised; and adverts that target vulnerable consumers or use high-pressure marketing language. The ASA cannot investigate paid-for editorial content, private communications (such as cold-call messages or unsolicited DMs), or fraud that does not take the form of a published advert.

How to make a complaint to the ASA

  1. Go to asa.org.uk/make-a-complaint.html. The complaints form is available online. You do not need to create an account.
  2. Provide the advert details. Include the URL of the advert or the platform it appeared on, a screenshot if you have one, and the date you saw it. The more specific you are, the easier it is for the ASA to locate the advert.
  3. Describe why the advert is misleading. State clearly why you believe the advert breaches the ASA's Advertising Codes — for example, that it promises guaranteed returns on a crypto investment, does not carry a required risk warning, or promotes a firm that appears on the FCA Warning List.
  4. Submit your complaint. The ASA will acknowledge receipt and, if the complaint is taken forward, will contact the advertiser. If a complaint is upheld, the ASA typically requires the advert to be removed or amended and publishes its ruling. Rulings are searchable at asa.org.uk/rulings.html.

Reporting crypto promotions to the FCA directly

For cryptoasset financial promotions that you believe are illegal — not merely misleading but published without FCA authorisation — you can also report directly to the FCA at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-us. The FCA has taken enforcement action against firms issuing illegal cryptoasset promotions, including issuing 146 alerts in the first year of the expanded regime. Reporting to both the ASA and the FCA simultaneously is entirely appropriate for the most serious cases.

What happens after you report: realistic expectations

One of the most important things to understand about reporting a cryptocurrency scam in the UK is the distinction between recording a report and securing an outcome. These are not the same thing, and confusing them leads to frustration and, sometimes, to victims falling prey to secondary scams offering to "fast-track" their case through official channels — which is not something any legitimate firm can do.

What typically happens with Action Fraud reports

When you submit a report to Action Fraud, it is reviewed by the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau. The NFIB's primary function is intelligence — identifying patterns across multiple reports rather than investigating individual cases. The majority of individual Action Fraud reports do not result in a direct police investigation into your specific case. This is not a reflection of the seriousness of your loss; it reflects the volume of fraud reported in the UK (the NFIB received over 800,000 reports in 2023–24) and the investigative resources available to police forces. Your report contributes to a larger dataset that the NFIB uses to build cases against organised fraud networks, some of which are pursued by specialist units including the National Cyber Crime Unit.

Where Action Fraud reports are most likely to lead to investigation: cases involving losses above significant thresholds, cases where multiple victims have reported the same firm or network, cases with clearly identifiable UK-based suspects, and cases supported by detailed on-chain tracing evidence that points to specific regulated exchanges or identifiable accounts. This is one of the reasons a written on-chain tracing intelligence report can add genuine value to a law enforcement submission.

What typically happens with FCA reports

The FCA receives thousands of reports about unauthorised firms each year. Its primary response is to add confirmed unauthorised operators to the Warning List and to issue public consumer alerts. In some cases — particularly where a firm is operating at scale, targeting large numbers of UK consumers, or where there is evidence of significant consumer harm — the FCA may take civil or criminal enforcement action. FCA enforcement proceedings are lengthy, typically taking months to years, and do not directly return funds to victims. However, in rare cases where enforcement results in an asset freeze or court order against a firm, victims who have formally reported to both Action Fraud and the FCA are better positioned to participate in any subsequent compensation process.

What your bank can realistically do

If you made payments via bank transfer, your bank has obligations under the Contingent Reimbursement Model Code (for PSPs that have signed up) and under the Payment Systems Regulator's (PSR) new mandatory reimbursement rules for authorised push payment fraud, which came into force in October 2024. For bank transfers made to purchase cryptocurrency that was then sent to a scammer — the chain is: bank transfer to exchange, exchange converts to crypto, crypto sent to scammer — the reimbursement picture is more complex. The PSR rules apply to the bank-to-exchange leg of the payment; the crypto-to-scammer leg is on-chain and cannot be reversed. Your bank should still be contacted immediately: faster payment recalls are sometimes possible within hours of a transfer.

A realistic timeline

Most victims who report to Action Fraud do not receive any direct police contact within the first weeks or months. The FCA Warning List is updated when the FCA identifies and confirms an unauthorised firm, which may not happen immediately after your report. An ASA complaint, if accepted and investigated, typically results in a ruling within several months. For most UK crypto fraud victims, the most realistic near-term financial pathway — if one exists — runs through their bank's dispute processes rather than through law enforcement. On-chain tracing supports that pathway by creating a documented evidential chain that did not previously exist.

Beware of recovery scammers. After reporting, you may be contacted by individuals or firms claiming to be able to recover your funds through official channels, for an upfront fee. No legitimate firm charges upfront fees as a condition of starting a trace, and no firm can "fast-track" a law enforcement investigation. How CountWize's on-chain tracing can support your report

On-chain tracing is a forensic discipline that analyses publicly available blockchain data to reconstruct the movement of cryptocurrency from a theft or fraud event. Because every confirmed blockchain transaction is permanently and publicly recorded — regardless of whether law enforcement has been involved — it is possible to trace the path of stolen or fraudulently obtained crypto from the victim's wallet through subsequent transactions, often to a point where the funds are deposited at a regulated exchange.

What a trace can establish

A CountWize on-chain trace starts from the transaction IDs and wallet addresses you provide and works forward through the blockchain. The trace can establish: which addresses received your crypto immediately after you sent it, whether those funds were subsequently moved to additional wallets, whether the funds were consolidated with funds from other victims (a common pattern in organised fraud operations), which exchanges those funds were ultimately deposited with, and whether any of those exchanges are regulated, KYC-compliant institutions operating in jurisdictions with mutual legal assistance arrangements with the UK. This information is compiled into a written intelligence report.

How the report supports your Action Fraud submission

An on-chain tracing intelligence report gives an Action Fraud submission something it would otherwise lack: a documented, forensically sourced evidential chain showing where your crypto went. This is the equivalent of a bank having a record of exactly which account received a transfer, rather than simply knowing that a transfer was made. Reports formatted for law enforcement submission can accompany your crime reference number and, in cases where the NFIB or a specialist police unit reviews the report, provide the exchange attribution data that is most useful for any subsequent legal process.

Norwich Pharmacal Orders and exchange disclosure

When a trace identifies a regulated exchange as a destination for stolen or fraudulently obtained funds, there is a specific UK legal mechanism that may be available: a Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO). This is a court order — made under the inherent jurisdiction of the High Court — that compels the exchange to disclose the identity of the account holder who received the funds. NPOs have been used successfully in UK cryptocurrency fraud cases. Obtaining one requires instructing a solicitor and pursuing a court application; CountWize is not a solicitor and cannot make that application on your behalf. However, the on-chain tracing intelligence report CountWize produces is the evidence base on which an NPO application is built. CountWize can refer you to specialist crypto-litigation solicitors who can advise on whether an NPO is viable in your specific case.

What tracing cannot do

On-chain tracing cannot reverse a blockchain transaction. Once a transaction is confirmed on the blockchain, it is permanent — no regulator, court, or technical process can undo it. Tracing also cannot compel an exchange to freeze funds or return them: that requires a court order obtained by a qualified legal practitioner. And tracing cannot guarantee that any investigation will result in the return of funds to you. CountWize will give you an honest assessment at the outset of any case review and will not proceed with a paid engagement in scenarios where tracing is unlikely to produce any useful intelligence.

Frequently asked questions

What is Action Fraud and what happens when I report?

Action Fraud is the UK's national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime, operated by the City of London Police. When you submit a report, you receive a crime reference number (CRN). Your report is assessed by the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB), which uses aggregated data from multiple victims to identify patterns and refer cases to the appropriate police force for investigation. Individual follow-up varies depending on case volume and the strength of available evidence. Reporting does not automatically trigger an investigation — but it creates a formal record and contributes to the data the NFIB uses to build cases.

What is the FCA Warning List and how do I use it?

The FCA Warning List is a database of firms the FCA knows are operating without authorisation or are targeting UK consumers. You can search it at register.fca.org.uk/s/search. Enter the firm's name, website, or phone number. If the firm appears on the Warning List, it is a confirmed unauthorised operator and you should report directly to the FCA. If it does not appear, it may not yet have been identified — search the Financial Services Register to check whether the firm holds any legitimate authorisation. Always verify the firm's contact details independently, as clone fraudsters impersonate legitimate firms using near-identical branding.

Can I report a misleading crypto advert to the ASA?

Yes. The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) regulates marketing communications in the UK, including crypto adverts on social media, websites, and search results. You can make a complaint at asa.org.uk/make-a-complaint.html. The ASA can uphold complaints and require advertisers to withdraw or amend misleading adverts. It cannot award compensation or fine firms. Since October 2023, financial promotions for cryptoassets that are not approved by an FCA-authorised person are illegal under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000.

What information do I need before I report a crypto scam?

Before reporting, gather: all transaction IDs (TXIDs) from your blockchain transactions, wallet addresses involved (both yours and the scammer's), screenshots of all communications, records of any payments made, the website URL, phone numbers, and email addresses used by the scammers, and a written timeline of events with dates. The more detail you can provide, the more useful your report will be to investigators and to any on-chain tracing that may follow.

What is realistic to expect after reporting a crypto scam?

Reporting is an important step, but it rarely leads to swift or direct recovery of funds. Action Fraud reports are triaged by the NFIB — the majority of individual cases are not actively investigated by police unless they form part of a larger pattern or exceed certain thresholds. The FCA can take enforcement action against unlicensed firms but this typically takes months or years. The ASA can require adverts to be removed but cannot compel repayment. For UK victims, the most realistic near-term paths to any financial outcome involve a bank chargeback, a Section 75 claim, or civil proceedings supported by on-chain tracing evidence.
