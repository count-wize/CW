What counts as a crypto scam

The term "crypto scam" covers a wide range of fraud types. Before deciding what steps to take, it helps to understand which category your situation falls into, because different fraud types create different evidence trails and different reporting routes.

Investment scams and fake platforms

You were approached — often via social media, a messaging app, or a romantic connection — with a persuasive investment opportunity. The platform may have appeared to show real-time returns. In reality, the platform was fabricated, and the funds were collected and moved to attacker-controlled wallets. The UK's FCA ScamSmart register lists several thousand of these firms. Victims of this fraud type frequently have detailed on-chain records of the fraudulent wallets.

Pig-butchering scams

A subcategory of investment fraud that involves a prolonged grooming period — typically weeks or months of messaging — before the investment "opportunity" is introduced. The name comes from the practice of fattening a pig before slaughter. UK victims of pig-butchering scams reported losses of over £37 million to Action Fraud in 2024. These cases produce extensive digital evidence across messaging platforms, which can support both a criminal report and a civil tracing exercise.

Recovery scams

You have already been defrauded, and a second actor has now contacted you claiming to be a law firm, a regulator, a government agency, or a specialist firm that can recover your funds — usually in exchange for an upfront fee. This is known as a recovery scam or reload fraud. The FCA and Action Fraud have both warned about a sharp rise in recovery scam activity targeting crypto fraud victims. See our recovery scam warning — how fake firms target victims twice.

Exchange fraud and collapsed platforms

Your funds were held on a centralised exchange that has since suspended withdrawals, entered administration, or disappeared entirely. The collapse of FTX in 2022 affected an estimated 80,000 UK users. In these cases, the appropriate route is usually to join the creditor process — action fraud reporting and on-chain tracing are less relevant unless you believe internal fraud was involved.

The first 24 hours: your step-by-step checklist

The actions you take — or do not take — in the first 24 hours after a crypto scam significantly affect what can be done later. Evidence degrades quickly: accounts are deleted, devices are wiped, and fraudsters move funds through multiple wallets to obscure the trail. The checklist below applies to most types of crypto fraud.

  1. Stop all further transactions immediately. Do not make any additional deposits, do not withdraw funds to a new wallet "for safety" as the scammer may suggest, and do not sign any new transactions. If your device is believed to be compromised, disconnect it from the internet.
  2. Screenshot everything before accounts are deleted. Capture: wallet addresses you sent funds to, transaction IDs (txIDs — a string of letters and numbers that identifies each on-chain transaction), any platforms or apps you used, conversations (WhatsApp, Telegram, email, dating app messages), URLs of websites or dashboards shown to you, any identification documents or photos the fraudster provided. Save copies to a secure location that does not rely on the same account or device as the fraud.
  3. Report to Action Fraud online at actionfraud.police.uk. You will receive a crime reference number (CRN). Keep this number — you will need it for every subsequent step, including bank disputes, exchange complaints, and civil claims. Action Fraud is the UK's national reporting centre for fraud and cybercrime and is operated by the City of London Police. Reports are assessed and disseminated to specialist policing units including the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB).
  4. Contact your bank immediately if you used a bank transfer to fund the purchase. Under the Payment Systems Regulator's Authorised Push Payment (APP) reimbursement rules, which came into force in October 2024, UK banks are required to reimburse most APP fraud victims up to £85,000 provided the payment was made via Faster Payments or CHAPS. Time limits apply and the claim is stronger the faster you report. Tell the bank you have a crime reference number from Action Fraud.
  5. If you bought crypto on a regulated exchange, file a formal complaint with that exchange within 72 hours. Many exchanges — Coinbase, Binance, Kraken, and others — operate internal fraud response teams and can, in some circumstances, freeze funds in destination accounts or provide transaction records to law enforcement. The 72-hour window is not universal, but acting quickly significantly improves the chance of any cooperative response.
  6. Do not pay anyone claiming they can "reverse" the transaction. On-chain transactions cannot be reversed. Any firm or individual claiming otherwise is running a recovery scam. This includes anyone who contacts you unsolicited, claims to be from the government, the FCA, a police unit, or a crypto-specialist law firm. The FCA's ScamSmart warning list includes many firms using these tactics. CountWize does not charge upfront fees and does not claim to reverse transactions — we trace them.
  7. Consult a solicitor or on-chain tracing firm if the amount is significant. For losses above approximately £5,000, a professional on-chain tracing report may support a civil Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) application to compel an exchange to disclose account holder details. For losses of this scale, obtaining specialist advice promptly is important because some civil remedies depend on the assets still being traceable to a known account. What on-chain tracing can tell you

On-chain tracing is the process of analysing public blockchain transaction data to follow the movement of cryptocurrency from the point of loss through subsequent wallets and accounts. Every transaction on a public blockchain — Bitcoin, Ethereum, and most other major chains — is permanently and publicly recorded. The record includes the sending address, the receiving address, the amount, and the timestamp.

What a tracing exercise produces

A professional on-chain tracing report typically documents: the movement of funds from the victim's wallet through a series of intermediate addresses; any clustering of addresses that suggests the same actor controls multiple wallets; whether funds reached a known centralised exchange (which can then be subject to a law enforcement request or a NPO application); and the current status of the funds — still held in a wallet, further dispersed, or exchanged into fiat currency.

The output is an intelligence report. It is evidence, not a remedy. What you and your legal advisers do with it — whether that is submitting it to Action Fraud, instructing a solicitor to pursue an NPO, or using it in civil litigation — is a separate step.

What on-chain tracing cannot do

On-chain tracing cannot reverse a transaction. It cannot force an exchange to freeze or return funds. It cannot identify an anonymous wallet holder without a further legal process. And it cannot guarantee that the intelligence will lead to a recovery outcome — that depends on many factors outside any tracing firm's control, including whether the funds still exist, whether the counterparty exchange cooperates, and whether law enforcement prioritises the case.

How to report to the FCA ScamSmart register

The FCA's ScamSmart service allows you to report an unregulated firm or individual that contacted you about a financial product or investment. Reporting to ScamSmart serves two purposes: it may help the FCA add the firm to its warning list, protecting future victims; and it creates a formal record that you believed the firm to be acting fraudulently, which can be relevant in later legal proceedings.

To report, visit fca.org.uk/scamsmart and use the "Report a scam" function. You will be asked for: the name of the firm or individual, any website URLs or contact details they used, and details of the investment or offer. You do not need to have lost money — you can report a suspected scam before transferring any funds.

The FCA will not investigate individual cases, and reporting to ScamSmart does not constitute a formal complaint that triggers FCA investigation. For that, you need Action Fraud. However, the ScamSmart warning list is used by banks, search engines, and potential victims to check firm legitimacy — contributing to it has real protective value.

You can also check whether the firm that contacted you already appears on the FCA warning list by searching at fca.org.uk/scamsmart before investing in any opportunity.

What happens after you report

It is important to have realistic expectations about what follows a report to Action Fraud. The City of London Police processes tens of thousands of crypto fraud reports each year. Most reports are assessed by the NFIB and graded by a combination of financial scale, available intelligence, and actionability. The majority of individual consumer reports do not result in a police investigation of the specific case.

Police resource constraints

UK policing of crypto fraud is improving, but it remains under-resourced relative to the scale of the problem. The National Crime Agency, Metropolitan Police Cyber Crime Unit, and regional Economic Crime Units handle the most significant cases, often those involving millions of pounds and organised crime groups. For individual consumer fraud, the realistic outcomes from a police report are: the case data contributing to a broader operation targeting the same group; and the crime reference number providing formal documentation of the fraud for use in civil proceedings, bank disputes, and tax reporting.

Exchange cooperation timelines

When funds reach a regulated exchange, the exchange may be requested to freeze the account under a court order or a voluntary cooperation agreement with law enforcement. Exchanges are generally required to respond to production orders from UK law enforcement. However, voluntary cooperation — where the exchange acts without a court order — is inconsistent, and many exchanges require a formal legal process before taking action. This is another reason why acting quickly and engaging specialist help matters: applications for interim injunctions or NPOs are time-sensitive.

Civil versus criminal routes

Criminal prosecution of overseas crypto fraudsters is rarely possible in practice. The more realistic route for significant individual losses is civil litigation: identifying the pseudonymous wallet owner via NPO, obtaining a freezing injunction on exchange accounts, and pursuing a civil claim for the loss. This requires legal representation and involves material costs — it is generally only economic for losses above £10,000–£20,000. The National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC) provides further guidance on reporting and response options at ncsc.gov.uk.

Common mistakes in the first 24 hours

In our experience reviewing cases, the same errors appear repeatedly. Avoiding them improves the prospects of every subsequent step.

Paying a recovery scammer

This is the most common and most damaging mistake. In the hours after discovering a fraud, victims are in a heightened state of distress and urgency. Fraudsters exploit this by contacting victims — sometimes within hours, sometimes via fake "Action Fraud" emails, fake law firm letters, or fake regulator calls — and offering to help for an upfront fee. This is always a scam. No legitimate firm charges an upfront fee to begin a case review. No regulator contacts you to offer individual recovery services. Any firm doing so should be reported to Action Fraud immediately and to the FCA at fca.org.uk/scamsmart.

Deleting or losing evidence

Apps are deleted, chat history is cleared, email threads are archived. Every piece of communication with the fraudster is potentially evidential. Screenshots must be made immediately and stored somewhere separate — email them to yourself, store them on a USB drive, or use a cloud account the fraudster does not have access to. Transaction IDs are especially important: they allow any tracing firm or law enforcement agency to locate the on-chain record independently.

Waiting too long before reporting

Bank dispute windows close, exchange complaint deadlines pass, and on-chain evidence becomes harder to interpret as funds move further down the chain. The PSR APP reimbursement scheme has a 13-month reporting window, but practically, claims reported within days are far more likely to be resolved in the victim's favour. Action Fraud reports submitted promptly are more likely to intersect with live intelligence operations. Every hour of delay costs options.

Making additional transactions in the belief you are "recovering" funds

Scammers frequently request a final payment — described as a "tax", "fee", "compliance charge", or "escrow" — before releasing fabricated profits. Any further payment to the same actors, or to new actors claiming to represent them, will be lost. If a platform or contact is requesting additional funds before releasing your investment, treat this as confirmation of fraud and do not pay.

Frequently asked questions

Can the police trace crypto?

UK law enforcement can and does use blockchain analytics tools to trace cryptocurrency transactions. However, police resources are finite and cases are prioritised by scale and complexity. Tracing can identify wallet addresses and exchange accounts, but converting that intelligence into an enforcement outcome depends on many further steps including cooperation from exchanges and, in some cases, international legal assistance. An on-chain tracing report from a specialist firm can be submitted alongside your Action Fraud report to help investigators.

Can my bank reverse a crypto payment?

Generally, no. Once funds are used to purchase cryptocurrency on an exchange, the payment to the exchange itself may be subject to a chargeback claim if you paid by card, or a dispute under the PSR APP reimbursement scheme if you paid by bank transfer. However, the crypto transaction on-chain cannot be reversed. Contact your bank as soon as possible — time limits apply under the PSR APP reimbursement rules that came into force in October 2024.

How long does on-chain tracing take?

A basic on-chain tracing exercise — following a transaction through the blockchain to identify destination wallets and any linked exchange accounts — typically takes between three and ten working days for a straightforward case. Complex cases involving multiple chains, mixers, or many hops can take longer. CountWize will give you an honest scope estimate before any work begins.

What is a recovery scam?

A recovery scam is a fraud that specifically targets people who have already been scammed. The fraudster poses as a specialist firm, lawyer, or regulator and claims they can recover your lost cryptocurrency — usually in exchange for an upfront fee. Once you pay, they disappear or invent further fees. The FCA ScamSmart register lists known firms operating this way. Never pay anyone an advance fee to recover funds.
