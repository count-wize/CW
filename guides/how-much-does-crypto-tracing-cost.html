What on-chain tracing actually costs

On-chain crypto tracing is investigative analytical work, and like most professional analytical services, its cost depends on how much time and expertise each case requires. There is no universal market rate, and any firm that publishes a simple flat fee without assessing your case first should be treated with scepticism — cases differ too significantly in complexity for a fixed price to be meaningful.

In the UK, professional on-chain tracing generally involves a structured engagement. A preliminary case review assesses whether tracing is feasible and is likely to produce intelligence that is useful to you — given the evidence you have, the blockchain involved, the age of the transactions, and your intended next steps. If tracing is viable, the analyst works through your transaction data, maps the fund flows through intermediate wallet addresses, identifies where the funds ultimately settled (typically a regulated exchange or custodial service), and produces a written intelligence report.

The time required for this work ranges from a few hours for a simple, well-documented case on Bitcoin or Ethereum to many hours for complex cases involving multiple blockchains, obfuscation techniques, or older and less well-preserved transaction records. This is why cost is always case-specific.

At CountWize, every case begins with a free 15-minute initial review. During that review, a senior analyst assesses the basic facts of your situation — the blockchain involved, the approximate value, the age of the transactions, and what documentation you have. If we do not believe tracing will produce useful intelligence in your case, we will tell you honestly, and there is no cost or obligation at that stage.

We do not publish fixed pricing tiers because they are not meaningful: a case involving a single Bitcoin transaction to a UK-regulated exchange and a case involving twenty hop addresses across three blockchains are not the same product. Presenting them as the same price would be misleading.

This approach is intentional. Publishing a simple pricing table can create a false impression — either that the service is more straightforward than it is, or that a higher price means a better outcome. Neither is true of on-chain tracing. What matters is whether the specific evidence in your case supports a trace that is likely to produce actionable intelligence. That assessment comes first.

Why costs vary: the key factors

Understanding what drives the cost of an on-chain trace helps you assess whether your case is likely to be at the simpler or more complex end of the spectrum. Below are the main variables that determine how much analyst time a trace requires.

Case complexity and the number of hop addresses

The most significant cost driver is how many wallet addresses and transactions are involved in moving your funds from the point of theft to a final destination. Some fraudsters send funds directly to an exchange in one or two transactions — these cases are relatively fast to trace. Others use a chain of intermediate wallets, sometimes dozens, to obscure the trail before depositing at an exchange. Each additional hop requires additional analytical time, and cases involving what are sometimes called “peel chains” — where small amounts are peeled off to different addresses at each step — can be significantly more time-intensive than they first appear.

Which blockchain is involved

Bitcoin and Ethereum are the most commonly traced blockchains because they are the most standardised and because the professional analytics tools used by serious on-chain analysts — including Chainalysis, Elliptic, and comparable platforms — have the most comprehensive attribution data for these networks. Tracing on other blockchains (for example, BNB Smart Chain, Tron, Solana, or lesser-known tokens) is possible but may require different tools or more manual work, which affects the time required. If funds have been converted from one blockchain to another — a technique called chain-hopping — the trace must follow the conversion point, which adds complexity.

How much was lost and why that matters

The amount lost does not directly set the price of a trace, but it affects whether tracing is proportionate to pursue. A trace that costs several hundred pounds in analyst time is unlikely to be a reasonable investment for a loss of two hundred pounds. Equally, for a loss of ten thousand pounds or more where there is a clear on-chain trail, the cost of a trace may be a very small fraction of the potential downstream value — particularly if the intelligence report supports a Norwich Pharmacal Order application or a civil action. Proportionality is something we discuss explicitly at the initial review stage.

How old the transactions are

Recent transactions — those within the past twelve to twenty-four months — are generally faster to work with because the on-chain data is unambiguous and exchange account activity is more likely to be preserved within standard data-retention windows. Older transactions present greater challenges: exchanges may have purged KYC records, wallets may have been emptied and abandoned, and the trail may be harder to reconstruct cleanly. This does not necessarily make older cases untraceable, but it typically increases the analytical time required and reduces the certainty of attribution.

The urgency of the case

Urgency affects resource allocation. In most cases, on-chain tracing proceeds on a standard timeline: the analyst works through the case methodically, and the intelligence report is delivered within an agreed timeframe. In some situations — for example, where a victim has reason to believe funds are still active on-chain and may be moved further soon — expedited analysis may be appropriate. This affects resource scheduling and therefore cost.

The quality of evidence the client can provide

This is an underappreciated factor. When a client can provide complete and accurate transaction IDs (TXIDs), destination wallet addresses, dates of transfers, and records of any communications with the scammer, the analyst can begin the trace immediately from a clean starting point. When the documentation is incomplete — for example, where TXIDs are missing and must be reconstructed from the client's account statements or exchange records — additional preliminary work is required before the trace can begin. If you have lost crypto to fraud, preserving your transaction records in as much detail as possible is the single most useful step you can take before requesting a case review. DIY tools versus professional tracing

Free and publicly accessible blockchain explorer tools — such as Blockchain.com, Etherscan, and BscScan — allow anyone to look up wallet addresses and transaction histories on the public blockchain ledger. If you have your transaction ID, you can see exactly where your funds went in the next transaction. This is genuinely useful information, and you should do it before speaking to anyone about your case.

However, there is a significant gap between knowing the next wallet address in a chain and being able to attribute that wallet to a specific exchange with sufficient confidence to support a legal action. Professional tracing adds several layers that free tools do not provide:

  • Cluster analysis: professional platforms aggregate data across millions of transactions to identify which wallet addresses are controlled by the same entity, even when they appear unrelated on the public chain.
  • Exchange attribution: commercial analytics platforms maintain databases that map wallet address ranges to known exchanges and custodial services — information that is not visible on the public chain and requires years of aggregated intelligence to build.
  • OSINT correlation: open-source intelligence techniques can link on-chain wallet activity to off-chain data sources — forum posts, exchange communications, IP data associated with transaction broadcasting — to strengthen attribution.
  • Documented report format: for a trace to support a solicitor's Norwich Pharmacal Order application or an Action Fraud submission, it needs to be presented in a structured, evidenced format that a court or law enforcement can act on. A blockchain explorer screenshot is not that format.

If your case involves a straightforward one or two hop trace to a well-known exchange and you are simply trying to understand what happened, free tools may give you enough information. If you are looking to use the trace as the basis for legal action, exchange disclosure, or a formal fraud report with specific attribution data, professional tracing produces a materially different output.

Important: There are a number of firms online that advertise “crypto tracing” and “crypto recovery” services, charge substantial upfront fees, and then produce nothing of evidential value. These are scams targeting fraud victims who are already vulnerable. Before engaging any tracing firm, check it is a registered UK company at Companies House and verify it does not make guaranteed recovery claims. Is tracing worth pursuing in your case?

This is the question that matters most, and it is one that only an honest preliminary assessment can answer for your specific situation. That said, there are some general indicators that help frame whether tracing is likely to be a proportionate and useful step.

Indicators that tracing is likely to be useful

  • The funds were sent in cryptocurrency on a major public blockchain such as Bitcoin or Ethereum within the past 24 months
  • The loss is significant enough that the cost of a trace is proportionate — in most cases this means a loss well above £1,000
  • You have preserved transaction IDs, wallet addresses, and any communications with the individual or platform that defrauded you
  • You intend to pursue formal action — whether through Action Fraud, a solicitor, or your bank — and need documented intelligence to support that
  • The funds have not been converted to a privacy coin such as Monero

Indicators that tracing may have limited value in your case

  • The transactions occurred more than 36 months ago with no recent on-chain activity
  • You have already engaged another firm that claimed to have traced or recovered the funds — subsequent tracing will need to establish what, if anything, the previous work produced
  • The loss involved cash or gift cards with no blockchain component — on-chain tracing requires on-chain data
  • Funds were routed entirely through Monero or other privacy coins with no exchange conversion points — these cases hit technical limits that current tracing methods cannot fully resolve
  • The amount lost is below £1,000 — the analytical cost is unlikely to be proportionate

If you are unsure which category your case falls into, the most efficient path is to request the free initial review. A 15-minute conversation with a senior analyst will give you a clearer picture of whether tracing is likely to produce anything useful, without any cost or obligation on your part. If the answer is no, we will tell you and suggest alternative steps you can take.

Frequently asked questions

How much does crypto tracing cost in the UK?

Costs are assessed on a case-by-case basis because every trace is different. Key factors include the complexity of the blockchain trail, the number of intermediate wallet addresses, which blockchain the funds moved through, how old the transactions are, and how complete your documentation is. At CountWize, every case begins with a free initial 15-minute review so we can assess whether tracing is likely to produce useful intelligence before any cost is discussed. We do not publish fixed price tiers because they are not meaningful for the variability of cases we see.

Is crypto tracing worth the cost?

It depends on two things: the value of what was lost and the quality of evidence available. Tracing is most likely to produce actionable intelligence when the amount lost is significant (typically above £1,000), the transactions occurred on a major public blockchain such as Bitcoin or Ethereum, the transfers are relatively recent, and you have preserved transaction IDs and wallet addresses. In cases where these factors are present, a documented intelligence report can support an Action Fraud submission, a Norwich Pharmacal Order application, or a solicitor-led civil action. CountWize will tell you honestly at the initial review stage whether tracing will add value in your specific situation.

Do I have to pay anything to find out if CountWize can help me?

No. CountWize offers a free initial 15-minute case review with no obligation. During that call, a senior tracing analyst will assess the basic facts of your case — when the funds were transferred, which blockchain was involved, what documentation you have, and the approximate value lost. If we do not believe tracing will produce useful intelligence in your situation, we will tell you clearly rather than proceed with a paid engagement.

What drives the cost of on-chain tracing up or down?

The main cost drivers are complexity and analyst time. A straightforward trace — one or two wallet hops on Bitcoin to a known exchange — takes less time than a trace involving dozens of hop addresses, cross-chain conversions, or mixing services. The blockchain involved matters too: Bitcoin and Ethereum traces are more standardised than lesser-known blockchains. Transaction age is also a factor — recent transactions with preserved TXIDs are faster to work with. Cases where clients can provide complete documentation (transaction IDs, dates, wallet addresses, communications) are always faster and less costly to trace.

Will CountWize guarantee to recover my crypto?

No — and any firm that does make that guarantee should be avoided. On-chain tracing establishes where funds went; it does not reverse blockchain transactions or compel exchanges to return funds. What a verified on-chain intelligence report can do is provide the evidential foundation for legal steps that may lead to recovery — including a Norwich Pharmacal Order application or civil court proceedings. Those legal steps are conducted by qualified solicitors, not by CountWize. We are transparent about this distinction because clients who understand it make better decisions about whether and how to proceed.

Who we cannot help

  • Your total crypto loss is under £1,000 in current value — the cost of a professional trace is unlikely to be proportionate at this level.
