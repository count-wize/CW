How on-chain evidence is used in UK court proceedings

This page explains what on-chain evidence is, how it is collected, its admissibility in UK civil and criminal courts, what courts expect from blockchain forensics reports, and the practical limitations of evidence-gathering in crypto fraud cases. What on-chain evidence is — and how it differs from traditional digital evidence

Every transaction recorded on a public blockchain — Bitcoin, Ethereum, and most other major networks — is permanently inscribed in a distributed ledger that anyone can inspect. When a crypto fraud occurs, the victim's loss is not simply a number on a screen: it corresponds to one or more on-chain transactions that can be identified, timestamped, and traced through the network. This is the raw material of on-chain evidence.

On-chain evidence differs from traditional digital evidence in several important ways. Traditional digital evidence — emails, server logs, device data — can be altered, deleted, or fabricated. The forensic challenge with traditional evidence is often authenticating that it has not been tampered with. On-chain data, by contrast, is cryptographically sealed into a consensus record maintained by thousands of independent nodes. No single party can alter a confirmed transaction after the fact. This makes blockchain records uniquely durable as evidence, but also raises distinct questions about interpretation and attribution that courts are increasingly having to address.

The components of on-chain evidence in a fraud case

In a typical crypto fraud case, on-chain evidence comprises several categories of data that, taken together, form the evidentiary picture a court or investigator needs:

  • Transaction IDs (TXIDs): The unique cryptographic hash identifying each transaction on the blockchain. TXIDs are the primary reference points that allow any analyst to locate and verify a transaction independently.
  • Wallet addresses: The pseudonymous identifiers used to send and receive cryptocurrency. Multiple addresses can be controlled by a single person or entity.
  • Timestamps and block heights: Each transaction is recorded in a specific block, which is assigned a sequential number and an approximate time. This creates an auditable chronological record.
  • Transaction values: The amounts transferred, denominated in cryptocurrency, which can be converted to fiat value at the relevant date and time using exchange rate data.
  • Clustering and flow analysis: Analytical conclusions about which addresses are likely controlled by the same entity, based on co-spending patterns, dust attacks, and other heuristics. This layer is where expert methodology matters most and where courts will scrutinise the reasoning.

The distinction between raw on-chain data (which is objective and publicly verifiable) and the analytical conclusions drawn from it (which require expert judgment and carry varying degrees of certainty) is one of the central issues in how courts evaluate blockchain forensics evidence. A competent expert report draws this distinction explicitly.

Pseudonymity, not anonymity: A common misconception is that cryptocurrency transactions are anonymous. They are pseudonymous — addresses are not directly linked to identities, but the transaction graph is publicly visible. On-chain tracing exploits this visibility to follow the path of funds. Attribution to a named person requires an additional step: linking an address to an identity, usually through exchange KYC data obtained via legal process.

Why this matters for UK fraud victims

UK fraud victims who have lost cryptocurrency to a scam often assume the funds are permanently untraceable. In practice, the public nature of most blockchain networks means that a skilled analyst can trace fraud proceeds through multiple hops, identify the exchanges where funds were deposited, and produce a detailed map of the movement. That map becomes the evidential foundation for legal remedies — but it is the start of a legal process, not the end of one. Understanding what on-chain evidence is and how it is produced helps victims make informed decisions about whether and how to pursue legal action.

How on-chain evidence is collected and preserved

The collection of on-chain evidence for use in legal proceedings follows a structured methodology. Because the underlying blockchain data is publicly accessible, the focus of collection is on documentation, chain of custody, and the integrity of the analytical process — rather than the extraction challenges that apply to device-based digital forensics.

Step 1: Identify and record the victim's starting transaction

The process begins with the victim's own transaction records. The victim should provide: the wallet address(es) from which they sent funds, the transaction ID(s) of each payment to the fraudster, the approximate date and time of each transaction, and the receiving wallet address(es) provided by the fraudster. Where victims do not have this information, exchange records and wallet app histories can usually reconstruct it. This initial data set is the anchor for all subsequent tracing.

Step 2: Trace fund flows through the blockchain

Starting from the identified transactions, the analyst follows the movement of funds through the blockchain network. This involves:

  • Querying public blockchain explorers (such as blockchain.com, etherscan.io, or equivalent) to map each hop in the transaction chain
  • Applying clustering heuristics to identify groups of addresses likely controlled by the same entity — for example, when multiple input addresses contribute to a single transaction (common-input ownership heuristic)
  • Cross-referencing identified addresses against commercial intelligence databases that attribute known exchange deposit addresses, mixer services, darknet markets, and other categorised entities
  • Documenting the entire transaction path with supporting screenshots, block explorer references, and data exports at each stage

Step 3: Attribution — linking addresses to entities

Attribution is the most legally significant and most contested step. When funds reach an exchange-hosted wallet address, the analyst can often identify the exchange from public intelligence databases. The exchange holds KYC (Know Your Customer) data that links the wallet to a named account holder — but that data requires a court order to access.

In some cases, additional OSINT (open-source intelligence) may permit partial attribution: a fraudster who reused an address across multiple transactions, left on-chain comments, or whose wallet interacted with identified social media or darknet accounts may be attributable through non-KYC routes. However, the reliability of OSINT attribution varies considerably, and any report relying on it should state clearly the basis and confidence level of each attribution claim.

Step 4: Documenting the analysis for legal use

Evidence collected for use in court proceedings must be documented in a way that is reproducible, transparent, and auditable. Best practice includes:

  • Recording all blockchain queries with timestamps and full URL references to public block explorers
  • Exporting raw transaction data in structured formats (CSV or JSON) alongside visual representations
  • Naming the analytical tools used (Chainalysis Reactor, Elliptic Investigator, TRM Labs, or equivalent) and their version numbers
  • Distinguishing clearly between objective data (the transaction record itself) and analytical inferences (the attribution of an address to an entity)
  • Retaining working files so that the analysis can be reviewed and challenged by an opposing expert
Preservation tip for victims: Even before instructing any analyst, victims can take steps to preserve evidence. Take screenshots of the fraudster's wallet addresses, any platform they used, and all communications. Export or save transaction records from exchanges and wallet apps. Do not delete any messages or emails. The earlier evidence is preserved, the more reliable the record — although the blockchain data itself does not expire.

Admissibility in UK civil and criminal courts

UK courts have accepted blockchain evidence in a growing number of civil and criminal cases. The legal framework for admissibility draws on established principles of documentary and expert evidence, applied to a novel type of data.

Civil proceedings

In civil proceedings before the High Court and lower courts, blockchain evidence is admissible as documentary evidence under the Civil Evidence Act 1995. The Act provides a broad framework for admitting hearsay and documentary evidence, and courts have treated blockchain transaction records as a form of documentary evidence — records produced by an automated process that are inherently reliable in the sense that they reflect what the network recorded.

The more significant issue in civil proceedings is not bare admissibility but weight: how much evidential weight a court will give to blockchain data depends heavily on how it is presented. A well-prepared expert report, following the requirements of Part 35 of the Civil Procedure Rules, carries substantially more weight than a raw data printout. Courts in cases involving cryptocurrency asset freezing and tracing — including cases before the Commercial Court and Chancery Division — have shown increasing familiarity with blockchain evidence and a willingness to act on it in urgent applications.

Criminal proceedings

In criminal proceedings, blockchain evidence is treated as digital evidence under the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (PACE) and the associated codes of practice. The Act recognises computer-produced records as evidence, subject to a presumption of reliability. For police investigations, blockchain analytics platforms are in routine use: forces including the Metropolitan Police use commercial tools to trace cryptocurrency fraud proceeds as part of investigations.

Victims who have obtained a private on-chain tracing report can submit it as part of their Action Fraud report. While this does not compel the police to use the report, it can assist the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB) in triaging and assigning cases, particularly where the tracing already identifies an exchange or jurisdiction that can be actioned.

Key cases illustrating judicial treatment

UK courts have addressed blockchain evidence in several significant cases:

  • Ion Science Ltd v Persons Unknown (2020): The Commercial Court granted a worldwide freezing injunction against unknown defendants in a crypto fraud case, accepting on-chain tracing evidence to establish the flow of funds and to identify the exchanges holding the proceeds.
  • AA v Persons Unknown (2019): The Commercial Court treated Bitcoin as a form of property capable of being subject to a proprietary injunction, and accepted blockchain evidence in support of the application — a landmark decision for crypto-asset litigation in England and Wales.
  • D'Aloia v Persons Unknown (2022): The Chancery Division accepted blockchain tracing evidence to support a worldwide freezing order and NPO applications against multiple cryptocurrency exchanges, demonstrating that courts are willing to act on on-chain evidence even against anonymous defendants where the asset trail is sufficiently documented.
Note on Scotland and Northern Ireland: This guide focuses on the law of England and Wales. Scottish civil procedure and Northern Irish courts operate under separate frameworks, though the broad principles of evidence law are similar. Victims in Scotland or Northern Ireland should seek advice from solicitors qualified in the relevant jurisdiction.

The "persons unknown" issue

A distinctive feature of many crypto fraud cases is that the defendant's identity is initially unknown. UK courts have developed a willingness to grant injunctions and make orders against "persons unknown" in crypto cases, provided the claimant can identify the relevant property (the on-chain asset trail) with sufficient precision. This makes the quality of the on-chain tracing evidence particularly important: the court must be satisfied that the property described in the order can actually be identified and that the defendant is likely to hold or control it.

What courts expect from blockchain forensics expert reports

In civil proceedings in England and Wales, expert witnesses are governed by Part 35 of the Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) and the associated Practice Direction 35. These rules impose a duty on experts that overrides their obligation to the party that instructs them: the expert's primary duty is to the court. This principle applies equally to blockchain forensics experts as to any other expert witness.

Core requirements for a Part 35-compliant expert report

Requirement What this means in practice
Expert's qualifications The report must set out the expert's relevant qualifications, training, and experience. For blockchain forensics, this includes familiarity with the specific tools used, any formal certifications (e.g. Chainalysis Reactor Certified Examiner), and relevant casework history.
Statement of instructions The report must state what the expert was asked to do — the specific questions they were instructed to address and any documents or data they were provided with.
Methodology and tools The analysis methodology must be described in sufficient detail for another expert to review it. Commercial tools must be named (with version). Analytical heuristics — such as common-input ownership or change-address identification — must be identified and their reliability addressed.
Distinction between facts and inferences The report must clearly distinguish between what the blockchain data objectively shows (transaction records, addresses, timestamps) and the inferences drawn from that data (attribution to an entity or person). Confidence levels should be stated for attributions that are probabilistic rather than certain.
Disclosure of limitations The expert must disclose the limitations of the analysis: gaps in the data, transactions through mixing or privacy services that obscure the trail, jurisdictions where attribution is not possible without legal process, and any assumptions made in the analysis.
Statement of truth and expert declaration CPR Part 35 requires the report to include a signed statement: "I confirm that I have made clear which facts and matters referred to in this report are within my own knowledge and which are not. Those that are not within my own knowledge I confirm are true to the best of my information and belief." Failure to include this renders the report non-compliant.
Duty to the court statement The report must include an express acknowledgement that the expert understands their duty is to the court, not to the instructing party, and that they have complied with that duty.

Why report quality matters

A blockchain forensics report that does not comply with CPR Part 35 can be excluded, given limited weight, or challenged effectively by an opposing expert. More fundamentally, a report that overstates the certainty of its conclusions — or that omits inconvenient gaps in the evidence — is likely to damage rather than assist the claimant's case if scrutinised at trial. Courts are increasingly sophisticated consumers of expert evidence and are not receptive to advocacy dressed as analysis.

For victims considering legal proceedings, the practical implication is this: before commissioning an expert report, ask any potential provider whether their reports are routinely used in Part 35 proceedings, whether they can produce a statement of truth and expert declaration, and whether they are willing to give oral evidence if required. A provider that cannot answer these questions confidently may not be producing court-ready evidence.

Single joint expert vs. party-appointed expert

In civil proceedings, courts sometimes direct that a single joint expert (SJE) be appointed rather than each party appointing their own expert. In crypto fraud cases, the cost and complexity of blockchain forensics analysis often makes SJE appointment impractical in practice — the claimant will typically have already commissioned a tracing report before proceedings begin. However, where an SJE is directed, the expert must be genuinely independent of both parties. Victims who have already obtained a tracing report should discuss with their solicitor how best to introduce it into proceedings where an SJE is directed.

Legal orders that use on-chain evidence — NPOs, freezing injunctions, and more

On-chain tracing evidence does not produce a legal outcome by itself. It enables specific legal remedies that a solicitor can apply for on the victim's behalf. The principal orders used in UK crypto fraud litigation are set out below.

Norwich Pharmacal Orders (NPOs)

A Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) is a civil court order requiring a third party that has been innocently mixed up in wrongdoing to disclose information. In the crypto fraud context, the "third party" is typically a cryptocurrency exchange that holds KYC data linking a wallet address to a named account holder.

The on-chain tracing report performs two functions in an NPO application:

  1. It demonstrates to the court that fraud has occurred and that funds moved from the victim to the specific exchange address identified in the order.
  2. It identifies precisely which exchange and which deposit address is the subject of the disclosure request, allowing the court to frame the order with sufficient precision.

Without a tracing report, an NPO application against an exchange has no evidential foundation. Courts will not make a disclosure order on the basis of a victim's assertion alone. The quality of the tracing report is therefore directly related to the prospects of a successful NPO application.

Many cryptocurrency exchanges are domiciled outside the UK, which complicates enforcement. However, exchanges with a UK operational presence — including many major platforms — have been successfully served with NPOs by the English courts. Enforcement against exchanges in non-cooperative jurisdictions remains more difficult.

Freezing injunctions (Mareva injunctions)

A worldwide freezing injunction prevents a defendant from dissipating assets — including cryptocurrency — pending the resolution of a claim. Courts can grant freezing injunctions on an urgent basis (without notice to the defendant, known as an ex parte application) where there is a real risk that assets will be dissipated if the defendant is informed.

On-chain tracing evidence supports a freezing injunction application by:

  • Identifying the crypto assets to be frozen with sufficient precision (wallet addresses, estimated value)
  • Demonstrating that those assets are the proceeds of the fraud alleged
  • Showing that there is a real risk of dissipation — for example, if the funds are still moving through the blockchain

In the cases of Ion Science (2020) and D'Aloia (2022), courts granted freezing injunctions against persons unknown on the strength of blockchain tracing evidence, a development that significantly expanded the practical utility of on-chain forensics for crypto fraud victims.

Bankers Trust orders

A Bankers Trust order is a form of disclosure order that requires an exchange or financial institution to provide information about assets held for an identified defendant. It is similar to an NPO but applies post-identification — once the defendant's identity is known — and requires the exchange to produce account records and transaction histories. On-chain tracing typically precedes a Bankers Trust application, providing the initial identification of the exchange and account.

Asset recovery via civil judgment

Where a defendant is identified (through NPO or otherwise), identified as being within the UK or a cooperative jurisdiction, and holds attachable assets, a civil judgment for the fraud can be enforced against those assets. Cryptocurrency held in an exchange account is attachable via a third-party debt order or charging order. The enforceability of judgments against defendants in non-cooperative jurisdictions — particularly common in crypto fraud cases originating in Southeast Asia or Eastern Europe — remains a significant practical constraint.

What on-chain evidence cannot achieve — the honest limits

The legal and analytical progress described above is genuine, but it sits alongside persistent limitations that any honest guide must set out clearly. UK fraud victims who approach legal action with realistic expectations about what on-chain evidence can and cannot achieve are better placed to make informed decisions about whether to proceed and what outcomes to plan for.

Attribution is probabilistic, not certain

On-chain tracing can follow the movement of funds with a high degree of accuracy. What it cannot always do is conclusively prove who controlled a particular wallet address. Wallet clustering heuristics are powerful tools, but they produce probabilistic inferences, not legal certainties. A competent expert report will state confidence levels for attribution claims — for example, "there is a high probability that addresses X, Y, and Z are controlled by the same entity, based on common-input patterns" rather than "X, Y, and Z are controlled by [Name]."

The step from address attribution to named-person attribution requires KYC data from exchanges, obtained via legal process. In cases where funds have passed through multiple exchanges across multiple jurisdictions, obtaining full KYC on every hop may require parallel legal proceedings in several countries — a process that is expensive, time-consuming, and not always possible.

Mixing services and privacy coins

Fraudsters who are aware of blockchain tracing methodologies can attempt to obfuscate the trail using mixing services (which pool and re-distribute cryptocurrency to break the transaction chain), chain-hopping (converting between blockchains to complicate tracing), or privacy coins such as Monero, which use cryptographic techniques to obscure sender, receiver, and amount.

Sophisticated obfuscation techniques do not make tracing impossible — analytics capabilities are advancing — but they can reduce the confidence of attribution conclusions and may make a report less useful as court evidence. In cases where significant obfuscation is involved, experts must be candid about the limitations of what can be inferred.

Jurisdiction and enforcement

A significant proportion of UK crypto fraud originates in or passes through exchanges and wallets located in jurisdictions that do not cooperate with UK court orders. An NPO obtained in the English courts has legal force in England and Wales; enforcing it against an exchange domiciled in, say, a jurisdiction with no mutual legal assistance treaty with the UK is a separate, difficult challenge.

Victims should discuss jurisdictional enforcement prospects frankly with their solicitor before committing to proceedings. A successful NPO against a UK-linked exchange is achievable for many cases; enforcing a judgment against a defendant domiciled in a non-cooperative overseas jurisdiction is a different proposition entirely.

Proportionality and cost

Civil litigation is expensive. Expert evidence costs money. NPO and freezing injunction applications involve court fees and solicitor time. For smaller losses — typically under £10,000–£20,000 — the cost of civil proceedings may exceed the realistic prospects of recovery, particularly where jurisdictional complexity is involved. Victims should seek a frank costs-benefits analysis from their solicitor before committing to proceedings, and should consider whether the criminal reporting route (Action Fraud, police investigation) is a more proportionate first step.

Evidence does not guarantee prosecution or recovery

Providing on-chain evidence to Action Fraud does not guarantee that the police will investigate, that the Crown Prosecution Service will charge anyone, or that any conviction will result in asset recovery for the victim. The criminal justice system has limited resources and a triage process that may not prioritise cases where the defendant is overseas or the amount is below a threshold that attracts investigative resource.

On-chain evidence is a tool — potentially a powerful one — in the hands of solicitors and law enforcement. It does not substitute for those processes. The purpose of obtaining a tracing report is to maximise the usefulness of whatever legal or investigative route is available to the victim, not to bypass those routes.

Beware of firms that promise outcomes: Any firm that guarantees asset recovery, commits to specific timelines, or claims that blockchain evidence alone will secure the return of funds is misrepresenting the legal process. No legitimate blockchain forensics provider or law firm can make those promises. See our guide: Recovery scam warning.

FAQ — on-chain evidence and UK court proceedings

Is blockchain evidence admissible in UK courts?

Yes, in both civil and criminal proceedings. UK courts treat blockchain transaction records as documentary evidence under the Civil Evidence Act 1995 and, in criminal matters, under PACE. Courts have accepted on-chain tracing reports in cases including freezing injunctions, Norwich Pharmacal Orders, and fraud prosecutions. The key question is not whether blockchain data is admissible but how it is presented — courts require expert evidence that explains the methodology, the limits of inference, and the reliability of any attribution to a named party.

What is a Norwich Pharmacal Order and how does it use blockchain evidence?

A Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) is a civil court order requiring a third party — most commonly a cryptocurrency exchange — to disclose the identity of an account holder. On-chain tracing identifies the exchange-hosted wallet address where fraud proceeds were deposited. That tracing report forms the evidence base for the NPO application, showing the court how funds moved from the victim's wallet to the specific exchange address. Without the tracing report, the claimant cannot demonstrate to the exchange or to the court which account requires disclosure.

What do UK courts expect from a blockchain forensics expert report?

Courts applying Part 35 of the Civil Procedure Rules expect expert witnesses to owe their primary duty to the court, not to the instructing party. A compliant blockchain forensics report must state the expert's qualifications and methodology, set out the limits of what the analysis can and cannot prove, identify the tools used (naming the software and version), explain how wallet clustering and attribution were performed, and include a signed statement of truth. Reports that assert conclusions beyond what the data supports — or that do not disclose analytical limitations — are likely to be challenged or given limited weight.

Can on-chain evidence alone recover my cryptocurrency?

No. On-chain tracing produces an intelligence report — it maps where funds moved and, where possible, attributes destination addresses to identifiable entities such as exchanges. That report then needs to be acted upon through legal process: typically an NPO to identify an account holder, followed by a civil claim or criminal referral. Evidence is the foundation for legal action, not a substitute for it. Whether legal action is viable depends on jurisdiction, the defendant's location, the amount involved, and other factors that a solicitor must assess.

Does on-chain evidence work for criminal proceedings in the UK?

UK police and prosecutors can and do use blockchain forensics in criminal investigations. Forces including the Metropolitan Police use commercial analytics platforms such as Chainalysis and Elliptic. Victims who report to Action Fraud can provide their own on-chain tracing report as supporting evidence, which may assist the NFIB in deciding how to prioritise the case. However, the decision to prosecute rests with the Crown Prosecution Service, and providing evidence does not guarantee prosecution. Independent tracing is most useful as a supplement to a police report, not as a replacement.

What are the limitations of blockchain evidence in court?

The main limitations are attribution, jurisdiction, and proportionality. On-chain tracing can prove that funds moved through a particular wallet address but cannot always prove who controlled that address — the link between an address and a named person typically requires exchange KYC data obtained via legal process. Cross-border cases are complicated by the need to enforce UK court orders in foreign jurisdictions. And proportionality matters: for smaller losses, the cost of expert evidence and legal proceedings may exceed the realistic prospects of any outcome. These limitations should be addressed honestly in any expert report.
