What MiCA is and when it came into full effect

The Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation — MiCA — is an EU-wide legal framework that creates a unified licensing and conduct regime for crypto-asset service providers across all 27 EU member states. It was adopted by the European Parliament in May 2023 and came into full effect in two stages: rules for stablecoins (asset-referenced tokens and e-money tokens) applied from June 2024, while the full regime for Crypto-Asset Service Providers (CASPs) — including exchanges, custodians, and trading platforms — came into force in December 2024.

Before MiCA, the regulatory landscape for crypto firms in the EU was a patchwork. Some member states had implemented national crypto registers or anti-money-laundering frameworks, while others had minimal or no sector-specific crypto regulation. CySEC in Cyprus, BaFin in Germany, the AMF in France, and CONSOB in Italy each had their own requirements, creating significant regulatory arbitrage opportunities. MiCA replaced this fragmentation with a single passport framework: a CASP authorised in any one member state can operate across all 27 EU countries without requiring separate national licences.

MiCA's scope: what is and is not covered

MiCA covers a defined list of crypto-asset services, including:

  • Operating a trading platform for crypto-assets (exchanges)
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency or other crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders for crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Placing crypto-assets (similar to securities underwriting)
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto-assets
  • Providing advice on crypto-assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto-assets

MiCA does not apply to crypto-assets that qualify as financial instruments under MiFID II (such as tokenised shares or bonds), nor to NFTs where there is a clear unique and non-fungible characteristic, nor to central bank digital currencies. Importantly for UK victims, MiCA does not cover peer-to-peer decentralised finance (DeFi) protocols where there is no identifiable legal entity — a gap that regulators have acknowledged.

How MiCA authorisation works in practice

To operate as a MiCA-authorised CASP, a firm must submit an application to its home-state National Competent Authority (NCA) — the financial regulator in the EU member state where the firm is registered. The NCA assesses the application against MiCA's requirements and, if approved, grants authorisation. Once authorised, the CASP notifies the European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA), which publishes the firm's details in a centralised register. The MiCA passport then allows the firm to offer its services across all EU member states.

The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) maintains the official register of authorised CASPs and crypto-asset white papers. This register is publicly searchable at esma.europa.eu and is the authoritative source for confirming whether a firm claiming MiCA authorisation is genuinely licensed.

Important: A firm claiming to be "MiCA-regulated" or "MiCA-licensed" is not necessarily authorised. The transition period following December 2024 allowed firms already operating under national regimes to continue operating provisionally while completing a full MiCA authorisation application. Always verify against the ESMA register. If a firm is not in the ESMA register and cannot provide its authorisation reference number, treat any MiCA claim as unverified.

How MiCA-licensed CASPs differ from unregulated platforms

Understanding the difference between a MiCA-licensed CASP and an unregulated platform matters significantly for a UK victim assessing what options are available. The presence or absence of MiCA licensing is not a guarantee of honesty — but it determines what regulatory infrastructure exists and which accountability mechanisms are potentially accessible.

What MiCA requires of authorised CASPs

MiCA imposes substantive conduct-of-business obligations on authorised CASPs, including:

MiCA Obligation What it requires in practice Relevance to fraud victims
Capital adequacy Minimum own-funds requirement depending on service type (€50,000–€150,000 for most services; higher for operating a trading platform) Creates financial substance — a MiCA CASP should not collapse instantly with no assets if investigated
Registered EU legal entity Must have a registered office in an EU member state with genuine management presence Creates a legally identifiable defendant or subject of regulatory investigation in an EU jurisdiction
Complaint-handling procedure Must have a written complaints procedure, handle complaints within defined timeframes, and keep records Provides a formal complaint route before escalating to the NCA
Travel Rule compliance Must collect, verify, and transmit originator and beneficiary information for qualifying crypto transfers Means the CASP holds KYC data on account holders — attributable to specific individuals
Segregation of client assets Must segregate client crypto-assets from the firm's own assets and maintain segregated records Reduces the risk that client funds are commingled with operational funds in insolvency
Clear disclosures and fair treatment Must publish a white paper for each crypto-asset offered; marketing must be fair, clear, and not misleading Creates a standard against which potentially misleading promotions can be assessed
AML and KYC Subject to EU Anti-Money Laundering Directive requirements — must verify client identity, monitor transactions, and report suspicious activity Creates an obligation to identify and flag transactions consistent with fraud proceeds

What an unregulated platform lacks

An unregulated crypto platform — one operating from an offshore jurisdiction with no MiCA, FCA, or equivalent national authorisation — has none of these obligations in practice. There is no NCA with supervisory jurisdiction. There is no mandatory complaint-handling procedure. There is no capital requirement. There is no Travel Rule compliance producing KYC records. There is frequently no registered EU legal entity that can be sued in a European court under accessible jurisdiction rules.

From a UK victim's perspective, the practical consequence is stark: if the platform that processed your funds is MiCA-licensed, there is a regulatory body with investigative powers and sanctioning authority you can formally approach. If the platform is unregulated and offshore, the immediate accountability routes are significantly narrower — on-chain tracing, civil proceedings in whatever jurisdiction the firm claims to be based (often unenforceable), and intelligence reports to law enforcement who may face significant cross-border constraints.

What MiCA means for a UK victim's legal and complaint position

MiCA is EU law. UK residents are not EU consumers and Brexit means that UK residents do not have automatic access to EU consumer protection regimes on the same basis as EU citizens. Understanding precisely what this means — and where the boundaries are — is essential for any UK victim assessing whether to pursue EU-based routes.

What MiCA does not give UK victims directly

MiCA does not create a private right of action that UK residents can exercise directly in an EU court simply by virtue of a CASP being MiCA-licensed. It does not entitle UK residents to financial compensation from a MiCA fund. It does not automatically apply English law or give English courts jurisdiction over an EU CASP. The Lugano Convention, which previously allowed EU and EFTA court judgments to be mutually recognised, no longer applies to the UK after Brexit. Enforcement of a UK court judgment in the EU (or vice versa) now involves more complex bilateral mechanisms.

What MiCA does give UK victims indirectly

Despite the direct-rights gap, MiCA creates several indirect benefits for UK victims:

  • A supervisory body to complain to: the CASP's home-state NCA has regulatory jurisdiction over the firm and can investigate conduct breaches. While a supervisory complaint is not the same as a private-law claim, it creates an official record, can trigger an investigation, and may result in sanction of the firm — which can in turn affect its reputation, regulatory status, and the leverage available to victims pursuing civil routes.
  • Access to KYC data through legal orders: because MiCA-compliant CASPs hold verified identity data on their account holders, a UK solicitor can potentially obtain a Norwich Pharmacal Order from an English court compelling the EU CASP to disclose that data — or instructing EU solicitors to seek equivalent disclosure orders in the relevant EU jurisdiction. The existence of the data (rather than its absence, as with unregulated platforms) is itself a prerequisite for this route.
  • Governing law and jurisdiction clauses: some MiCA-licensed CASPs — particularly those with UK customer bases — include English law as the governing law in their terms and conditions, potentially giving English courts jurisdiction even over an EU entity. If the CASP's terms specify English law, the UK courts may be the appropriate venue for a civil claim.
  • Evidence from the NCA investigation: in some cases, a formal NCA complaint can trigger an investigation that produces evidence (or NCA findings) useful in parallel civil proceedings. This is not guaranteed, and NCAs typically do not share investigation materials with individual complainants, but the fact of an ongoing investigation can be relevant context in civil litigation strategy.

The post-Brexit jurisdiction question

One of the most practically significant issues for UK victims pursuing EU-based routes is jurisdiction: which courts can hear a claim, and where can a judgment be enforced? This is a fact-specific legal question that depends on the governing law of the CASP's terms, the location of the CASP's registered entity, the nature of the claim, and the jurisdiction clause agreed between the parties. UK victims should obtain legal advice from a solicitor with EU cross-border experience before committing to any particular litigation route. CountWize does not provide this legal advice — we produce on-chain intelligence that solicitors can use as evidence within whatever legal strategy they adopt.

EU National Competent Authorities and how to submit a complaint

The key regulatory bodies that supervise MiCA-authorised CASPs are the National Competent Authorities (NCAs) of each EU member state. Under MiCA's home-state supervision model, the relevant NCA for any complaint is the one in the EU member state where the CASP is authorised — not necessarily the member state where the victim is based or where the alleged harm occurred.

Identifying the correct NCA

The first step is to identify which EU member state authorised the CASP. The ESMA register of authorised CASPs (available at esma.europa.eu) lists authorised firms with their home-state NCA identified. If the CASP is not yet on the ESMA register but was previously registered under a national crypto regime in a specific EU state, check the national register for that state's NCA.

How to submit a supervisory complaint to an NCA

Supervisory complaints to NCAs follow a broadly similar process across member states, though the specific forms and requirements vary by NCA. The general approach is:

  1. Exhaust the CASP's own complaint process first. Most NCAs require evidence that you have attempted to resolve the matter directly with the firm through its mandatory complaint-handling procedure before they will accept a supervisory complaint. Submit a formal written complaint to the CASP specifying the nature of your complaint, the transactions involved, and the outcome you are seeking. Request a written response within the timeframe specified in the CASP's complaints policy. Keep copies of all correspondence.
  2. Confirm the CASP's authorisation status and home-state NCA. Search the ESMA register at esma.europa.eu to confirm the firm's authorisation details and identify the correct NCA. Note the authorisation number — you will need this when submitting a complaint. If the CASP is not on the ESMA register, it may be in a national pre-MiCA regime register or may not be genuinely licensed at all. Do not assume a firm is licensed because it claims to be.
  3. Locate the NCA's supervisory complaint form or contact point. Each NCA has a specific process for receiving complaints about regulated firms. This is typically separate from consumer-facing complaint resolution services. For example, BaFin's supervisory complaint form is available at bafin.de/whistle; the AMF accepts complaints via its website at amf-france.org. Most NCA websites have English-language versions of their complaint portals. Note that submitting a supervisory complaint does not automatically trigger an NCA investigation — NCAs triage complaints based on regulatory priority.
  4. Compile your evidence pack. A supervisory complaint is more likely to be taken seriously if it presents clear, structured evidence of the alleged regulatory breach. Include: transaction records (account statements, withdrawal and deposit confirmations, blockchain transaction IDs); communications with the firm (email correspondence, chat logs, screenshots of the platform); a clear chronological narrative of what happened; and — where available — on-chain tracing documentation showing where funds moved after leaving the platform. NCAs are more likely to investigate complaints that allege specific, demonstrable conduct breaches (failure to apply KYC, failure to follow complaint procedures, misleading marketing) than general expressions of dissatisfaction.
  5. Submit and keep records. Submit the complaint via the NCA's official channel and retain confirmation of receipt. Response times vary significantly by NCA — some acknowledge complaints within days, others may take weeks. If you do not receive an acknowledgement within a reasonable period, follow up by the NCA's recommended contact method. Be aware that NCAs will generally not provide you with detailed updates on any investigation that results from your complaint; supervisory investigations are confidential regulatory processes, not consumer dispute resolution mechanisms.
Note: A supervisory complaint to an NCA is not the same as a private-law claim for compensation. NCAs can investigate the firm, impose sanctions, suspend authorisation, or refer matters to law enforcement. They do not award individual compensation to complainants as part of their supervisory role. If you are seeking compensation, you will need separate civil legal proceedings — for which on-chain tracing evidence and any NCA complaint record may be useful inputs.

How on-chain tracing evidence supports complaints and legal action

Whether you are pursuing a supervisory complaint to an EU NCA or instructing a solicitor to consider civil action against a MiCA-licensed CASP, on-chain tracing evidence plays a specific and practical role. Understanding what that role is — and what it is not — helps set realistic expectations before any tracing engagement begins.

What an on-chain intelligence report provides

An on-chain intelligence report produced by CountWize traces the movement of cryptocurrency through the blockchain from a starting point you provide — typically the wallet address you sent funds to, or the exchange deposit address identified in your transaction history. The report documents:

  • Transaction IDs (TXIDs) and timestamps: every transfer recorded on the blockchain with cryptographic precision — verifiable by any party independently against the public ledger.
  • Wallet address clustering: using heuristic techniques, addresses likely controlled by the same entity are grouped — helping to identify the scale of funds an address cluster holds and its pattern of activity.
  • Exchange identification: where funds reached a deposit address attributable to a known exchange, the report identifies the exchange. For MiCA-licensed exchanges, this means identifying a regulated entity that holds KYC data on the relevant account.
  • OSINT attribution: where publicly available information links wallet addresses to specific entities — for example, through published exchange hot wallet lists, court documents, or blockchain analytics attribution data — this context is included.
  • Fund-flow narrative: the report presents a chronological narrative of the fund flow that a non-technical reader (an NCA officer, a solicitor, a judge) can follow without specialist knowledge.

How on-chain evidence strengthens an NCA complaint

A supervisory complaint to an EU NCA that is supported by on-chain tracing evidence is substantively stronger than one based on account statements and communications alone. Specifically:

  • If the on-chain trace shows that your funds were processed through the CASP's identified deposit addresses and then moved in a pattern inconsistent with normal customer account management — for example, rapid consolidation through a series of addresses consistent with fraud operations — this provides evidence relevant to potential AML and Travel Rule breaches.
  • If the trace identifies that funds moved to an external wallet address immediately after arriving at the CASP — without any on-platform trading activity consistent with a legitimate customer experience — this is potentially relevant to a complaint about the CASP's KYC and transaction monitoring obligations.
  • The existence of verifiable blockchain evidence distinguishes a complaint from an unsubstantiated allegation. NCAs are more likely to initiate a supervisory review when complaints are grounded in objective, verifiable data.

How on-chain evidence supports civil legal action

For UK victims working with a solicitor to pursue civil proceedings, on-chain evidence is typically used in two ways. First, it establishes the factual transaction chain — showing precisely what happened to the funds — which is the foundation of any claim. Second, it identifies the regulated destination of funds, which enables the solicitor to target a Norwich Pharmacal Order or equivalent EU disclosure order at the identified CASP to compel production of the KYC identity data held for the relevant account. Without the on-chain trace identifying the destination exchange, the NPO application has no specific target.

MiCA's Travel Rule and what it means for evidence availability

One of the most practically significant aspects of MiCA for fraud victims is its Travel Rule requirement. The Travel Rule — originating from the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) guidance and implemented in the EU through the Transfer of Funds Regulation (TFR) — requires regulated crypto-asset service providers to collect and transmit verified identity information alongside qualifying crypto-asset transfers.

What the Travel Rule requires

Under MiCA and the updated Transfer of Funds Regulation, EU-regulated CASPs must, for crypto-asset transfers above €1,000 (and in some cases for all transfers regardless of size):

  • Collect the originator's name, distributed ledger address, and account number from the sending party
  • Collect the beneficiary's name and distributed ledger address
  • Transmit this information alongside the transfer to the receiving CASP where the beneficiary uses a regulated service
  • Screen counterparty information against sanctions lists
  • Retain records for the regulatory retention period

What this means for fraud victims

For a UK victim whose funds can be traced — through on-chain analysis — to a deposit address at a MiCA-regulated CASP, the Travel Rule compliance requirement has a significant practical implication: the CASP should hold verified identity data for the account holder at the destination address. If a fraudster deposited your cryptocurrency into a MiCA-compliant exchange, that exchange's Travel Rule and KYC records should identify who the fraudster is.

This data is not publicly available — it is protected by data-protection law and the exchange's confidentiality obligations. But it is obtainable through legal process. A UK solicitor can seek a Norwich Pharmacal Order from the English courts (where the exchange is subject to English jurisdiction) or equivalent disclosure orders in the relevant EU member state's courts, compelling the exchange to disclose the account-holder identity. The existence of the data — rather than the absence of any records, as with unregulated platforms — is a necessary prerequisite for this route to work.

Travel Rule gaps: the unhosted wallet problem

The Travel Rule applies to transfers between regulated CASPs and to transfers from regulated CASPs to unhosted (self-custody) wallets above threshold. However, if funds were transferred directly from your wallet to a fraudster's unhosted wallet — with no regulated CASP in the chain — the Travel Rule produces no identity data because there is no regulated entity required to collect and transmit it. In these cases, on-chain tracing may still identify the unhosted wallet's transaction history and potentially link it to subsequent deposits at a regulated exchange, but the evidence trail is harder to construct and the identity data is not guaranteed to exist.

Practical steps for UK victims with EU crypto losses

The following steps reflect what victims in this situation can practically do. This is educational information only — specific legal decisions should be taken with the benefit of professional legal advice from a solicitor familiar with EU cross-border financial disputes.

  1. Identify whether the platform involved is genuinely MiCA-licensed. Search the ESMA register at esma.europa.eu for the exact company name and any related entity names. Check the home-state NCA's published register of authorised CASPs. Note the authorisation number, the home-state NCA, and the date of authorisation. Many fraudulent platforms claim regulatory status they do not hold — verify independently rather than accepting a firm's claims about its own licensing. If the firm is not in any EU register, it is unregulated for EU purposes and the MiCA routes described in this guide do not apply.
  2. Preserve all evidence immediately. Secure screenshots of your account dashboard, transaction history, deposit and withdrawal records, communications with the platform (emails, live chat transcripts, WhatsApp messages), any marketing materials, and your own bank or exchange records showing the outgoing funds. Export your blockchain transaction IDs (TXIDs) from any on-chain transactions — these are permanent records on the public ledger and are the starting point for any on-chain trace.
  3. Report to Action Fraud. Regardless of whether the platform is EU-regulated or not, report the matter to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. You will receive a crime reference number. This is a necessary step that may be required by a solicitor or NCA when you submit further complaints.
  4. Submit a formal complaint to the CASP's complaint function. If the CASP is MiCA-licensed, it is required to have a formal complaint procedure. Submit a written complaint specifying the transactions, the nature of your loss, and the regulatory obligations you believe were breached. This creates a paper trail and — if the CASP fails to respond adequately — satisfies the pre-complaint NCA requirement in most EU member states.
  5. Commission an on-chain intelligence report. An on-chain trace of the transaction from your wallet or the exchange deposit address provides the factual evidence base for both an NCA complaint and any solicitor-led action. CountWize provides an initial case review free of charge. If the tracing prospects are limited — because of mixing, privacy coins, or an untraceable jurisdiction — we will advise you of this before any paid engagement.
  6. Submit a supervisory complaint to the CASP's home-state NCA. Using the process described in the NCA complaints section above: identify the correct NCA from the ESMA register, locate the NCA's supervisory complaint portal, compile your evidence pack (including any on-chain report and the CASP's complaint response), and submit the formal complaint. Keep copies of everything.
  7. Consult a solicitor about civil legal routes. This step runs in parallel with the NCA complaint and is relevant where the financial loss justifies the cost of civil proceedings. A solicitor with EU cross-border financial dispute experience can advise on: governing law and jurisdiction; whether a Norwich Pharmacal Order application is viable; whether equivalent EU disclosure orders are available; and the overall viability of a civil claim given the facts of your case. Limitations: what MiCA cannot do for UK victims

This guide would be incomplete without an honest assessment of the limitations that UK victims face when pursuing MiCA-related routes. Understanding these limitations is not pessimistic — it is necessary for setting realistic expectations and making informed decisions about where to focus effort and resources.

MiCA does not create a compensation fund for victims

Unlike the UK's Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS), which provides up to £85,000 in compensation when an FCA-regulated firm fails, MiCA does not create an equivalent EU crypto victim compensation fund. If a MiCA-licensed CASP fails or is found to have acted fraudulently, there is no automatic compensation mechanism for affected clients. The MiCA capital adequacy requirements and client asset segregation rules reduce the risk of total loss, but they do not guarantee it. Any compensation must come from civil proceedings, the firm's assets in administration or bankruptcy, or — in the case of fraud — from law enforcement asset recovery processes.

NCA investigations are supervisory, not compensatory

A successful supervisory complaint to an NCA may result in the firm being investigated, sanctioned, fined, or having its authorisation suspended or revoked. It does not result in the NCA awarding you compensation. The NCA's role is regulatory — protecting the integrity of the financial system and the public interest — not adjudicating individual disputes. If you want compensation, you need civil legal proceedings.

Cross-border enforcement of judgments post-Brexit

If you obtain a UK court judgment against an EU-based CASP, enforcing that judgment in the EU is now more complex than it was before Brexit. The Brussels I Recast Regulation — which provided a straightforward mutual recognition framework for EU court judgments — no longer applies to the UK. Enforcement of a UK judgment in an EU member state now depends on bilateral treaties, domestic enforcement rules in the relevant member state, and the practical willingness of the defendant to comply. This is a genuine barrier that solicitors specialising in cross-border enforcement will be able to advise on specifically.

Unregulated firms claiming MiCA status

A significant proportion of fraudulent crypto platforms have adopted the language of regulation — claiming to be "MiCA-compliant", "EU-registered", or "licensed in Europe" — without genuine authorisation. This is a deliberate tactic to create false confidence. The practical consequence is that many victims who believe they are dealing with a regulated entity are in fact dealing with an unregistered fraud platform, and the MiCA routes described in this guide do not apply. Always verify against the ESMA register. A regulated firm's authorisation can be confirmed in minutes through the official register — do not rely on a firm's own marketing materials, website disclaimers, or licence number claims without independent verification.

Privacy coins and mixing services

On-chain tracing has meaningful limitations when funds pass through privacy-enhancing technologies — privacy coins such as Monero (XMR), coin mixers or tumblers, or cross-chain bridges designed to break attribution. Where a fraudster has routed funds through these techniques, the on-chain trace may reach a technical dead end before identifying a regulated destination. In these cases, CountWize will advise you of the limitation before any paid engagement, and the NCA complaint and civil litigation routes may be significantly harder to pursue without clear attribution evidence.

Frequently asked questions

Does MiCA apply to UK residents who lose money to an EU crypto exchange?

MiCA is EU law and UK residents are not EU consumers. MiCA does not give UK residents direct rights equivalent to EU citizens. However, if a MiCA-licensed CASP processed your funds, MiCA creates a supervisory framework you can use — particularly by submitting a formal complaint to the CASP's home-state National Competent Authority. Whether a private-law claim is viable depends on the governing law in the CASP's terms, jurisdiction clauses, and the specific facts of your case — this requires legal advice from a solicitor.

Which EU regulator should I contact about a MiCA-licensed firm?

The relevant regulator is the NCA in the EU member state where the CASP is authorised — its home-state supervisor. Confirm this from the ESMA register at esma.europa.eu. Key NCAs include: BaFin (Germany) at bafin.de; AMF (France) at amf-france.org; CNMV (Spain) at cnmv.es; AFM (Netherlands) at afm.nl; CySEC (Cyprus) at cysec.gov.cy. Each NCA has a supervisory complaint mechanism for regulated firms. Note that NCA complaints are supervisory — they can result in investigation and sanction of the firm, but do not award individual compensation.

Can on-chain tracing evidence help with a MiCA-related complaint?

Yes. On-chain tracing documentation — transaction IDs, wallet attribution, exchange identification, fund-flow narratives — strengthens an NCA supervisory complaint by providing verifiable, objective evidence of what happened to funds. It also supports a solicitor's preparation of civil proceedings and, specifically, enables a Norwich Pharmacal Order application where the trace identifies a regulated exchange holding KYC records. CountWize produces on-chain intelligence reports; we do not provide legal advice or guarantee any outcome.

What is MiCA's Travel Rule and why does it matter for evidence?

MiCA requires EU-regulated CASPs to collect and retain verified identity information for parties to qualifying crypto-asset transfers — the Travel Rule. This means that if your funds can be traced to a deposit address at a MiCA-licensed exchange, that exchange should hold KYC records identifying the account holder. This data can be obtained through legal process (such as a Norwich Pharmacal Order) by a solicitor. The availability of this data — by contrast with unregulated offshore platforms that hold no such records — is a significant practical difference that MiCA licensing makes for fraud victims.

Does post-Brexit UK law give me rights against a MiCA-licensed EU firm?

Following Brexit, UK residents lost automatic access to EU consumer protection regimes. MiCA does not extend direct rights to non-EU residents. However, practical routes remain: some MiCA CASPs specify English governing law in their terms; UK solicitors can apply for Norwich Pharmacal Orders against EU exchanges with UK customer bases; and NCA complaints are available as a supervisory route regardless of your nationality. Cross-border enforcement of UK judgments in the EU is more complex post-Brexit and requires specialist legal advice.

What if the platform was not actually MiCA-licensed despite claiming to be?

This is a common scenario. Many fraudulent platforms claim EU or MiCA licensing without genuine authorisation. If the ESMA register does not confirm a firm's authorisation, the MiCA supervisory routes described in this guide do not apply — there is no NCA with jurisdiction. The platform is unregulated and the accountability routes are more limited: Action Fraud reporting, on-chain tracing to identify any regulated destination for funds, civil proceedings if any legal entity is identifiable, and law enforcement intelligence reporting. Always verify independently against official registers before assuming a firm is genuinely licensed.

Who we cannot help

  • If you have already paid a fraudulent "recovery service" — this does not automatically exclude you from a case review, but it complicates the evidence trail and may indicate an active secondary fraud. See the FCA warning on recovery scams at fca.org.uk/scamsmart.
  • If the transaction is more than 5 years old — on-chain traceability does not expire, but the practical prospects of any further action are substantially reduced after this period.
  • If the counterparty is an unidentified peer-to-peer wallet not attributable to any exchange — without exchange attribution, civil legal routes are limited.
  • We cannot guarantee that tracing results will lead to any outcome — we provide intelligence, not guarantees.
  • We do not act as a solicitor, authorised claims management company (CMC), or financial adviser. If you need legal representation for a Norwich Pharmacal Order, NCA complaint guidance, or cross-border civil proceedings, you will need separate legal advice from a qualified solicitor.
