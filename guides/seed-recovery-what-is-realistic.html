What a seed phrase is and why losing it matters

A seed phrase — also called a recovery phrase, mnemonic phrase, or Secret Recovery Phrase depending on the wallet application — is a sequence of 12 or 24 common English words generated by a cryptocurrency wallet when it is first created. These words are not arbitrary: they are a human-readable encoding of a master private key, derived using a standard called BIP39 (Bitcoin Improvement Proposal 39). From that master key, the wallet derives every individual private key it ever needs to control funds across any supported blockchain.

The critical consequence of this design is that the seed phrase is not merely a password. It is the cryptographic root of the entire wallet. Whoever has the seed phrase has the wallet — and whoever loses the seed phrase has, in most circumstances, permanently lost the ability to control the assets held in it. This is by design: decentralised cryptocurrency is designed to give individuals sovereign control over their funds, without relying on a bank or other intermediary. The downside of that sovereignty is that there is no forgotten password reset, no customer support team with administrative access, and no central authority that can restore your access.

Why seed phrases are commonly lost

Seed phrases are lost in many ways. Physical copies — handwritten on paper at the time of wallet creation — are destroyed in house fires, floods, or simply misplaced after a house move. Digital copies stored in email drafts, screenshots, or note-taking apps are deleted or locked behind an inaccessible account. Some users never write down their seed phrase at all, relying on the wallet application remaining installed on the same device indefinitely, until a phone is lost or replaced and the wallet with it. Hardware wallets — physical devices such as a Trezor or Ledger — protect the private key inside the device, but if the device is lost or damaged and the seed phrase was never recorded separately, the keys are gone.

The distinction between a lost password and a lost seed phrase

Many people conflate two separate access problems. A wallet password (sometimes called a spending password, vault password, or device PIN) is an additional encryption layer applied by the wallet application on top of the underlying private key. Losing a wallet password does not necessarily mean losing the wallet — if you have the seed phrase, you can restore the wallet in any compatible application and set a new password. A lost seed phrase, by contrast, is a fundamentally different problem: without it, the private key cannot be derived, and the wallet cannot be restored. These two scenarios lead to very different honest assessments of what is possible.

What on-chain tracing can achieve

On-chain tracing is a forensic discipline, not a key-recovery mechanism. It operates on the publicly readable blockchain ledger — the permanent, distributed record of every transaction ever made on a given network. There are specific situations in which on-chain tracing can produce genuinely useful intelligence, even in cases involving lost wallet access.

Wallet identification and address attribution

If you have any record of your wallet address — even a partial one — on-chain tracing can confirm the full address, its current balance, and its complete transaction history. Even without the address, if you have records of transactions you sent to the wallet (from an exchange, for example), the destination address can often be identified from exchange records or on-chain data. CountWize can produce a full address attribution report documenting every transaction associated with the wallet, the amounts, the timestamps, and where counterpart funds originated from or moved to.

Transaction mapping after a theft

If the access problem stems from a theft — someone obtained your seed phrase, private key, or wallet credentials and emptied the wallet — on-chain tracing can follow the movement of those funds from the point of theft through subsequent transactions. Stolen cryptocurrency is rarely held in the thief's initial destination wallet for long: it is typically moved through a series of intermediate addresses before being deposited at an exchange or converted. Tracing documents every hop in that chain.

Exchange identification and reporting support

When traced funds reach a deposit address at a regulated, KYC-compliant cryptocurrency exchange — particularly one operating in the UK, EU, or another jurisdiction with mutual legal assistance arrangements — there is a legal mechanism available to pursue identification of the account holder. CountWize produces a written intelligence report documenting the exchange attribution, which you or your solicitor can use to support a Norwich Pharmacal Order application: a UK court order compelling the exchange to disclose the account holder's details. This route is relevant where funds were stolen; it is not relevant where the access problem is simply a lost seed phrase with no third-party theft involved.

Evidential reporting for law enforcement

Any on-chain tracing report produced by CountWize is formatted for submission alongside an Action Fraud crime reference number. Law enforcement agencies, including the National Cyber Crime Unit (NCCU), use blockchain intelligence from third-party tracing firms as part of broader fraud investigations, particularly in cases involving multiple victims of the same operation. Even if a direct civil action is not immediately viable in your case, a well-documented trace preserves the evidential chain for any future investigation.

What on-chain tracing cannot achieve

Honest advice about limitations is as important as any description of capabilities. There are things that on-chain tracing fundamentally cannot do, and any firm that claims otherwise is not being truthful with you.

Brute-forcing a seed phrase

A standard BIP39 seed phrase drawn from a 2,048-word wordlist produces a key space of approximately 2128 possible combinations for a 12-word phrase, or 2256 for a 24-word phrase. To put the 12-word figure in context: the estimated number of atoms in the observable universe is approximately 1080, which is far smaller than 2128 (approximately 3.4 × 1038). No hardware that exists or is foreseeable can exhaust this search space in any meaningful timeframe. Any firm claiming it can "recover" a lost seed phrase through brute-force computation is misrepresenting what is technically possible. CountWize does not make this claim.

Reversing a blockchain transaction

Blockchain transactions are irreversible by design. Once a transaction is confirmed and included in the chain, there is no mechanism — no administrator, no protocol function, no court order — that can undo it. This is not a limitation of current technology; it is a fundamental property of how public blockchains achieve trustlessness. On-chain tracing can document where funds went, but it cannot reverse the direction of travel.

Accessing a self-custodied wallet where the seed is completely lost

If a wallet is self-custodied — meaning the private key is held by the user, not by an exchange or custodial service — and the seed phrase is completely lost with no partial record and no hardware device still retaining the keys, there is no path to access. No on-chain tracing service, no forensic firm, and no technology that currently exists can reconstruct a lost private key from blockchain data alone. The blockchain records what addresses hold funds; it does not record the private keys that control those addresses. CountWize will tell you this clearly at the outset of a case review and will not proceed with a paid engagement in a scenario where tracing cannot produce any useful intelligence.

Guaranteeing any outcome

CountWize does not guarantee that any on-chain tracing engagement will lead to the return of funds, the identification of any responsible party, or any specific legal outcome. Whether tracing leads to anything actionable depends on where the funds went, the jurisdiction of any exchange involved, law enforcement priorities, and many other factors outside CountWize's control. We provide a written intelligence report; what is done with that report is a matter for you, your solicitor if you have one, and law enforcement.

Specific wallet contexts: Trezor, Ledger, and MetaMask

The practical implications of lost access differ depending on the type of wallet involved. The three wallet types most commonly mentioned in UK enquiries about wallet access loss are hardware wallets (principally Trezor and Ledger) and the MetaMask browser extension wallet. Each has its own access model, and each presents a different honest assessment.

Trezor hardware wallets

Trezor devices store the private key inside the hardware security element of the device itself. Access to the device requires a PIN. After a set number of incorrect PIN attempts, the device performs a factory reset and wipes the stored keys — this is a deliberate security feature to prevent physical attacks. If you still have the Trezor device, the correct PIN, and the stored keys have not been wiped, the wallet is accessible. If you have lost the PIN but have the 12 or 24-word seed phrase, you can restore the wallet to a new Trezor device or any BIP39-compatible application. If the device has been wiped and the seed phrase is lost, the keys are gone. CountWize cannot bypass the hardware security of a Trezor device, and no legitimate firm can do so either. What CountWize can do is conduct an on-chain trace of the wallet's addresses — for example, if the wallet was accessed and drained by an unauthorised party before you discovered the loss.

Ledger hardware wallets

Ledger devices operate on a similar principle to Trezor: the private key is held within a secure element chip and a PIN is required for device access. The 24-word recovery phrase (Ledger uses 24 words by default) is the master backup. Ledger's "Recover" subscription service — launched in 2023 — offers an optional encrypted backup of the seed phrase split across three custodians, but this service must be actively enrolled; it is not retroactive and does not apply to wallets where the seed was already lost before enrolment. If you have the 24-word phrase, you can restore your Ledger wallet. If both the device and the phrase are lost, the funds are inaccessible. As with Trezor, CountWize can trace on-chain activity associated with your Ledger wallet addresses, but cannot reconstruct the private key.

MetaMask browser wallet

MetaMask is a software wallet that stores an encrypted copy of your private key locally in your browser or mobile device. Unlocking MetaMask requires a password you set when you created the wallet — this is distinct from the 12-word Secret Recovery Phrase (MetaMask's term for the seed phrase). If you have forgotten your MetaMask password, you can reset it by using the Secret Recovery Phrase via the "Forgot Password" function within the MetaMask application. If you have lost both the password and the Secret Recovery Phrase, the private key cannot be decrypted or reconstructed. MetaMask does not hold a copy of your keys — they are stored only on your device. If your device is lost or damaged, without the Secret Recovery Phrase the wallet cannot be restored. CountWize can trace on-chain activity from MetaMask addresses, including any unauthorised movements of funds if the wallet was compromised.

Note on exchange-held wallets: If your cryptocurrency is held on an exchange such as Coinbase, Binance, or Kraken — rather than in a self-custodied wallet — account access loss is a matter for that exchange's customer support, not on-chain tracing. Exchanges hold the private keys for custodial accounts and have their own identity-verification processes for account recovery. Contact the exchange directly. CountWize's services are not relevant to custodial exchange account access issues.

  2. On-chain trace. If the assessment indicates that tracing can produce useful intelligence — for example, because funds were moved from the wallet by an unauthorised party, or because traced funds have reached an exchange with KYC records — CountWize conducts a full on-chain trace.   3. Intelligence report. CountWize produces a written intelligence report documenting the findings of the trace. The report is formatted for submission to Action Fraud alongside your crime reference number, for use as supporting evidence in civil proceedings, or as part of an application to a UK court. The report states clearly what the trace found, what it did not find, and what the limitations of the analysis are. CountWize does not overstate findings or make claims the blockchain data does not support.

Throughout this process, CountWize does not ask for your seed phrase, private keys, wallet passwords, or remote access to any device. All on-chain analysis works from publicly available blockchain data and any wallet addresses you choose to share with us.

What to do in the first 48 hours

The immediate steps you take after discovering a wallet access problem or a theft can significantly affect what evidence is available for any subsequent investigation. The following list covers the most time-sensitive actions.

  • Do not transfer any remaining funds to the lost wallet. If you discover a wallet has been partially drained, do not send any additional funds to the same wallet addresses — they may remain accessible to the thief.
  • Document everything you can recall. Write down the wallet address or addresses (even partial ones), any transaction IDs you have, any exchange records showing deposits or withdrawals, and a timeline of when you last had access. This information is critical for any trace.
  • Check whether any exchange holds a record. If you ever deposited from the wallet to a centralised exchange, your exchange account history may include the sending wallet address. This is often the fastest way to confirm the wallet address if you did not record it separately.
  • Report to Action Fraud if theft is involved. If you believe your wallet was accessed without your authorisation — whether through phishing, malware, or physical access to your device — report to Action Fraud on 0300 123 2040 or at actionfraud.police.uk. You will receive a crime reference number (CRN) which is essential for any subsequent investigation or legal process.
  • Preserve all digital evidence. Do not delete any emails, messages, or notifications related to the wallet. Do not factory reset any device that may hold wallet data. In some hardware wallet scenarios, a specialist data recovery firm may be able to retrieve an encrypted key from a damaged device — but only if the physical media is intact and untampered.
  • Be cautious of unsolicited contact. If you post about your situation on social media or crypto forums, you are likely to receive unsolicited messages from accounts claiming to be able to recover your wallet. The overwhelming majority of these are scams. See our companion guide: Crypto recovery scams: how to spot a fake firm.
  • Request a free case review from CountWize. There is no obligation and no upfront fee. CountWize will review the information you have and give you an honest assessment of whether on-chain tracing is relevant to your situation before any further step is taken.

Frequently asked questions

Can I recover a crypto wallet if I have lost my seed phrase entirely?

If a seed phrase is completely and irretrievably lost with no partial record — no written backup, no digital copy in any form — and there is no hardware device still holding the keys in memory, then the wallet's private keys cannot be reconstructed. On-chain tracing can confirm which addresses belong to the wallet and document the balance, but without the private key the funds cannot be moved or accessed. There is no legitimate service that can brute-force a standard 12 or 24-word BIP39 seed phrase — the key space is astronomically large. CountWize will tell you this honestly at the outset of any case review.

What is the difference between losing a wallet password and losing a seed phrase?

These are two distinct problems with different prospects. A wallet password (also called a PIN or spending password) is an additional layer of encryption on top of the private key. In many wallet implementations, including hardware wallets like Trezor and Ledger, losing the password does not mean losing the wallet if you still have the seed phrase. The seed phrase itself is the master key: with it, you can restore the wallet in any compatible application. Losing the password but keeping the seed phrase is recoverable in most cases. Losing the seed phrase entirely — regardless of whether you remember the password — means the private key cannot be derived.

What can on-chain tracing achieve in a lost wallet situation?

On-chain tracing can identify the wallet addresses associated with your funds, confirm the current on-chain balance, map all historical transactions in and out of those addresses, and — if funds have been moved by a third party (for example, in a theft scenario) — trace where those funds went. If stolen funds reach a regulated exchange with KYC records, the trace can support a legal application to compel the exchange to identify the account holder. On-chain tracing cannot reconstruct a lost seed phrase, cannot reverse a blockchain transaction, and cannot access a self-custodied wallet where the seed is lost.

I have a Trezor hardware wallet but forgot the PIN — what can CountWize do?

Trezor hardware wallets wipe themselves after a set number of incorrect PIN attempts — this is a security feature, not a bug. If you have your 12 or 24-word seed phrase, you can restore your wallet to a new Trezor or any BIP39-compatible wallet application. If you do not have the seed phrase and cannot recall the PIN, the funds in that wallet are inaccessible. CountWize cannot bypass the hardware security of a Trezor device and will tell you this clearly. What we can do is document the on-chain history of the wallet addresses if that information is needed for any related legal or investigative purpose.

I lost access to my MetaMask — can CountWize help?

MetaMask stores an encrypted copy of your private key locally. If you have the MetaMask password, you can unlock the wallet. If you have lost the password but have the 12-word Secret Recovery Phrase, you can restore the wallet via the MetaMask import function. If you have lost both the password and the Secret Recovery Phrase, the private key cannot be recovered through any means — CountWize included. On-chain tracing can document what happened to the funds in your MetaMask addresses if they were drained by a third party, and can support a law enforcement report or exchange disclosure request if the funds were moved to a traceable destination.

Is there any legitimate service that can crack a Bitcoin wallet password?

For encrypted wallet files (such as a Bitcoin Core wallet.dat) where you have a partial recollection of the password, specialist cryptographic firms — not CountWize — can attempt dictionary or rule-based password recovery against the encrypted file. This is a separate discipline from on-chain tracing, requires the actual encrypted file, and has realistic success only where the password is a recognisable word, phrase, or number pattern the owner can partly remember. CountWize does not offer this service and does not represent that it can guarantee any outcome from it.
