What CP25/41 proposes — a plain-English summary

In 2025, the Financial Conduct Authority published Consultation Paper CP25/41 — a substantial regulatory document setting out a proposed framework for supervising cryptoasset businesses in the United Kingdom. The publication represents the most significant regulatory development in UK crypto since the government announced its intention to bring cryptoassets into the regulatory perimeter. This page provides a plain-English summary of the main proposals and their practical implications, with a particular focus on what they mean for individuals who have suffered cryptocurrency fraud.

The overall intent: bringing crypto into the regulatory mainstream

The FCA's stated objective in CP25/41 is to create a regime that promotes consumer protection, market integrity, and financial stability, while allowing the UK to remain competitive in the global cryptoasset market. The proposed framework takes as its model the existing regulated financial services regime, applying analogous obligations — licensing, conduct rules, prudential requirements, and market-abuse prohibitions — to cryptoasset businesses that are currently operating under a much lighter-touch registration-only regime.

At present, most cryptoasset firms operating in the UK are required only to register with the FCA under the Money Laundering Regulations for anti-money laundering (AML) purposes. CP25/41 proposes moving beyond that registration model to a full authorisation regime, under which firms would need to demonstrate ongoing fitness, competence, and compliance with conduct-of-business rules to obtain and retain an FCA licence.

Who CP25/41 covers

The proposals primarily target three categories of regulated entity:

  • Cryptoasset exchanges and trading platforms — businesses that facilitate the buying, selling, or exchange of cryptoassets, whether operating order books, quote-driven markets, or peer-to-peer matching services.
  • Cryptoasset custodians — businesses that hold cryptoassets on behalf of clients, including both hardware and software wallet service providers who are in control of client private keys.
  • Cryptoasset intermediaries — brokers, investment managers, and arrangers who deal in cryptoassets on behalf of clients or provide cryptoasset-related investment advice.

Importantly, CP25/41 focuses on these centralised, identifiable entities. It does not propose comprehensive regulation of decentralised protocols or purely peer-to-peer arrangements where there is no identifiable legal entity in control. The FCA has acknowledged the challenge this creates and has indicated it intends to consult separately on the treatment of decentralised finance (DeFi). This distinction matters significantly for fraud victims, as discussed in Section 2 below.

Key conduct-of-business obligations proposed

The proposed conduct rules represent a significant step up from current practice. Among the most substantive obligations proposed are:

Proposed obligation What it means in practice
Consumer duty compliance Regulated cryptoasset firms would need to demonstrate that their products and services deliver good outcomes for retail customers — mirroring the Consumer Duty obligations already applicable to mainstream financial services firms. This would include fair pricing, clear and not misleading marketing, and adequate customer support.
Robust AML and fraud-prevention frameworks Firms would face enhanced due-diligence requirements and must implement transaction-monitoring systems capable of identifying and reporting suspicious activity to the National Crime Agency (NCA). Exchanges would be required to apply the Travel Rule to on-chain transfers, meaning they must collect and transmit sender and recipient information above a threshold.
Prudential requirements and segregation of assets Cryptoasset custodians would need to hold sufficient capital to cover operational and credit risks, and would be required to segregate client assets from firm assets. This addresses the risk of losses to customers in the event of firm insolvency — a risk crystallised catastrophically in the FTX collapse in 2022.
Market-abuse prohibitions The FCA proposes extending market-abuse rules to cryptoassets — prohibiting insider dealing, market manipulation, and the misuse of inside information in cryptoasset markets. Regulated platforms would be required to implement market-surveillance systems.
Admission and disclosure standards Exchanges listing cryptoassets for trading would need to conduct due diligence on the assets being listed and publish disclosure documents (analogous to a prospectus) enabling consumers to make informed investment decisions.
Complaints handling and access to redress Regulated firms would need to implement compliant complaints-handling procedures. The FCA is also consulting on whether access to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) should be extended to cover cryptoasset-related complaints — though this has not yet been confirmed.

What CP25/41 does not propose

It is as important to understand what CP25/41 does not cover as what it does. The consultation does not propose:

  • A direct asset-recovery mechanism for fraud victims
  • Any obligation on exchanges to freeze or return assets simply because a consumer reports a fraud
  • An extension of the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) to cryptoasset losses
  • Regulation of pure peer-to-peer wallets or non-custodial arrangements
  • Any licensing regime for on-chain tracing and blockchain analysis services (discussed further in Section 3)
Where to read the original consultation: CP25/41 is published in full on the FCA's website at fca.org.uk/publications/consultation-papers. CountWize strongly encourages affected businesses and consumers to read the primary source. This page is an educational summary, not a substitute for the consultation document itself or for legal advice on its implications.

How the proposed rules affect people who have lost crypto to scams

For individuals who have already lost cryptocurrency to fraud — whether through investment scams, romance fraud, pig-butchering operations, or other forms of manipulation — CP25/41 represents a significant, if gradual, improvement in the systemic environment. The proposals do not provide any immediate remedy, but their structural effects are likely to be meaningful over time.

Better due diligence at the point of fraud

A substantial proportion of crypto fraud proceeds pass through or terminate at centralised exchanges. Under the current AML-registration regime, exchanges have obligations around customer due diligence and suspicious transaction reporting, but these obligations are not always robustly enforced and the level of implementation varies significantly across firms. CP25/41's proposed transition to a full authorisation regime — with ongoing supervisory oversight and the threat of licence withdrawal — creates stronger compliance incentives. Over time, this is likely to make it harder for fraudsters to cash out at regulated exchanges without triggering detection, which has direct relevance to the traceability and potential interceptibility of fraud proceeds.

The Travel Rule and traceability of funds

The proposed mandatory implementation of the Travel Rule is particularly significant for fraud victims. The Travel Rule requires regulated firms to collect and transmit identifying information about the originators and beneficiaries of cryptoasset transfers. When applied consistently, this creates a much richer data trail than currently exists — which substantially improves the intelligence value of on-chain tracing analysis. A tracing report that can link a blockchain address to a specific regulated entity holding Travel Rule data is far more useful in legal proceedings than one that can only trace to an anonymous wallet.

Current position: The Travel Rule has been legally required of UK cryptoasset firms since September 2023 under the Money Laundering Regulations. However, implementation has been inconsistent across the industry. CP25/41's proposed authorisation regime would create stronger enforcement mechanisms to ensure consistent compliance — improving the quality and completeness of the Travel Rule data trail that tracing analysis relies on.

Complaints handling and potential FOS access

If the FCA proceeds with extending Financial Ombudsman Service access to cryptoasset complaints, this would represent a potentially significant avenue for some fraud victims — particularly those whose losses arose partly from failures by a regulated exchange (for example, inadequate fraud warnings, failure to identify suspicious transaction patterns, or deficient Know Your Customer processes that allowed a fraudulent account to operate). The FOS provides a free, binding dispute-resolution service for eligible consumers, with awards of up to £415,000 available in appropriate cases. Whether cryptoasset fraud claims would be eligible would depend on the specific facts and the scope of any extended jurisdiction — this is still under consultation and has not been confirmed.

Where CP25/41 does not help — and why this matters

It is important to be direct about the limitations of CP25/41 from a fraud victim's perspective. The proposals do not create a right to asset recovery. They do not compel exchanges to freeze funds on a victim's request. They do not create a compensation fund for cryptoasset fraud losses. For the large category of fraud where proceeds have been moved through decentralised protocols, privacy-enhancing tools such as mixers, or overseas exchanges outside the UK regulatory perimeter, CP25/41's proposed rules have limited practical reach.

Even where funds reach a UK-regulated exchange, accessing them requires a court order — typically a freezing injunction and/or a Norwich Pharmacal Order directing the exchange to disclose account-holder information — obtained through a solicitor. CP25/41 does not change this fundamental process, though the compliance environment it creates may make exchanges more responsive to properly obtained court orders.

Important: Do not contact exchanges directly demanding that they freeze funds or disclose account information — exchanges are not obliged to act on unilateral victim requests, and doing so can alert a fraudster to take evasive action. Any communication with an exchange about a fraud should be coordinated through a solicitor following the proper legal process. See our guide: When to instruct a crypto solicitor.

The systemic benefit: a higher bar for market participants

Perhaps the most important medium-term benefit of CP25/41 for fraud victims is the systemic one: a more robustly regulated cryptoasset ecosystem is one in which it is harder for fraudsters to operate undetected, harder to cash out proceeds anonymously, and harder to use the credibility of a regulated exchange as cover for fraudulent activity. This does not eliminate fraud risk, but it narrows the operational space available to fraudsters — which over time should reduce the volume and scale of cryptoasset fraud in the UK market.

On-chain tracing under the proposed regulatory framework

A natural question for any firm offering blockchain analysis services is whether the activities it performs would become regulated under CP25/41. CountWize's view, based on the proposals as published, is that on-chain tracing and blockchain forensic analysis are not regulated activities under the proposed framework — and that CP25/41 does not materially change the position.

What on-chain tracing involves

On-chain tracing is the analytical process of tracking the movement of cryptoassets across a blockchain, using the publicly available transaction data recorded on that blockchain. A tracing analyst maps which wallet addresses sent and received funds, identifies clustering patterns that suggest multiple addresses are controlled by the same entity, cross-references wallet addresses against known exchange deposit addresses and other public intelligence databases, and produces a report documenting the movement of funds from point of origin to point of termination.

This is a forensic information service. The output is a structured analysis of publicly available data — analogous in regulatory terms to a due diligence report or an expert witness report. CountWize does not hold, transfer, manage, or advise on cryptoassets. It does not operate an exchange, provide custody, or arrange cryptoasset transactions. It produces reports. This distinction is central to the regulatory analysis.

Why tracing is not covered by CP25/41's proposed regime

CP25/41 proposes to regulate activities that involve the holding, transfer, exchange, or management of cryptoassets on behalf of clients, or the provision of advice in relation to such activities. The proposed regulated activities are modelled on the existing FSMA regulatory perimeter and on the MiCA framework adopted in the European Union.

Blockchain analysis involves none of these activities. It involves the analysis of data that is already publicly available. The analyst does not hold the client's assets, does not transact in cryptoassets, and does not provide investment advice. The regulatory position of blockchain analysis firms has been considered in comparable jurisdictions — including the US, where the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) has confirmed that blockchain analysis providers are not money services businesses — and the consistent conclusion is that analytical services of this kind fall outside the regulatory perimeter.

CountWize maintains that on-chain tracing is not a regulated financial service and does not require FCA authorisation under CP25/41 or any other current regulatory framework. We note, however, that this position should be kept under review as the regulatory framework evolves, and that firms and consumers should seek their own legal advice if they need a definitive regulatory opinion.

How tracing fits into the regulated ecosystem CP25/41 creates

While on-chain tracing is itself unregulated, it operates within and in support of the regulated ecosystem. In the post-CP25/41 world, assuming the proposals are implemented, the role of tracing analysis is likely to become more clearly defined in several ways:

  • Improved data quality from regulated exchanges: As noted above, consistent Travel Rule compliance creates a richer data environment for tracing analysis. A tracing report produced in a well-regulated ecosystem is more evidentially useful than one produced from a patchwork of inconsistently compliant data sources.
  • A clearer pathway from tracing to legal process: When a tracing report identifies that fraud proceeds have been received by a UK-regulated exchange, the compliance obligations of that exchange — including its obligations to cooperate with law enforcement and to respond to properly served court orders — provide a clear procedural pathway for solicitors to pursue. CP25/41 reinforces this pathway by raising the minimum compliance bar across regulated entities.
  • Tracing as input to regulatory complaints: If the FOS or another dispute-resolution mechanism is extended to cryptoasset complaints, a tracing report documenting the movement of funds may become relevant evidence in establishing the factual basis of a complaint. CountWize's reports are structured to be usable in legal and regulatory proceedings.
  • Tracing as part of a firm's own AML toolkit: Regulated exchanges and custodians under CP25/41 would face enhanced AML obligations. Many are likely to engage blockchain analytics services — including both proprietary tools (Chainalysis, Elliptic, TRM Labs) and specialist forensic firms — as part of their compliance infrastructure. This creates a larger and more structured market for on-chain analysis as an input to regulated compliance processes.
CountWize's position: CountWize welcomes the direction of travel in CP25/41. A better-regulated cryptoasset ecosystem benefits everyone — including fraud victims — by improving the traceability of funds, strengthening the compliance obligations of the entities through which fraud proceeds typically flow, and creating clearer accountability when regulated firms fail in their obligations. CountWize is not FCA-authorised and does not provide financial or legal advice, but the work it does supports victims in gathering the intelligence they need to pursue legal remedies through properly authorised channels.

Key dates and next steps

CP25/41 is a consultation paper — meaning it sets out proposals on which the FCA is inviting responses, not rules that are already in force. The process from consultation to final rules typically takes between 12 and 24 months, and implementation timelines are often extended further to allow industry adaptation. The following timeline summarises the expected process based on standard FCA consultation procedure and the complexity of the proposals involved.

2025 Q3–Q4

Consultation period open

CP25/41 was published in 2025 with a consultation period during which firms, industry bodies, consumer groups, and individuals could submit responses to the FCA. Responses are submitted in writing via the FCA's online portal at fca.org.uk/publications/consultation-papers.

2026

FCA analysis of responses and Policy Statement

After the consultation period closes, the FCA analyses all responses received and publishes a Policy Statement (PS) setting out the final rules, with an explanation of how the FCA has taken respondent feedback into account. For a consultation of CP25/41's scope, the Policy Statement process typically takes 9–18 months from the close of consultation.

2026–2027

Final rules published; application window opens

Once final rules are published, firms currently operating under the MLR registration regime would need to apply for full FCA authorisation within a defined transition window. The FCA is expected to set a phased implementation timetable to avoid overwhelming its authorisation function. Firms unable to achieve authorisation within the window would be required to cease operating regulated activities.

2027 onwards

Full regime in force (indicative)

The fully implemented regime — with all proposed conduct obligations, prudential requirements, and market-abuse prohibitions in force — is unlikely to be operational before 2027 at the earliest for most provisions. Some requirements may have longer transitional arrangements. These are indicative dates based on standard FCA timescales and should be treated as estimates, not confirmed deadlines.

What this means if you have lost crypto to fraud now

The proposed CP25/41 regime will not help you today. The rules are not yet in force. Even when they come into force, they will not create retrospective rights or remedies. If you have lost cryptocurrency to fraud, the relevant steps are immediate and do not depend on CP25/41:

  1. Report to Action Fraud immediately. Call 0300 123 2040 or report online at actionfraud.police.uk. You will receive a crime reference number, which is required for all subsequent steps.
  2. Report to your bank's fraud team. If you transferred funds to the fraudster by bank transfer, contact your bank immediately. The PSR mandatory reimbursement scheme may apply. See our guide on APP fraud reimbursement and crypto.
  3. Preserve all evidence. Screenshots, transaction records, communications, wallet addresses. Do not delete anything. This evidence is the foundation of any subsequent action.
  4. Consider an on-chain tracing assessment. CountWize can assess in a free 15-minute consultation whether on-chain tracing would add intelligence value to your situation — whether that is for a police report, a solicitor's civil action, or a regulatory complaint.
  5. Consult a solicitor if the amount is significant. Civil legal action — freezing injunctions, Norwich Pharmacal Orders, proprietary claims — requires a solicitor regulated by the SRA. CountWize works alongside solicitors and can provide tracing reports that solicitors use to support court applications.

FAQ — CP25/41 and what it means for crypto fraud victims

Will regulated exchanges be required to cooperate with on-chain tracing firms under CP25/41?

CP25/41 does not create a direct obligation on regulated exchanges to cooperate with tracing firms. However, the proposed rules would require regulated cryptoasset exchanges to implement robust AML and fraud-prevention frameworks, which may include sharing information with law enforcement and regulated intermediaries in certain circumstances. Formal cooperation with tracing firms still typically requires a court order — such as a Norwich Pharmacal Order — obtained through a solicitor. The proposed regulatory framework does not change that fundamental process.

Does CP25/41 cover decentralised finance (DeFi) protocols?

The FCA has acknowledged in CP25/41 that decentralised finance presents significant regulatory challenges. The primary focus of the consultation is on centralised entities — exchanges, custodians, and intermediaries that can be clearly identified and held accountable. The FCA has indicated it intends to publish further guidance on DeFi separately. For fraud victims whose funds passed through DeFi protocols, the regulatory framework proposed in CP25/41 is unlikely to provide a direct avenue for assistance; on-chain tracing remains the principal tool for understanding the movement of funds through decentralised systems.

Will CP25/41 make it easier for fraud victims to get their crypto back?

CP25/41 is likely to improve the systemic environment over time — regulated exchanges will have clearer obligations around fraud prevention, customer due diligence, and cooperation with authorities. However, the proposed rules do not create a direct restitution mechanism for fraud victims, nor do they oblige exchanges to freeze or return assets simply because a victim reports a fraud. Recovery where it occurs still depends on court orders, exchange cooperation, and the specific facts of each case. On-chain tracing intelligence remains an important first step in any civil legal process.

Is on-chain tracing a regulated activity under CP25/41?

Based on the current proposals in CP25/41, on-chain tracing and blockchain analysis — which involve analysing publicly available blockchain data to map the movement of cryptoassets — are not classified as regulated activities requiring FCA authorisation. On-chain tracing is a forensic information service, not a financial service. CountWize does not require FCA authorisation for the tracing work it performs, and CP25/41 does not appear to change this position. Clients should note that CountWize is not FCA-authorised and does not provide financial advice.

When will the rules proposed in CP25/41 come into force?

CP25/41 is a consultation paper, not final rules. After the consultation period closes, the FCA will analyse responses and publish a Policy Statement setting out final rules. Given the volume of the proposals and standard FCA consultation timelines, implementation of final rules is unlikely before 2027 at the earliest for most provisions. Some requirements — particularly those requiring exchanges to apply for full FCA authorisation — may have transitional periods extending further. Businesses and individuals should not assume the proposed rules are already in force.
