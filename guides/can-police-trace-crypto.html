The November 2025 Met Police £5bn Bitcoin seizure

£5bn
Bitcoin seized by the Metropolitan Police, November 2025. One of the largest cryptocurrency seizures by any law enforcement agency globally — and the clearest recent demonstration of what UK police can achieve with sufficient investigative resources.

In November 2025, the Metropolitan Police announced the seizure of approximately £5 billion worth of Bitcoin as part of a major criminal investigation. The seizure followed a multi-year investigation combining on-chain forensic tracing, exchange cooperation under court orders, and coordination with overseas law enforcement agencies through mutual legal assistance channels. The funds were attributed to a criminal network that had laundered proceeds through a series of intermediary wallets before attempting to consolidate them at regulated exchanges.

The case is significant for two reasons. First, it demonstrates that UK law enforcement has the technical capability, legal powers, and cross-agency coordination to trace and seize very large amounts of cryptocurrency when sufficient resources are applied. Second, and equally important, it illustrates the constraints that apply to most individual fraud cases: the investigation took years, required significant dedicated resource from specialist units, and depended on the scale of the criminal operation making it viable to pursue through international coordination. The same level of resource is not typically available for individual fraud victims reporting losses of thousands or tens of thousands of pounds.

Understanding what the Met Police seizure represents — and what it does not — is important context for anyone who has been the victim of cryptocurrency fraud and is wondering whether police can or will trace their funds.

Sources: Metropolitan Police press releases; Action Fraud UK; FCA ScamSmart.

What UK law enforcement can do: on-chain forensics and legal powers

UK law enforcement has developed substantial capability to trace cryptocurrency transactions. This capability spans technical forensic tools, formal legal powers, and cooperation agreements with both private exchanges and overseas agencies. Understanding each element helps victims set realistic expectations about what reporting to police can achieve.

Blockchain analytics tools

The starting point for any police cryptocurrency trace is the same public blockchain that any member of the public can access: every confirmed Bitcoin or Ethereum transaction is permanently recorded and visible on the respective blockchain ledger. What police have that most individuals do not is access to professional blockchain analytics platforms. The two dominant tools used by UK law enforcement are Chainalysis and Elliptic, both of which aggregate enormous datasets about wallet clusters, exchange attribution, and known criminal infrastructure.

These platforms use a combination of computational clustering algorithms and human intelligence to build attribution models: they can often identify which exchange or service controls a given wallet address, which other addresses belong to the same entity, and whether a wallet has previously been associated with known criminal activity. When law enforcement submits a wallet address to these tools, they can frequently identify which regulated exchange received the funds within hours — a task that would take an individual analyst weeks of manual blockchain research.

Exchange cooperation under court orders

Identifying the exchange that received stolen or fraudulently obtained funds is only the first step. The exchange holds Know Your Customer (KYC) records for the account that received the funds — typically a name, address, date of birth, and identity document. To obtain those records, police need to compel the exchange to disclose them.

For UK-regulated exchanges, the primary legal mechanism is a Production Order under Schedule 5 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 (PACE). A Production Order compels the holder of material relevant to a criminal investigation to produce it to police. Most major UK-regulated or UK-accessible exchanges — including Coinbase, Kraken, and Binance (where it operates under UK registration) — will comply with valid Production Orders within their legal review period.

Police also have powers under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2002 (POCA), including Search and Seizure Orders that can extend to cryptocurrency wallets where a suspect has been identified, and Civil Recovery Orders that can target assets without a criminal conviction where proceeds of unlawful conduct can be shown. These powers have been used increasingly in cryptocurrency fraud cases as prosecutors and law enforcement have developed familiarity with the asset class.

Asset freezing and seizure

Where funds are traced to a wallet that police can access — typically because a suspect has been arrested and devices seized — the funds can be frozen and seized under POCA. The November 2025 Met seizure involved exactly this mechanism: once the funds were attributed to suspects with identifiable on-chain and off-chain connections, police were able to apply for and execute a Search and Seizure Order. Frozen cryptocurrency is typically transferred to a police-controlled wallet pending disposal proceedings.

The National Cyber Crime Unit (NCCU), part of the National Crime Agency (NCA), leads on the most complex cryptocurrency cases. Regional Organised Crime Units (ROCUs) also have cryptocurrency investigation capability. The City of London Police, which operates Action Fraud, has a dedicated fraud investigation command with growing cryptocurrency expertise.

How police trace Bitcoin transactions step by step

Understanding the actual process police follow helps victims understand both the capability and the timelines involved. A typical cryptocurrency fraud investigation — where one is opened — follows a sequence of steps that each take time and depend on prior steps being completed successfully.

  1. Receipt of the Action Fraud report and NFIB triage. When a victim reports to Action Fraud, their report is assessed by the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau. The NFIB's primary function is pattern intelligence: it aggregates reports across thousands of victims to identify criminal networks rather than investigating individual cases. Reports are assigned a priority score based on factors including loss amount, availability of evidence, and whether multiple victims have reported the same network. High-priority reports are referred to the appropriate police force or specialist unit.
  2. Extraction of transaction evidence from the victim's report. Where a referral is made, investigators extract the transaction IDs (TXIDs) and wallet addresses provided by the victim. The quality of the victim's evidence at this stage directly affects how quickly the trace can proceed. Well-documented reports with complete TXIDs and wallet addresses give investigators a clean starting point; incomplete reports require additional work to reconstruct the transaction history.
  3. On-chain tracing using blockchain analytics platforms. Investigators run the TXIDs and wallet addresses through Chainalysis, Elliptic, or equivalent tools. These platforms map the flow of funds across the blockchain, identify wallet clusters that likely belong to the same entity, and attempt to attribute destination wallets to known exchanges or services. This step can produce results within hours for straightforward cases, or may take considerably longer for cases involving multiple hop wallets or mixing services.
  4. Exchange identification and formal production order application. Where a regulated exchange is identified as a destination, investigators prepare a Production Order application. In the UK, this requires demonstrating to a circuit judge that the material sought is relevant to a criminal investigation and that there are reasonable grounds for believing it is held by the exchange. UK exchanges typically have compliance teams that review and respond to production orders within 30 to 90 days.
  5. KYC disclosure and suspect identification. On receipt of the account-holder's KYC records from the exchange, investigators cross-reference the identity against other intelligence sources. Where a UK-based suspect is identified, the investigation may proceed to arrest or civil asset recovery. Where the suspect is overseas, mutual legal assistance proceedings begin — a process that can take months or years.
  6. Asset freeze or arrest proceedings. If the suspect is identified, located, and within reachable jurisdiction, police can apply for an asset freeze or execute an arrest warrant. In cryptocurrency fraud cases, the period between identification and seizure is often the most time-sensitive: funds can be moved further if a suspect becomes aware of police interest. For this reason, freeze orders are often sought before an arrest is made.

Realistic constraints: where police tracing runs into limits

UK law enforcement's cryptocurrency capabilities are genuine and growing. But there are structural constraints that affect the likelihood of any individual case leading to a traced outcome. Being clear about these constraints is important — both to set realistic expectations and to explain why independent on-chain tracing can add value alongside police reporting.

Pseudonymity, not anonymity

A common misconception is that Bitcoin is anonymous. It is not: it is pseudonymous. Every transaction is permanently recorded on the public blockchain, linked to wallet addresses rather than to named individuals. In most fraud cases, pseudonymity is the main protection scammers rely on — their wallets are visible on the blockchain, but the person controlling those wallets is not immediately identifiable. Blockchain analytics tools and exchange KYC disclosure are the primary mechanisms for converting pseudonymity into identity. For most cases where funds reach a regulated, KYC-compliant exchange, the pseudonymity can eventually be broken — the constraint is investigative resource, not technical impossibility.

Cross-border jurisdiction complexity

Many cryptocurrency fraud operations are run from jurisdictions outside the UK — frequently from countries with limited or slow mutual legal assistance arrangements. Even where a UK court can issue a Production Order against an exchange, exchanges operating outside the UK's regulatory reach may not be compelled to comply without a local court order in their own jurisdiction. Obtaining local orders through MLAT processes can take one to three years. In the meantime, fraudsters may have moved funds further or withdrawn them. Cross-border cases are by far the most common outcome in organised cryptocurrency fraud, and they represent the primary practical constraint on police effectiveness.

Mixing, tumbling, and chain-hopping

Sophisticated fraud operations use additional obfuscation techniques to complicate tracing. Cryptocurrency mixers (also called tumblers) pool funds from multiple sources and redistribute them, making it harder to trace a specific victim's funds through the mix. Chain-hopping involves converting funds from one blockchain to another — for example, from Bitcoin to Monero — at a point where the trail would otherwise lead to an exchange. These techniques do not make tracing impossible, but they significantly increase the difficulty and reduce the confidence of attribution. Professional analytics tools have methods for probabilistic attribution even through mixers, but these methods carry lower certainty than straightforward hop-to-exchange traces.

Privacy coins

Privacy-focused cryptocurrencies — most notably Monero (XMR) — are designed from the ground up to obscure transaction details. Unlike Bitcoin, Monero transactions do not expose wallet addresses or amounts on a public ledger: they use ring signatures and stealth addresses to conceal both sender and recipient. If a fraudster converts stolen Bitcoin to Monero before dispersing it further, the on-chain trail effectively ends at the conversion point. UK law enforcement and commercial analytics vendors have some capability to work with Monero-adjacent data (for example, at the exchange conversion points), but Monero itself remains a meaningful constraint on tracing.

Investigative resource allocation

Perhaps the most significant practical constraint for individual victims is resource. The NFIB receives over 800,000 fraud reports per year. Specialist cryptocurrency investigation units — including the NCCU and ROCUs with crypto capability — are well-resourced by UK standards but cannot actively investigate every individual report. Cases are prioritised by size, pattern, and the quality of available evidence. A victim who has lost £5,000 to a cryptocurrency investment scam faces a realistic prospect that their individual Action Fraud report will not receive active investigation, regardless of the technical capability that exists in specialist units. This is not a criticism of law enforcement; it is the arithmetic of fraud volume versus investigative capacity. It is also one of the reasons independent on-chain tracing — which can produce an intelligence report formatted for law enforcement submission — can add value to individual cases.

Key point: Police can trace Bitcoin. Whether they will trace it in your specific case depends on the size of your loss, the quality of evidence, whether your case forms part of a larger pattern, and whether suspects are within reachable jurisdiction. For most individual victims, the answer is that police capabilities exist but are not routinely deployed at the individual case level.

What victims can do when police cannot act fast enough

Knowing that law enforcement capabilities exist but may not be deployed for your individual case is frustrating. It does not mean you are without options. There are parallel steps available to cryptocurrency fraud victims that do not depend on police investigation timelines.

Report to Action Fraud immediately

Reporting to Action Fraud (0300 123 2040) remains the essential first step. Your Crime Reference Number (CRN) is the formal record of your loss and is required for any subsequent bank dispute, Section 75 claim, solicitor instruction, or civil court proceedings. Even if your case is not actively investigated by police, the CRN creates the paper trail on which any legal action is built. For a step-by-step guide to what to include in your Action Fraud report, see our companion guide: How to report a crypto scam in the UK.

Contact your bank immediately

If you made a bank transfer to purchase cryptocurrency that was then sent to a scammer — which is the most common payment chain in UK crypto investment fraud — contact your bank's fraud team as soon as possible. Faster payment recalls can sometimes be executed within hours if the receiving bank account is still active. Under the Payment Systems Regulator's mandatory reimbursement rules (in force from October 2024), banks are required to reimburse victims of authorised push payment fraud in many circumstances. The rules apply to the bank-to-exchange leg of the payment; the on-chain crypto leg is not reversible. Your bank should still be contacted regardless of how you paid.

Commission independent on-chain tracing

Independent on-chain tracing — conducted by a specialist analyst outside of law enforcement — can produce a documented intelligence report identifying where your crypto went, which exchange received it, and what supporting on-chain evidence exists. This intelligence report serves three practical purposes: it supports your Action Fraud submission with specific exchange attribution data; it is the evidence base for a Norwich Pharmacal Order application (see below); and it gives you a clear, documented picture of what happened on-chain before the trail goes cold or funds are moved further.

Norwich Pharmacal Orders

A Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) is a UK court order made under the inherent jurisdiction of the High Court. It compels a third party — typically a regulated exchange — to disclose information about an account holder. NPOs have been used successfully in UK cryptocurrency fraud cases, including cases where law enforcement was not actively investigating. To obtain an NPO, you need to instruct a specialist crypto-litigation solicitor and make a court application; this requires a clear on-chain trace identifying the specific exchange and account. CountWize does not provide legal advice and cannot make this application on your behalf, but the on-chain intelligence report we produce is precisely the evidence on which an NPO application is built.

Civil asset recovery proceedings

Where a suspect's identity is established — through an NPO, through law enforcement disclosure, or through other means — civil proceedings in the English courts can be used to pursue asset recovery. The English High Court has extensive experience in freezing orders and judgment enforcement in fraud cases. Civil proceedings are faster than criminal prosecutions and do not depend on the CPS deciding to prosecute. They are also more expensive and require the identification of a defendant with reachable assets. Specialist crypto-litigation firms have pursued civil recovery in UK courts successfully in recent years, and this route is increasingly viable as both court familiarity with crypto assets and the ecosystem of legal support around these cases grows.

Frequently asked questions

Can police trace Bitcoin transactions?

Yes, in many cases. Bitcoin and most other cryptocurrencies use public blockchains where every transaction is permanently recorded and visible to anyone. UK law enforcement agencies use professional blockchain analytics tools — including Chainalysis and Elliptic — to trace the movement of funds from victim to suspect wallets. The November 2025 Metropolitan Police seizure of £5 billion in Bitcoin is the most prominent recent example of police successfully identifying, tracing, and seizing crypto assets. However, tracing identifies where funds went — it does not automatically result in recovery, and significant constraints apply in cross-border and privacy-coin cases.

How do police track cryptocurrency transactions in the UK?

UK police track cryptocurrency through a combination of on-chain forensics, exchange cooperation, and legal powers. On-chain forensics involves analysing the public blockchain ledger using specialist tools to follow the movement of funds across addresses. When funds reach a regulated exchange, police can apply for Production Orders under PACE Schedule 5 to compel the exchange to disclose account-holder identity. In cross-border cases, mutual legal assistance treaties (MLATs) are used to obtain disclosure from exchanges operating in other jurisdictions. The process is thorough but slow — individual case investigations can take months to years.

Should I report crypto fraud to the police?

Yes — you should always report to Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk), which is the UK's national centre for fraud and cybercrime reporting. You will receive a Crime Reference Number, which is the formal record of your loss and is needed for any subsequent bank chargeback claim, Section 75 claim, solicitor instruction, or civil proceedings. Reporting also contributes to the NFIB's pattern intelligence, which is how police identify and pursue organised fraud networks. Reporting to the police does not guarantee an investigation into your specific case, but it is a necessary first step.

What can I do if police cannot recover my crypto?

If police are unable to act swiftly on your individual case, there are parallel steps available to you. First, contact your bank immediately — mandatory reimbursement rules under the PSR's October 2024 scheme may apply to the bank-to-exchange leg of the payment. Second, commission independent on-chain tracing: a documented intelligence report identifying which exchange received the stolen funds is the foundation for a Norwich Pharmacal Order — a UK court order compelling the exchange to disclose the account holder's identity. Third, instruct a specialist crypto-litigation solicitor who can advise on civil recovery proceedings using the tracing evidence as a basis. CountWize does not provide legal advice, but can refer you to appropriate specialist practitioners.

What was the Met Police £5bn Bitcoin seizure in 2025?

In November 2025, the Metropolitan Police announced the seizure of approximately £5 billion worth of Bitcoin — one of the largest cryptocurrency seizures by any law enforcement agency globally. The seizure followed a multi-year investigation involving on-chain forensic tracing, exchange cooperation under court orders, and coordination with overseas agencies. The case demonstrates that law enforcement has the technical capability to trace and seize very large amounts of cryptocurrency when sufficient investigative resources are applied. It also illustrates the constraints: the investigation took years, involved significant resources, and required complex international coordination — conditions that are not present in most individual fraud cases.

Who we cannot help

  • Your loss involved cash or bank transfers with no blockchain component — on-chain tracing requires on-chain data to work from.
