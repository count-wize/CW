What FCA authorisation actually means — and what it does not cover

The Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) is the UK financial services regulator responsible for authorising and supervising firms that conduct regulated financial activities. Under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA), it is a criminal offence to carry on a regulated activity in the UK without FCA authorisation. This framework was designed to protect consumers from unscrupulous operators — but it is routinely exploited by fraudulent firms who claim to hold authorisation they do not have.

What FCA authorisation covers

FCA authorisation permits a firm to conduct specific regulated activities, which are defined by statute. These include activities such as:

  • Managing investments or operating collective investment schemes
  • Providing investment advice or dealing in investments as principal or agent
  • Arranging deals in investments
  • Operating as a payment institution or e-money institution
  • Providing consumer credit or carrying on mortgage lending and administration
  • Operating as a claims management company (CMC) — which includes pursuing financial claims on behalf of consumers

Crucially, each FCA-authorised firm is authorised only for specific permitted activities listed on its Financial Services Register entry. Being FCA-authorised for one activity does not mean a firm can lawfully carry out other regulated activities. A firm authorised to provide payment services is not automatically authorised to manage investments or to operate as a claims management company.

What FCA authorisation does not mean

FCA authorisation is frequently misrepresented — both by fraudulent firms and in popular understanding. Several important points to understand:

  • FCA authorisation does not mean the FCA endorses or recommends a firm. It means the firm has met minimum regulatory standards to conduct specific activities. The FCA does not vouch for the quality of service or the honesty of the firm's management.
  • FCA authorisation does not guarantee that a firm will not defraud you. There have been cases of FCA-authorised firms engaging in fraud. Authorisation is a threshold, not a guarantee.
  • On-chain tracing and blockchain analysis are not regulated financial activities under current UK law. A company providing on-chain tracing reports does not need FCA authorisation to do so lawfully, but it also cannot claim FCA authorisation as a selling point for tracing services. CountWize does not claim, and does not need, FCA authorisation for the on-chain tracing work it performs.
  • Claims management is regulated by the FCA. Any firm that pursues financial claims on behalf of consumers for a fee must be authorised by the FCA as a claims management company (CMC). Many fake "crypto recovery" firms are operating illegally as unlicensed CMCs. If a firm charges you an upfront fee to pursue a financial claim on your behalf, check whether it holds FCA CMC authorisation specifically — not just any FCA authorisation.

Why fraudsters claim FCA authorisation

The FCA's brand is strong. Consumers have been told repeatedly that they should only deal with FCA-authorised firms for financial services. Fraudsters exploit this awareness by inserting FCA-related claims into their marketing, emails, and websites — sometimes using real FCA registration numbers taken from genuine firms — to bypass the consumer's natural scepticism. A consumer who checks "is this firm FCA-regulated?" and receives a confident "yes" with what appears to be a verifiable registration number is significantly more likely to proceed without further scrutiny.

The FCA publishes a fraud reporting tool and maintains a ScamSmart resource specifically because this pattern of FCA impersonation is so widespread in financial fraud. The FCA Warning List published on ScamSmart documents known firms operating without authorisation or cloning genuine firms' identities.

How to check the FCA register — step by step

The Financial Services Register is the definitive public record of firms and individuals that are authorised or registered by the FCA. It is publicly accessible at register.fca.org.uk. Checking it takes less than two minutes and is the single most important verification step you can take before engaging with any firm that claims FCA status.

  1. Go directly to the FCA register — do not use a link provided by the firm. Navigate independently to register.fca.org.uk. Do not click a link in an email or on the firm's website — clone firm operators create fake FCA-lookalike pages at similar-looking URLs.
  2. Search for the firm's exact trading name. Use the search bar at the top of the register. Enter the full, exact name of the firm as given to you. Try variant spellings. If the firm has given you a Firm Reference Number (FRN), you can also search by that.
  3. Check that the name on the register exactly matches the firm contacting you. Clone firms often use names almost identical to real FCA-authorised firms — for example, adding "Ltd", changing "UK" to "UK Ltd", or substituting a single character. Look carefully at the exact registered name, not just a broad match.
  4. Verify the permitted activities listed on the register entry. Even if the firm appears on the register, confirm that the activity it is offering is actually within its permitted permissions. A firm may be FCA-registered for payment services but not for investment management or claims management.
  5. Check the contact details on the register against the contact details the firm gave you. The register entry will list the firm's registered address, phone number, and sometimes email. Compare these directly against the contact information the firm has given you. A genuine FCA-authorised firm will have consistent, verifiable contact information. A clone firm's contact details will differ from the genuine entry.
  6. Check the FCA Warning List separately. The FCA Warning List at fca.org.uk/scamsmart documents firms that have been reported as operating without authorisation or impersonating genuine firms. Search the name there as well. Note that a firm not yet appearing on the Warning List is not necessarily legitimate — the list only reflects firms already reported.
  7. Call the genuine firm using the register's contact details to confirm. If a firm claims to be a well-known FCA-authorised institution — a bank, a real investment firm, or a known regulated entity — call that institution directly using the phone number on the FCA register (not the number the firm gave you) to confirm whether you are dealing with them.
Important: An FRN (Firm Reference Number) provided by a firm is not itself proof of legitimacy. Clone firms routinely provide the real FRN of the genuine firm they are impersonating. The FRN will therefore appear valid on the register — but the contact details, trading name variant, and website will differ from the genuine registration. Always verify all fields, not just the number.

What to do if the firm does not appear on the register

If a firm claims FCA authorisation but does not appear on the register — or if the firm appears on the FCA Warning List — you should:

  • Cease all communication with the firm immediately
  • Do not send any funds, even if the firm claims they are already owed
  • Do not provide any additional personal information
  • Report the firm to the FCA at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-us
  • Report the firm to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040

Red flags used by fake FCA impersonators

Fraudulent firms that falsely claim FCA status use a consistent set of tactics that, once you know to look for them, are identifiable. The following patterns should each trigger immediate scepticism and verification before you proceed.

  • ! Clone firm tactics — using a real firm's identity. The most sophisticated category. The fraudster creates a website, phone presence, and email domain that closely mimics a real FCA-authorised firm. They may use the genuine firm's FRN in their communications, knowing it will verify correctly on the register — while their own contact details are entirely different from the genuine firm's. The FCA has issued multiple warnings about clone firm activity, including in the crypto sector. Always call the genuine firm using register-verified contact details.
  • ! FCA logos and seals on their website or documents. The FCA logo is publicly available — any fraudster can copy it. The presence of an FCA logo on a website, email footer, or document is not evidence of authorisation. Equally, a "regulated by the FCA" statement in an email signature or website footer is not self-validating. Only the register confirms actual authorisation.
  • ! Spoofed FCA phone numbers or caller ID. Caller ID can be manipulated to show any number, including the FCA's own published contact line (0800 111 6768). If someone calls you claiming to be from the FCA or from a firm acting on the FCA's behalf, hang up and call the FCA directly on the published number to verify. The FCA does not make unsolicited calls to consumers about individual cases or offer to assist with fund recovery.
  • ! Email domains that look like the FCA or a genuine firm. Clone firms use email addresses from domains such as "fca-recovery.co.uk", "authorised-uk-recovery.com", or domains that substitute a character in a genuine firm's name (e.g., "coinbase-official.co.uk" rather than "coinbase.com"). The FCA's own emails come from @fca.org.uk addresses only. Any communication purportedly from the FCA using a different domain is fraudulent.
  • ! Unsolicited contact offering to help with an existing fraud. Victims of cryptocurrency fraud are frequently targeted a second time by fraudsters who have obtained their details from initial fraud intelligence networks. A cold call or email from a firm you have not heard of, offering to help you with a specific fraud you experienced, is a strong warning sign regardless of any FCA claims made. Legitimate firms do not conduct unsolicited outreach of this kind.
  • ! Requests for upfront fees before any work is performed. Legitimate FCA-authorised claims management companies (CMCs) and solicitors do sometimes charge fees — but the structure of those fees, when they are due, and what they cover will be set out clearly in a written client agreement before you commit. A firm that asks you to pay a fee (described as a "compliance fee", "processing fee", "verification fee", or "advance tax") before any work is undertaken, and before you have signed a clear written agreement, is exhibiting a primary warning sign of fraud.
  • ! Pressure to act quickly or keep the engagement secret. Fraudsters create artificial urgency ("your funds will be released in 48 hours if you act now") and discourage victims from seeking independent advice ("do not tell your bank about this — it will complicate the process"). These tactics serve to prevent the victim from pausing, verifying, or seeking a second opinion. Legitimate authorised firms do not operate this way.
  • ! Requests for seed phrases, private keys, or wallet access. No legitimate financial services firm, law firm, or authorised claims manager will ever ask for your cryptocurrency wallet seed phrase, private key, or password. These credentials provide direct, irreversible access to your wallet. Any request for them — regardless of how legitimate the firm appears — is a fraud signal. See the "We will never ask for..." box on this page.
The "second fraud" pattern: Victims of an initial crypto fraud are statistically at higher risk of being targeted again by fake recovery operators. The FCA has published specific guidance on this pattern at fca.org.uk/scamsmart. If someone contacts you unsolicited after you have suffered a crypto fraud, treat any FCA claims with heightened scepticism and verify independently before taking any action.

What fake crypto recovery firms typically claim — and why the claims are misleading

Beyond the false FCA-authorisation claim, fake crypto recovery operators use a standard set of substantive claims about what they can do. Understanding why these claims are misleading will help you evaluate any firm's communications more critically.

What the firm claims Why it is misleading
"We specialise in recovering crypto assets and have a high success rate." Cryptocurrency transactions are cryptographically irreversible. A firm cannot "reverse" or "recover" crypto in the way a bank can claw back a payment. What may be achievable — through civil legal action following on-chain tracing — is rarely a straightforward high-probability process. Any firm quoting success rates without explaining what "success" means and what the process involves is misrepresenting the nature of the work.
"We are FCA-authorised and have relationships with major exchanges." FCA authorisation does not give a firm special access to cryptocurrency exchanges. Exchanges cooperate with court orders (Norwich Pharmacal Orders, freezing injunctions) obtained by solicitors through proper legal process — not with informal requests from unverified third-party "recovery" firms. A firm claiming privileged exchange relationships without court-backed legal process is making an unverifiable and implausible claim.
"Pay our fee now and you will receive your funds within [X] days." No legitimate firm — FCA-authorised or otherwise — can commit to returning funds within a fixed timeframe because asset recovery (where it is achievable at all) depends on court processes, exchange cooperation, defendant location, and many other factors outside any firm's control. A guarantee of return within a defined period is a hallmark of the advance-fee fraud model, in which the "fee" is the fraud's actual profit and no recovery ever follows.
"Your funds are being held by us / by the FCA / by the exchange pending verification — pay a release fee to unlock them." This is the "funds in escrow" or "tax clearance" variant of advance-fee fraud. Neither the FCA, nor an exchange, nor any legitimate third party holds a fraud victim's funds pending a fee payment. Cryptocurrency assets lost to fraud are typically in the control of the fraudster, not held in escrow awaiting verification. This claim has no basis in how blockchain technology or UK financial regulation operates.
"We are regulated by the FCA and your money is protected under the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS)." The FSCS protects deposits and investments held by regulated firms — it does not cover money paid to a recovery firm as a service fee. Even if a firm were genuinely FCA-authorised, any fees you paid to it would not be FSCS-protected. The FSCS claim is a manipulation of consumer protections to create false confidence.
"We have unique software / forensic tools that the police do not have access to." Blockchain analytics is a real and mature field. Legitimate firms including Chainalysis, Elliptic, and TRM Labs provide tools to law enforcement agencies and regulated businesses. Claiming access to "unique" or "secret" forensic tools that only the firm possesses is not a credible description of how blockchain analysis works. The underlying data (blockchain transaction records) is publicly accessible — the value is in the analytical methodology and the intelligence databases, not secret tools.

How to assess a firm's claims

When evaluating any firm offering to assist with cryptocurrency fraud, apply the following tests before proceeding:

  • Verify FCA registration independently using the steps in Section 2 above — not through any link or documentation provided by the firm.
  • Ask for a written explanation of the process — what exactly will the firm do, in what sequence, and what does each stage cost? Legitimate firms can explain their process clearly. Fraudulent firms will be vague or inconsistent.
  • Ask specifically whether the firm is authorised as a Claims Management Company (CMC) — if the firm intends to pursue a financial claim on your behalf for a fee, it must hold CMC authorisation from the FCA specifically. Ask for the CMC FRN and verify it on the register.
  • Do not pay any upfront fee until you have verified the firm's identity, read and understood the written client agreement, and taken independent legal or financial advice if the amount is significant.
  • Check the FCA Warning List and consumer review sites — the FCA ScamSmart list and independently moderated review platforms can surface warning signs that are not immediately visible from the firm's own materials. Be aware that fake positive reviews are a common tactic.

What to do if you have already contacted a fake firm

If you have engaged with a firm that you now believe is falsely claiming FCA status — whether you have only made contact, paid a fee, or shared personal information — there are specific steps to take immediately. Time matters: acting quickly can limit further exposure.

Stop all further contact and payments

Do not respond to further calls or messages from the firm. Do not send any additional funds, regardless of the reason given — including "release fees", "processing fees", "verification charges", or promised refunds that require a further payment to unlock. Fraudulent operators rely on the sunk-cost psychology of previous payments to extract further sums. The only way to stop this cycle is to stop engaging entirely.

Report to Action Fraud

Report the fraud to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk (0300 123 2040). Provide: the firm's name, contact details, website URL, any registration numbers it claimed, all communications you received, dates and amounts of any payments, and bank or payment details you used. You will receive a crime reference number, which is important for all subsequent steps.

Report the firm to the FCA

Use the FCA's online reporting tool at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-us to report the fraudulent firm. The FCA can investigate, take enforcement action, and add the firm to its Warning List — protecting others from falling victim to the same operation. Your report contributes to the national picture even if it does not result in direct individual action on your case.

Contact your bank's fraud team immediately

If you made payments via bank transfer, contact your bank's fraud team immediately and ask:

  • Whether the payment can be recalled (most likely if the receiving account is still active and the payment was recent)
  • Whether an APP fraud claim applies under the PSR mandatory reimbursement scheme — if you were deceived into authorising the payment, you may have a claim. See our guide on UK banks and crypto scam reimbursement (2026)
  • Whether a Section 75 claim applies if you paid by credit card

Preserve all evidence

Do not delete any communications, emails, or records related to the firm. Take screenshots of the firm's website, social media presence, and any documents provided. Save all email and message threads. Record the dates and amounts of all payments. This evidence will be required for your Action Fraud report, any bank claim, and any subsequent police or legal action.

Seek independent advice before further action

Citizens Advice (citizensadvice.org.uk) can provide free, independent guidance on your options. If a significant sum is involved, consider consulting a solicitor regulated by the Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA). Be very cautious of any firm that contacts you after the fraud offering to help you "recover" what you lost — this is the "double fraud" pattern and is extremely common. See our guide: Recovery scam warning.

FAQ — common questions about fake FCA-authorised firms

How do I check if a crypto recovery firm is on the FCA Warning List?

Visit register.fca.org.uk and use the search bar to look up the firm's exact trading name. If you cannot find it there, check the FCA Warning List at fca.org.uk/scamsmart. A firm that claims to be FCA-authorised but does not appear on the Financial Services Register is operating illegally if it is promoting financial services in the UK. Do not rely on a firm-provided FRN without verifying it yourself directly on the register.

What is a clone firm and how does it work?

A clone firm copies the name, registration number, and branding of a real FCA-authorised firm to appear legitimate. They may use spoofed phone numbers or email domains that appear similar to the genuine firm. When victims check the FCA register and see the cloned firm's details, they believe they are dealing with a legitimate business. Always verify by contacting the genuine firm directly using contact details from the FCA register — not from the website or email you received.

Is it illegal for a firm to claim FCA authorisation it does not have?

Yes. Under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA), it is a criminal offence to carry on regulated financial activities — including promoting financial services — without FCA authorisation. Falsely claiming FCA status is a serious criminal offence. The FCA can prosecute such firms and individuals. If you encounter a firm making false FCA claims, report it to the FCA via their online reporting tool at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-us.

I have already paid a firm that claimed to be FCA-authorised. What should I do?

Stop all contact immediately — do not send any further money, do not share additional personal information, and do not accept any "refund" offers that require an upfront fee. Report the fraud to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk (0300 123 2040). Report the firm to the FCA at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-us. Contact your bank's fraud team to report the payment and ask whether it can be recalled or whether a chargeback applies. Preserve all evidence: communications, payment records, the firm's website, and any documents they provided.
