What this guide covers — and what it does not

Not every crypto fraud situation requires a solicitor immediately. In some cases, instructing a solicitor before you have any blockchain evidence is premature and costly. In other cases, waiting too long can mean assets are further dissipated and limitation periods begin to close. This guide is designed to help a UK crypto fraud victim understand the decision points clearly — and what on-chain tracing evidence can provide before or alongside legal action.

This guide does not provide legal advice. It does not recommend specific law firms. It does not promise that instructing a solicitor will result in your funds being returned. The decision to take legal action, and the strategy behind it, should be made with a qualified solicitor regulated by the Solicitors Regulation Authority (SRA). What this guide does is explain, in plain language, what the two primary tools available to victims — on-chain tracing and civil legal action — can each do independently and together.

Two related guides may be useful alongside this one: How crypto tracing works explains the methodology behind blockchain analysis in more detail, and Recovery scam warning explains the risks of engaging unregulated firms that claim to recover crypto funds.

What a crypto solicitor does

A solicitor specialising in crypto fraud is a qualified legal professional, regulated by the SRA, who uses civil legal tools to pursue a claim against a party responsible for a crypto fraud. Their role is fundamentally different from that of an on-chain tracing firm. Where tracing maps what happened on the blockchain, a solicitor uses courts, orders, and legal correspondence to compel action from exchanges, custodians, and other third parties.

The primary tools used by crypto fraud solicitors in UK civil proceedings include:

  • Letters Before Action (LBA): A formal written notice to an exchange or other party, informing them that legal proceedings will be issued unless they cooperate with an investigation or freeze an account linked to fraud proceeds. An LBA from a solicitor carries significantly more weight than a direct complaint from a victim.
  • Freezing injunctions and proprietary injunctions: Court orders that prevent a named party from moving, dissipating, or dealing with specific assets. A proprietary injunction asserts that the claimant has a proprietary claim over the assets themselves — relevant in crypto fraud where the victim's property has been traced into a specific wallet or account.
  • Norwich Pharmacal Orders (NPO) and Bankers Trust Orders: Court orders that compel a third party — typically an exchange — to disclose information they hold, such as the identity of an account holder, KYC documents, or transaction history. These are widely used in UK crypto fraud litigation to unmask pseudonymous wallet operators.
  • Compiling evidence packages for civil litigation: A solicitor will gather and present evidence — including blockchain tracing reports, bank records, communications, and expert witness statements — in a format suitable for court proceedings.
  • Liaison with Action Fraud and law enforcement: A solicitor can formally represent you in communications with the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB), the Metropolitan Police Cyber Crime Unit, and other agencies, and can press for action where a case meets the evidential threshold.
An important technical limit: A solicitor cannot reverse a blockchain transaction. Cryptocurrency transactions are irreversible at the protocol level. Legal tools work by freezing or recovering assets through court proceedings — compelling an exchange to freeze an account, ordering disclosure of identity information, or pursuing civil judgement against a named defendant. The speed and value at which these tools work depends heavily on whether the exchange is UK- or EU-regulated and whether assets remain in an accessible form.

For a solicitor to be effective in a crypto fraud case, they generally need: a traceable path from the victim's wallet to a regulated exchange deposit address; identification of the exchange concerned; and ideally a police reference number establishing that fraud has been formally reported. This is where on-chain tracing becomes the necessary first step for many victims.

What on-chain tracing does

On-chain tracing is a forensic analytical process that maps the flow of cryptocurrency transactions across the blockchain, from a starting point (typically the victim's wallet or the transaction in which funds were sent to the fraudster) through subsequent wallet hops to a destination. The destination — if it can be identified — is the critical piece of intelligence that enables legal action.

What on-chain tracing can produce

  • Fund flow mapping: A documented map of transactions from the victim's wallet, through any intermediate wallets or mixer services, to the final destination addresses — including exchange deposit addresses where identifiable.
  • Exchange cluster identification: Many exchange deposit addresses can be attributed to known exchanges through address clustering and proprietary blockchain intelligence databases. Where funds have reached a regulated exchange, this is the key finding that makes legal action viable.
  • Blockchain evidence report: A written report documenting the analysis methodology, the transaction graph, the attribution conclusions, and the caveats. This document is suitable for submission to a solicitor, court, or law enforcement agency as supporting evidence.
  • Timing and routing intelligence: Approximate timing of fund movements, the number of hops, any use of mixing services or chain-hopping (converting between blockchains), and any consolidation patterns that suggest organised fraud activity.

The single most important output of a tracing analysis, from a legal action perspective, is the identification of an exchange deposit address linked to a regulated custodian. If your funds can be shown to have arrived at a UK-regulated or EU-regulated exchange, that exchange can be the subject of an NPO or a freezing order — provided legal action is taken promptly.

Honest limits of on-chain tracing

  • Tracing shows where funds went — it cannot compel action. Identifying the exchange is necessary but not sufficient. Only legal authority — a court order obtained by a solicitor — can compel an exchange to freeze an account or disclose KYC records.
  • Funds routed through non-custodial mixers or privacy coins (such as Monero conversions or Tornado Cash transactions) may become untraceable at that point. The analysis will document where traceability ends.
  • Unregulated overseas exchanges may not respond to UK court orders. The practical value of a tracing report diminishes if funds have reached an exchange outside the jurisdiction of UK or EU courts.
  • Tracing is investigative intelligence, not a guarantee of outcome. We provide a factual written report based on on-chain data. What happens after that report depends on legal action, exchange cooperation, and court decisions that are outside CountWize's control.

When you should instruct a solicitor

The following criteria are not a checklist that must be completed before you take any action — they are indicators that a case is likely to be viable for civil legal action and that instructing a solicitor is a proportionate step. You do not need to meet every criterion; the more that apply, the stronger the case for proceeding. This assessment is educational only — the decision should ultimately be made with a qualified solicitor after a case-specific review.

  • 1 You lost £5,000 or more. Below this threshold, the cost of instructing a specialist crypto fraud solicitor — including court fees, NPO applications, and hourly rates — often risks exceeding the potential amount recovered. For smaller losses, consider the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) complaint route if a regulated firm was involved, or small claims. For losses above £5,000, the cost-benefit calculation shifts and specialist legal advice is worth obtaining.
  • 2 You have a blockchain tracing report showing exchange-linked deposit addresses. The single most actionable finding in an on-chain tracing report is evidence that your funds reached a regulated exchange's deposit address. Without this, a solicitor has limited tools. With it, they can issue an LBA and apply for an NPO to obtain the account holder's identity. Commissioning a tracing report before instructing a solicitor is often the correct sequencing.
  • 3 The exchange is UK-regulated or EU-regulated. A UK-regulated exchange (FCA-registered) is subject to UK court orders and is obligated to respond to legal process. A major EU exchange is subject to equivalent regulation in its jurisdiction. If the destination exchange is regulated, a solicitor can apply for an NPO with a reasonable prospect of the exchange cooperating. If the exchange is unregulated and based outside major jurisdictions, legal tools have far less traction.
  • 4 You received no response from the exchange after a formal complaint. Most reputable exchanges have a compliance or fraud reporting function. If you have already sent a formal complaint (in writing, with transaction IDs, police reference, and your tracing evidence) and received no substantive response or refusal to act, that sequence strengthens the case for legal intervention. An LBA from a solicitor signals that court proceedings are imminent and is often more effective than direct victim contact.
  • 5 You have a police reference number from Action Fraud. A reference number from Action Fraud (which you obtain at actionfraud.police.uk) establishes that the fraud has been formally reported to UK law enforcement. This reference is typically required or strongly recommended by solicitors at the point of engagement. It also demonstrates to courts and exchanges that this is not a civil dispute but a reported criminal matter.
  • 6 The fraud occurred within the last 6 years. The standard limitation period for civil fraud claims in England and Wales is six years from the date the fraud was discovered (or reasonably discoverable). Acting before this period expires is essential — once limitation passes, a civil claim may be time-barred regardless of the strength of the evidence. If the fraud occurred recently, time is a factor but not yet critical. If it occurred several years ago, obtaining legal advice promptly is more urgent.

When to hold off on instructing a solicitor

There are circumstances in which instructing a solicitor immediately is not the most effective first move. This is not a reason to delay indefinitely, but a recognition that the quality of evidence and the size of the claim affect whether legal action is viable.

  • Loss under £2,000. For smaller losses, consider the small claims route at the County Court (for claims up to £10,000 in England and Wales), a Financial Ombudsman Service complaint if a regulated firm's conduct is in question, or a formal complaint to the exchange under its published complaints procedure. Specialist solicitor fees at these loss levels would not be proportionate. Citizens Advice (citizensadvice.org.uk) can help you understand your options at no cost.
  • Funds traced to a non-regulated overseas wallet with no exchange link. If on-chain tracing identifies that funds have moved to self-custodied wallets or non-regulated overseas platforms with no KYC requirements and no UK or EU jurisdiction, a solicitor has very limited tools available. The practical value of legal action in this scenario is low unless additional intelligence becomes available. A solicitor can confirm this assessment — but they will typically say the same thing.
  • No blockchain evidence yet. Instructing a solicitor before you have any tracing evidence means the solicitor will be working without the key intelligence needed to identify a target for legal process. The more appropriate sequencing is usually: commission a tracing report first, then take the report to a solicitor. This approach also gives the solicitor a clearer picture of whether the case is viable before you commit to legal fees.
  • You have already received a full refund from your bank under the APP scam reimbursement scheme. Under the Authorised Push Payment (APP) fraud reimbursement rules that came into force in October 2023, many UK banks are required to reimburse victims of APP scams up to £85,000. If your bank has already reimbursed you in full, civil legal action against the exchange may not be necessary. If your bank has partially reimbursed you, legal action for the unreimbursed portion may still be appropriate. See our guide on APP fraud reimbursement and crypto for more detail.

The typical process: step by step

For a UK crypto fraud victim where civil legal action is appropriate, the following is the typical sequence of steps. This is a general description only — individual cases vary considerably in timeline and complexity, and a solicitor will advise on the specific strategy for your situation.

1

Commission an on-chain tracing report

Engage a blockchain analysis firm to map the flow of your funds from the sending transaction through to their current or last-known location. The report should identify, where possible, the exchange deposit addresses where funds arrived, the exchange(s) involved, and the timing and routing of movements. This report becomes the foundation for all subsequent steps.

2

Obtain an Action Fraud reference number

Report the fraud to Action Fraud at actionfraud.police.uk and obtain a crime reference number. This is a formal step that most solicitors will require before engagement, and it establishes the matter as a criminal complaint on the public record. The report should include dates, amounts, wallet addresses, and the tracing evidence you have gathered.

3

Consult a solicitor with your evidence package

Take your tracing report, your Action Fraud reference, and any other relevant documentation (bank records showing the fiat-to-crypto purchase, exchange transaction records, communications with the fraudster) to an initial solicitor consultation. Many specialist crypto fraud solicitors offer a free or low-cost initial consultation. They will assess the viability of the claim and advise on the likely costs and process.

4

Solicitor issues a Letter Before Action to the identified exchange(s)

If your tracing report has identified a regulated exchange, the solicitor will typically begin by sending a formal Letter Before Action to that exchange's legal team, setting out the fraud, the evidence, and what action is required — typically freezing the account in question and preserving the relevant records. This letter gives the exchange an opportunity to cooperate before court proceedings are issued.

5

Apply for a Norwich Pharmacal Order or Bankers Trust Order if needed

If the exchange does not cooperate with the LBA — or if it has responded but declines to identify the account holder or freeze the assets — the next legal step is typically an application to the High Court for a Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) or a Bankers Trust Order. An NPO compels the exchange to disclose the identity, KYC records, and account details of the holder of the flagged account. This is one of the most powerful disclosure tools available in UK civil fraud proceedings and has been successfully obtained against a number of major exchanges operating in the UK.

6

Civil litigation or negotiated settlement

Once the identity of the account holder is established via NPO disclosure, the solicitor can issue civil proceedings against them directly. In many cases, the threat of identified proceedings leads to a negotiated settlement without a full trial. Where the defendant cannot be located or is overseas, the options narrow — but in some cases, orders can still be obtained and enforced against UK-held assets. The solicitor will advise at each stage on the prospects and costs of continuing.

Honest limits: what neither tracing nor a solicitor can guarantee

CountWize is committed to being direct about the limits of what blockchain evidence reports can achieve, because victims are routinely misled by firms that promise outcomes they cannot deliver. The following are facts about how this process works that every victim should understand before spending money on any service, including ours.

On-chain tracing produces a forensic evidence document. It documents, to the best of our analytical capability, where your funds went on the blockchain, based on publicly available on-chain data and proprietary intelligence about exchange address clusters. It is not legal advice. It does not by itself freeze any account, initiate any legal process, or guarantee any outcome. It is a piece of evidence that can be used — by a solicitor, by law enforcement, or by a court — to support a legal or investigative process.

The outcomes achievable through civil legal action depend on a number of factors that are outside anyone's control at the outset of a case:

  • Whether your funds reached a regulated exchange. If funds have been fully converted and moved to unregulated, non-KYC platforms or to self-custody wallets with no exchange link, the primary legal tools available to a solicitor have no target to operate against. A tracing report will document this honestly — and in such cases, the prognosis for civil recovery is poor regardless of the quality of legal representation.
  • Whether the exchange cooperates with legal orders. Regulated exchanges are legally obligated to respond to court orders in their jurisdiction. However, the timeline of compliance, the completeness of the information disclosed, and whether a defendant's account still contains assets all affect what happens next. An exchange may disclose the account holder's identity only to find the account was registered with false documents or holds no remaining assets.
  • Whether assets have been further dissipated. Fraudsters move assets quickly. From the moment of the initial fraud transaction, time is a factor. Assets that remain in an exchange account at the point of an NPO application can potentially be frozen. Assets that have already been withdrawn — particularly to cash, privacy coins, or overseas non-regulated platforms — may not be recoverable through civil process.
  • The financial position and location of the defendant. Even where a civil judgment is obtained, enforcement depends on the defendant having assets within the jurisdiction. Where fraud is committed by overseas operators or organised crime groups, obtaining a judgment and enforcing it are two different challenges.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a crypto solicitor cost?

Costs vary considerably. Some specialist crypto fraud solicitors offer free initial consultations. Hourly rates for specialist solicitors typically range from £250 to £500 per hour, though some firms offer conditional fee arrangements (no-win, no-fee) for high-value cases where the prospects of recovery are assessed as strong. Court fees for NPO applications and other High Court applications add to the total cost. CountWize does not provide legal advice or referrals to specific solicitors — verify any firm's status at sra.org.uk.

Can I go straight to the police instead of a solicitor?

You should report to Action Fraud (actionfraud.police.uk) and obtain a reference number — this is useful for any future legal action and is a step most solicitors will require. However, police rarely investigate individual crypto fraud cases below six figures, and the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau triages reports against resource constraints. Civil action via a solicitor is usually the faster route to asset recovery in mid-range cases, and the two approaches are not mutually exclusive — you can report to Action Fraud and pursue civil proceedings simultaneously.

Does having a tracing report guarantee a solicitor will take my case?

No. A tracing report significantly strengthens your position by giving the solicitor concrete evidence of where funds went, but solicitors assess each case individually. Their assessment will consider the regulated status of the exchange identified, the current balance of any accounts linked to the fraud, the value of the claim relative to likely costs, and the prospects of locating and enforcing against a defendant. A report showing clear exchange attribution is far more actionable than one showing funds at unidentified wallets — but a solicitor may still conclude that the economics of the case do not support proceeding.

What is a Norwich Pharmacal Order?

A Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) is a UK court order that compels a third party — such as a cryptocurrency exchange — to disclose information it holds, including the identity of an account holder, KYC documents, and transaction records associated with a specific wallet address or account. NPOs are frequently used in UK crypto fraud litigation to unmask pseudonymous wallet operators. The application is made to the High Court and must demonstrate that the respondent is mixed up in the wrongdoing (even if innocent) and that the order is necessary and proportionate. Obtaining an NPO requires legal representation and carries court fees in addition to solicitor costs.
