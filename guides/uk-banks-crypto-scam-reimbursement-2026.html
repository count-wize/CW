UK banks and crypto scam reimbursement (2026) — what the PSR APP rules cover and exclude

Overview — the October 2024 PSR APP reimbursement scheme

Authorised Push Payment (APP) fraud is the deception of a bank customer into authorising a payment to an account controlled by a fraudster. Because the customer themselves instructs the bank to make the transfer, traditional banking rules treated the bank as having properly executed the customer's authorised instruction — and victims were frequently left with no reimbursement at all.

That position changed materially on 7 October 2024, when the UK Payment Systems Regulator (PSR) introduced a mandatory APP fraud reimbursement requirement. The new scheme, which replaced the voluntary Contingent Reimbursement Model (CRM) Code that had governed most major UK banks, gives victims a legally enforceable right to reimbursement from their bank — subject to defined exceptions.

What the scheme does at a glance

  • Mandatory reimbursement — the default position is that banks must reimburse APP fraud victims, rather than refuse with the burden on the victim to argue the case.
  • Maximum claim value of £85,000 — set by reference to the FSCS deposit protection limit. Claims above this value are only partially covered.
  • Cost shared 50/50 between the sending bank (where the victim's account is held) and the receiving bank (where the fraudster's account is held). This creates an incentive for both ends of the payment chain to detect and prevent fraud.
  • Maximum excess of £100 — banks cannot apply a higher excess even on large claims.
  • Statutory time limits — banks must resolve claims within 5 business days for typical cases, with up to 35 business days for complex cases requiring further investigation.
  • Independent escalation — refused claims can be referred free of charge to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS).

What payment systems are covered

The scheme covers payments made through the two UK regulated payment systems over which the PSR has jurisdiction:

  • Faster Payments — the everyday UK retail payments system used for typical bank transfers.
  • CHAPS — the Clearing House Automated Payment System, used for larger value, same-day transfers (often property purchases).

The scheme does not apply to card payments (which have their own chargeback protections under the card networks' rules), to international wire transfers (SWIFT), or — critically for many crypto victims — to direct cryptocurrency transfers between wallets. Blockchain transactions are not regulated UK payment systems, and no UK financial regulator currently has jurisdiction over on-chain transfers.

This jurisdictional point is the single most important factor in whether the PSR scheme can apply to a crypto-related fraud, and it is dealt with in detail in the next section.

Eligibility — who and what qualifies in a crypto context

Two questions determine eligibility for any individual claim: who you are (the "consumer" test) and what kind of payment you made (the "scope" test). Both must be satisfied for the scheme to apply.

Who qualifies — the consumer test

The PSR scheme covers two categories of victim:

  • Personal customers — individual consumers holding a personal account at a UK payment service provider that participates in Faster Payments or CHAPS.
  • Micro-enterprises and charities — businesses and charities with fewer than 10 employees and an annual turnover or balance sheet of less than €2 million (set at the EU micro-enterprise threshold).

Larger SMEs and corporates are not covered by the mandatory scheme. They retain such rights as may exist under contractual terms with their bank, common law, or other regulatory provisions, but they cannot rely on the PSR scheme as a default reimbursement right.

What qualifies — the scope test in a crypto context

For a crypto-related fraud, the eligibility question turns on which leg of the transaction chain the victim is seeking reimbursement for. Crypto fraud cases typically follow a sequence: GBP held in a UK bank account → GBP transferred to a crypto exchange via Faster Payments → cryptocurrency purchased on the exchange → cryptocurrency sent on-chain to a fraudster's wallet. The PSR scheme can only cover the GBP Faster Payments leg.

Transaction type Within PSR scope?
GBP transferred from a UK bank account by Faster Payments to a fraudulent recipient's UK bank account Yes — squarely within scope. This is the textbook APP fraud case.
GBP transferred by Faster Payments to a UK-regulated crypto exchange to fund an account that was then used in fraud Potentially — depends on whether the fraud was at the bank-transfer stage or later (the "buying crypto" issue, see below).
GBP transferred by CHAPS for a high-value crypto-investment fraud Yes — CHAPS is within scope on the same basis as Faster Payments.
Cryptocurrency sent directly from a personal wallet to a fraudster's wallet on the blockchain No — on-chain transfers are not regulated UK payment systems.
Card payment (debit or credit) to a fraudulent crypto platform No — but card chargeback protections under the card scheme rules may apply separately.
International wire transfer (SWIFT) to fund a fraudulent overseas crypto platform No — SWIFT is outside PSR jurisdiction.

What qualifies as APP fraud in a crypto investment context

To fall within the PSR scheme, the GBP transfer itself must be the result of fraudulent deception. The clearest crypto-related scenarios are:

  • Fake investment platform fraud: the victim is induced to send GBP by Faster Payments to a "broker" or "trading platform" that is in fact a fraudulent operation. The fraudster's UK bank account is the direct recipient.
  • Pig-butchering / romance-investment fraud: the victim is groomed online over weeks or months and then directed to send GBP to a wallet-funding account controlled by the fraudsters. See our companion guide: Pig-butchering scams explained.
  • Impersonation fraud directing payment to crypto: the fraudster impersonates a bank, the police, HMRC, or a government agency and instructs the victim to "secure" their funds by buying crypto and sending it onwards.
  • Recovery scam fraud: a victim of an earlier crypto fraud is approached by a "recovery firm" claiming to be able to retrieve the lost funds for an upfront Faster Payments fee, which is the second-stage fraud.

In each of these cases, the GBP Faster Payments transfer is the regulated transaction the scheme can cover — provided the bank cannot establish one of the recognised exceptions discussed below.

Exclusions — what the scheme does NOT cover

The PSR scheme is mandatory in default but contains defined exceptions under which a bank can legitimately decline to reimburse, in whole or in part. Understanding these exceptions is essential — they are the grounds banks rely on when refusing crypto-related claims.

1. Out-of-scope payment types

As covered above, payments outside Faster Payments or CHAPS are simply not within scope. This includes direct on-chain crypto transfers, card payments, international wires, and internal book transfers within a single non-bank platform. The PSR scheme cannot reimburse losses from these channels.

2. The "buying crypto" argument

One of the most contested exclusions in practice. Banks frequently refuse APP fraud claims involving GBP transfers to a regulated crypto exchange (such as Coinbase, Kraken, or Binance) on the basis that the bank transfer itself was a voluntary purchase of cryptocurrency, not a fraud. Under this argument, any fraud that subsequently took place — for example, the customer being persuaded to send the cryptocurrency on to a fraudster — happened after the customer received what they paid for (the crypto on the exchange), and therefore outside the bank's chain of responsibility.

This argument is more or less persuasive depending on facts including: whether the GBP went to a regulated exchange or directly to a fraudster's UK account; whether the customer made repeated transfers over time; whether bank warnings were displayed at the point of payment; and whether the customer engaged with the bank's fraud team. Many crypto-investment APP refusals turn on this point and are subsequently re-examined by the FOS, which has reached varied decisions on similar facts.

3. The consumer standard of caution

Under PSR rules, a bank can reduce or refuse reimbursement if the victim did not meet the "consumer standard of caution." This requires that the victim:

  • Took heed of clear, unambiguous warnings issued by their bank at or before the time of payment — including automated scam-warning overlays, text or call interventions from the fraud team, and Confirmation of Payee (CoP) name-mismatch notices.
  • Reported the fraud to the bank promptly after becoming aware of it, without unreasonable delay.
  • Cooperated with the bank and, where relevant, the police in any subsequent investigation — including providing information on request.

Crucially, the warnings the bank relies on must be specific, clear and unambiguous. A generic "protect yourself from fraud" message at the bottom of a payment screen is not the same as a targeted warning that this specific payment pattern is consistent with investment fraud. The Financial Ombudsman has, in published decisions, reduced the weight given to generic warnings.

4. Voluntary payments and civil disputes

Payments that are not the result of deception are excluded. The principal categories are:

  • Civil disputes — payments made for goods or services where there was a genuine commercial relationship and the dispute is about delivery, quality, or terms. The bank will direct the customer to the small-claims courts or the seller's dispute process.
  • Private purchase transactions — payments to a private individual for items such as vehicles, tickets, or rental deposits. Even where the seller turned out to be fraudulent, private-purchase transactions are excluded from mandatory reimbursement.
  • Gifts and intra-family transfers — payments where the customer voluntarily intended to make an unconditional transfer.

5. First-party fraud

If the bank has reasonable grounds to believe the customer was complicit in the fraud — for example, knowingly acting as a "money mule" to receive and onward-move fraud proceeds, or fabricating an APP fraud claim — no reimbursement is required. This category is intended to capture genuine wrongdoing by the claimant, not to provide a refusal route in genuine victim cases.

6. Gross negligence

Banks retain a residual gross-negligence exception, distinct from the consumer standard of caution. This is a high bar: ordinary inattention, even substantial carelessness, is not gross negligence. Gross negligence in PSR practice is intended to capture extreme circumstances — for example, deliberately ignoring direct, repeated bank warnings issued in clear terms.

7. Claim value over £85,000

The statutory cap means that losses above £85,000 are only partially reimbursed under the scheme. The cap is per-claim. A victim who lost £200,000 to APP fraud can recover up to £85,000 under the scheme; the remaining £115,000 must be pursued by other routes — civil litigation against any identifiable counterparty, an NPO application via a solicitor, or law-enforcement-led asset recovery. How to claim — step-by-step process

If a GBP Faster Payments or CHAPS leg of a crypto-related fraud falls within scope, the claims process is straightforward but time-sensitive. The single most important factor in the outcome is how quickly and completely the claim is reported and documented.

  1. Report to your bank's fraud team immediately. Phone your bank as soon as you suspect fraud. Many UK banks have 24/7 fraud lines listed on the back of debit cards and on the bank's website. State explicitly that you wish to make an APP fraud claim under the PSR mandatory reimbursement scheme. Ask for the agent's name, the time and date of the call, and a reference number for the report. If a recall of the funds is still feasible (where the destination account has not yet drained the money out), the bank may attempt this immediately.
  2. Report to Action Fraud. Report the fraud to the UK national fraud reporting centre at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. You will be issued a crime reference number (CRN). Your bank will ask for this, and the FOS will require it if the claim is escalated. When reporting, include all destination account details, wallet addresses if any cryptocurrency was involved, transaction IDs, screenshots of the fraudulent platform or communications, and a clear timeline of events.
  3. Submit the APP fraud claim in writing. Do not rely on the phone call alone. Follow up by email or via the bank's secure message centre setting out: each payment date and amount; why the payment was the result of deception; the Action Fraud crime reference number; any relevant evidence; and an explicit request for reimbursement under the PSR mandatory scheme. A written record is essential evidence in any subsequent FOS referral.
  4. Compile evidence in parallel. While the bank investigates, gather: bank statements showing all relevant payments; communications with the fraudster (text, email, WhatsApp, voice notes); URLs and full-page screenshots of any platform used; identity documents the fraudster provided; the timeline of events; and screenshots of any warnings the bank displayed at the point of payment. For crypto cases, also gather all on-chain transaction IDs and wallet addresses involved.
  5. Respond promptly to the bank's information requests. The bank's investigation will typically include questions about how the customer was contacted, what warnings were given, and whether the destination account was new. Respond fully and promptly. Keep copies of every response.
  6. If the decision is favourable, check the calculation. If reimbursement is approved, verify it covers all in-scope payments, and that the excess applied is no more than £100. Partial reimbursement is sometimes offered — accepting a partial offer does not necessarily preclude a subsequent FOS challenge, but you should record any objection in writing before accepting.
  7. If refused, request the formal final response (deadlock letter), then escalate to the FOS. Refusal is not the end of the road — see the If your bank refuses section below.
Practical tip: Keep a single dated chronological file (digital or paper) recording every conversation, every document, and every reference number. This file is the backbone of any subsequent FOS referral or civil action and can also support an on-chain intelligence engagement.

Timelines — statutory deadlines under the PSR rules

The PSR scheme imposes hard time limits both on the customer and on the bank. Missing them can be fatal to a claim.

Customer timelines

  • Reporting promptness: the customer must report the fraud "without unreasonable delay" once they become aware. There is no fixed statutory deadline, but the longer the delay, the more likely the bank will rely on the consumer standard of caution to reduce reimbursement.
  • 13-month outer cooperation limit: reimbursement claims must be raised within 13 months of the disputed transaction.
  • FOS referral window: the customer has 6 months from the bank's final response (deadlock letter) to refer the matter to the Financial Ombudsman Service. After this, the FOS will not generally accept the case.

Bank timelines

Stage Bank deadline
Initial decision on a typical APP fraud claim 5 business days from receipt of all required information
Initial decision on a complex APP fraud claim Up to 35 business days, with reasoned justification for the extension
Reimbursement once a claim is approved Without undue delay following the decision
Final response (deadlock letter) on a complaint 8 weeks from formal complaint registration (FCA complaint-handling rules)

Note the distinction between the PSR's APP-fraud-claim deadlines (5 / 35 business days) and the FCA's general complaint-handling deadline of 8 weeks for the deadlock letter. The PSR clock applies to the substantive APP fraud assessment; the FCA clock is the umbrella deadline that triggers the right to FOS escalation.

If your bank refuses — escalation paths

A bank refusal is the start of the next stage, not the end of the case. Several routes are available, and they can run in parallel rather than sequentially.

1. Internal appeal — request reconsideration in writing

Before escalating externally, ask the bank to reconsider in writing, setting out specifically which findings or arguments you challenge and providing any new evidence. Internal reconsideration is faster than the FOS and sometimes succeeds, particularly if you can produce evidence the bank did not have at first decision (for example, an Action Fraud disclosure, an on-chain intelligence report, or a documented warning the bank claimed to have given but cannot evidence).

2. Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS)

The Financial Ombudsman Service is a free, independent dispute-resolution body. If your bank has issued a final response or 8 weeks have passed since you raised a formal complaint, you can refer the matter to the FOS within 6 months of the final response.

The FOS reviews the case on its individual facts — it is not bound to follow the bank's reasoning, and it has the power to direct the bank to reimburse you in full, in part, or with interest. Many crypto-APP-fraud cases that are refused at first instance are upheld in whole or in part by the FOS; outcomes depend on the specific facts. The FOS publishes redacted decisions on its website that are useful as comparator authority.

3. Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) reports

The FCA does not adjudicate individual disputes — that is the FOS's role — but reporting concerns about a bank's APP-fraud handling to the FCA contributes to its supervisory data. If a pattern of poor APP-fraud handling emerges across many customers of a single firm, the FCA can act on a regulatory basis. Reports can be made via the FCA's website.

4. Civil litigation

If the bank claim is refused and FOS escalation either fails or does not cover the full loss, civil litigation against the receiving bank, the fraudster, or any identifiable enabler may be available. This requires legal advice. For crypto-related cases, an on-chain intelligence report identifying the destination exchange and wallet attribution is often the foundation for an NPO application to compel exchange disclosure of KYC records. See our companion guide: When to instruct a crypto solicitor.

5. Police and law enforcement

Action Fraud reports feed into the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB) and may contribute to broader operations. While individual case prosecution is rare, intelligence-sharing matters.

Avoid recovery scams: after a fraud, victims are sometimes contacted by firms claiming to "specialise in crypto recovery" for an upfront fee. The FCA has issued repeated warnings about this category at fca.org.uk/scamsmart. No legitimate firm asks for upfront fees, control of your wallet, or your seed phrase. See our guide: Recovery scam warning.

FAQ — common crypto-APP-fraud questions

Does the PSR scheme cover money I sent in crypto directly to a scammer's wallet?

No. The PSR scheme covers Faster Payments and CHAPS — UK regulated bank payment systems. On-chain cryptocurrency transfers are not within scope. If your only loss was a direct wallet-to-wallet crypto transfer, the PSR scheme does not apply. Other routes — Action Fraud reporting, on-chain tracing, civil legal action via a solicitor — may still be relevant.

I sent GBP by Faster Payments to Coinbase, then bought crypto and sent it to a scammer. Am I covered?

It depends on the facts. Your bank may argue this was a voluntary purchase of cryptocurrency, not APP fraud — because the Faster Payments transfer reached a real, regulated exchange and you received the crypto you paid for. Whether this argument holds depends on the surrounding circumstances: whether you were instructed to buy crypto by a fraudster, whether warnings were given, whether transfers were repeated, and other context. Banks regularly refuse on this basis; the FOS regularly reviews and sometimes overturns such refusals. The case must be assessed on its own facts.

What is the maximum I can claim under the PSR scheme?

£85,000 per claim. This is set at the FSCS deposit-protection limit. Losses above this value are only partially recoverable under the scheme — the remainder must be pursued through other routes (civil litigation, NPO disclosure applications, or law-enforcement-led asset recovery).

How long do I have to report an APP fraud to my bank?

You must report "without unreasonable delay" once you become aware of the fraud, and in any event within 13 months of the disputed transaction. The longer the delay, the more likely the bank will reduce reimbursement under the consumer standard of caution. Report as quickly as you can — even if you are not yet certain whether you have been defrauded, you can update the report as facts emerge.

My bank rejected my claim. Is that final?

No. You can ask the bank to reconsider in writing, and you have the right to refer the case to the Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) free of charge — within 6 months of the bank's final response letter. The FOS reviews the bank's decision independently and has the power to direct the bank to reimburse you. Many crypto-related APP-fraud refusals have been overturned by the FOS on review.
