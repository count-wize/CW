What the ASA is and what enforcement notices do

The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) is the UK's independent advertising regulator. It is a self-regulatory body — funded by the advertising industry rather than by government — but it operates under a regulatory framework backed by statute and by the cooperation of government bodies including the FCA, Trading Standards, and Ofcom. Its remit covers almost all advertising in the UK: online advertising (paid search, display, social media), broadcast advertising (TV, radio), print, direct mail, and marketing communications.

The ASA enforces two main codes. The UK Code of Non-broadcast Advertising and Direct & Promotional Marketing (the CAP Code) governs online and print advertising. The UK Code of Broadcast Advertising (the BCAP Code) governs TV and radio. Both codes require advertising to be legal, decent, honest, and truthful — with specific provisions that apply to financial advertising, investments, and claims about the ability to help consumers in financial difficulty.

What an ASA enforcement notice does in practice

When the ASA investigates a complaint and upholds it, several things happen. The advertiser is required to amend or withdraw the non-compliant advertisement. The ruling is published on the ASA website at asa.org.uk/rulings — publicly searchable by company name, advertiser, and sector. This means a victim researching a firm can find upheld ASA rulings against it in a straightforward search, and those rulings appear in Google results for searches on the firm's name.

Beyond publication, the ASA has several escalation routes for persistent non-compliance or serious breaches:

  • Referral to Trading Standards: for advertisers who persistently ignore ASA rulings, particularly where the breach involves misleading financial claims or consumer harm, the ASA can refer the matter to Trading Standards for investigation under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 — which does carry criminal penalties.
  • Referral to the FCA: where an advertisement relates to financial services and may constitute a financial promotion or involve an unauthorised firm, the ASA can and does refer cases to the FCA for regulatory action under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000.
  • Ad blocking through platforms: the ASA works with Google, Meta, and other digital platforms through the Internet Advertising Bureau's agreement to block paid advertising from persistent non-compliant advertisers. This means a firm that ignores ASA rulings can have its ability to purchase digital advertising removed.
  • Referral to Ofcom: for broadcast advertising breaches, the ASA can refer persistent offenders to Ofcom, which has direct statutory powers over broadcasters.
Important: The ASA does not fine advertisers directly and does not award compensation to complainants. Its power is reputational — the public ruling — combined with the threat of escalation to bodies that do have enforcement and prosecution powers. For fraud victims, the ASA database is a due-diligence tool, not a compensation mechanism.

How to use the ASA rulings database

The ASA publishes all adjudicated rulings at asa.org.uk/rulings. You can search by advertiser name, by sector (including "finance" and "cryptocurrency"), and filter by outcome ("Upheld" for cases where the ASA found the advertising breached its codes). Any person researching a firm that claims to help with crypto losses should run a search on that firm's name before engaging. Multiple upheld rulings — especially relating to recovery claims, outcome guarantees, or false regulatory status — should be treated as serious warning signals.

The 2025–2026 wave of ASA enforcement actions in crypto and financial services

The period from mid-2025 into 2026 has seen a materially heightened level of ASA enforcement activity in the crypto and financial-services advertising space. This is not coincidental. Several regulatory and market developments converged to make crypto advertising a priority enforcement area.

The October 2023 FCA financial promotions regime: the turning point

In October 2023, the FCA's new financial promotions regime for cryptoassets came into force. This regime requires that any crypto financial promotion communicated to UK consumers must either be made by an FCA-authorised person, or approved by an FCA-authorised person before communication. Promotions must include prescribed risk warnings. This created, for the first time, a statutory framework governing the content of crypto advertising in the UK — and a clear legal basis for both FCA and ASA enforcement action against non-compliant promotions.

The October 2023 regime generated a significant volume of non-compliant advertising that came into the ASA's investigation pipeline during 2024 and into 2025. The ASA and FCA have coordinated enforcement activity, with the FCA acting against firms that communicated unapproved promotions and the ASA acting against the specific misleading content of those promotions.

Fake celebrity endorsements: a major enforcement focus

Fake celebrity endorsements have been one of the most consistently enforced categories of misleading crypto advertising in the 2025–2026 period. The ASA, working in coordination with the FCA and social media platforms, has taken action against numerous advertisements using the likenesses of high-profile public figures — including politicians, business leaders, sports personalities, and entertainment figures — to promote crypto investment platforms and, in some cases, firms claiming to assist with crypto losses.

How fake endorsement advertising works

The technical production of fake endorsement advertising has become increasingly sophisticated. Scammers and fraudulent advertising networks use several methods:

  • Deepfake video: AI-generated or manipulated video content that appears to show a known public figure speaking positively about a crypto platform or "opportunity". The audio is often synthesised to match the individual's voice and cadence. These videos can be of sufficient quality to appear credible to a casual viewer.
  • Still image and fabricated quote: combining a real photograph of a public figure with a fabricated quote — presented as a news headline, a social media post, or a screenshot of an interview — to imply endorsement. The image may have been taken from a legitimate news source and repurposed.
  • Cloned news website pages: creating fake versions of well-known UK news websites (the BBC, Daily Mail, The Guardian) that appear to carry a news story about the public figure's endorsement of the platform. The page may look authentic except on close inspection.
  • Social media account impersonation: creating fake social media profiles that closely replicate the appearance of a public figure's legitimate account, used to promote crypto schemes and link to fake investment platforms.

The ASA's rulings in this area have been clear: any advertisement that implies the endorsement, recommendation, or involvement of a public figure who has not actually provided that endorsement is misleading and breaches the CAP Code, regardless of the format. The harm is material — research consistently shows that consumers place significant weight on celebrity or authority-figure endorsements when making financial decisions, and the fraudulent use of such endorsements exploits that trust directly.

What to do if you encounter a fake endorsement advertisement

If you see an advertisement using a public figure's name, photograph, or apparent endorsement to promote a crypto investment or crypto "recovery" firm, you should:

  1. Verify the endorsement independently. Search the public figure's verified official social media accounts and official website. If they have actually endorsed a platform, it will be prominently featured on their own channels. A brief internet search combining the public figure's name and the platform's name will typically reveal whether the "endorsement" is genuine or whether others have flagged it as fabricated.
  2. Check the FCA Financial Services Register. If the advertisement promotes a crypto investment or a firm claiming to assist with crypto losses, check whether the firm appears in the FCA Financial Services Register at register.fca.org.uk or the FCA Warning List at fca.org.uk/scamsmart.
  3. Report the advertisement to the ASA. Submit a complaint at asa.org.uk/make-a-complaint. Include the URL of the advertisement, a screenshot, and a description of why you believe it is misleading. The ASA cannot respond to individual complainants about the outcome of investigation, but complaints are assessed and contribute to enforcement priorities.
  4. Report to Action Fraud. If you believe the advertisement is part of a fraud operation — rather than a technical advertising breach — report to actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040.
  5. Report to the social media platform. Most platforms have reporting mechanisms for fraudulent advertising. Use these to flag the advertisement. Under the UK Online Safety Act 2023 and its implementing obligations, platforms face increasing pressure to remove fraudulent advertisements promptly.

Unsubstantiated asset recovery claims and high-pressure tactics

Among the most serious categories of ASA enforcement activity in the 2025–2026 period is action against firms advertising on the back of crypto fraud — specifically firms claiming to recover lost cryptocurrency, trace stolen funds with guaranteed success, or deliver specific monetary outcomes for clients. This category of advertising targets people who have already suffered a primary fraud loss and are in a psychologically and financially vulnerable position. The ASA and FCA have both classified it as a priority enforcement area.

Why these advertisements are inherently problematic

An advertisement by a firm claiming to recover lost cryptocurrency faces a fundamental challenge: no firm can honestly guarantee outcomes in this space. On-chain tracing is a forensic intelligence service. It produces evidence — traceable fund flows, exchange identification, wallet attribution. Whether that evidence leads to any outcome for the victim depends on legal proceedings, law enforcement action, exchange cooperation, court orders, and the ultimate enforceability of any judgment against the fraudster. A firm that claims a specific success rate, a guaranteed recovery, or a defined timeline is either misrepresenting its services or misrepresenting what "recovery" means.

The ASA's CAP Code requires that all material claims in advertising are substantiated by evidence that the advertiser holds before publication. Specific claims — "94% of clients recover funds", "average recovery time of 21 days", "we have recovered £X million for clients" — must be backed by robust, verifiable evidence. In the absence of such evidence, these claims are misleading and will be upheld against the advertiser if investigated.

Warning signs in advertising by firms claiming to help with crypto losses

The following patterns — drawn from the types of claims that have appeared in ASA enforcement cases and FCA warning notices — should be treated as significant red flags when evaluating any firm's advertising:

  1. Specific success rate claims without evidence. Any advertisement claiming a defined percentage of cases result in recovery — "92% success rate", "most clients see full recovery" — without providing verifiable evidence of how that figure was calculated, over what period, and based on what definition of "success".
  2. Guaranteed or highly likely outcome language. Phrases such as "we will get your money back", "guaranteed recovery", "your funds are safe with us", "we can retrieve your crypto" imply certainty about an inherently uncertain process. No honest tracing firm can make these guarantees.
  3. Celebrity or authority endorsement not verifiable through official channels. Any advertisement that implies endorsement by a known public figure — whether through a named quote, a photograph, a video clip, or a "featured in" media logo — where that endorsement cannot be verified through the public figure's own official communications.
  4. Upfront fee requirements combined with outcome promises. An advance fee combined with an outcome guarantee is a classic secondary fraud pattern. A firm that charges a significant upfront fee while promising recovery is combining two red flags. Legitimate on-chain tracing engagements typically begin with a case review before any paid engagement is agreed.
  5. False regulatory status. Any advertisement claiming FCA authorisation, FCA approval, FCA registration, or FSCS protection for a service that is not an FCA-regulated financial product. On-chain tracing is not a regulated activity — firms providing it are not FCA-authorised for that activity, and the FSCS does not cover it.
  6. Countdown timers and urgency language designed to prevent research. Advertisements that use artificial time pressure — "offer expires in 47 minutes", "only 3 places remaining this month", "act now or lose your chance" — to prevent the consumer from pausing to verify the firm's credentials.
  7. Unverifiable testimonials claiming specific monetary outcomes. Testimonials that name specific amounts ("I got back £47,000"), specific timeframes ("within 3 weeks"), or that cannot be independently verified. The ASA has consistently ruled that testimonials must reflect genuine, verifiable experiences and must not mislead by omission about the typical experience of clients.
  8. Contact initiated by the firm rather than the consumer. Unsolicited approaches — via social media, email, WhatsApp, or phone — from a firm claiming it has already identified where your lost crypto is and can recover it. Legitimate tracing firms do not proactively contact potential clients with claims about identified funds.

The FCA financial promotions regime: how it sits alongside ASA rules

The ASA's enforcement activity in the crypto space does not operate in isolation. Since October 2023, the FCA's financial promotions regime for cryptoassets has established a parallel — and legally more powerful — regulatory framework governing the content and communication of crypto advertising to UK consumers. Understanding how the two regimes interact is relevant for any victim assessing whether an advertisement they saw was lawful and what remedies exist.

What the FCA financial promotions regime requires

Under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (as amended by the Financial Services and Markets Act 2023), any person who communicates an invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity — a "financial promotion" — must either be FCA-authorised themselves, or have the promotion approved by an FCA-authorised person before communicating it to UK consumers. For cryptoassets, this means:

  • Any advertisement for a crypto investment product or service targeting UK consumers must be either made by or approved by an FCA-authorised person.
  • The promotion must include a prescribed risk warning in a specific format and with defined minimum prominence: "Cryptoassets are highly volatile and unregulated in the UK. No consumer protection. Tax on profits may apply."
  • Promotions must not make misleading comparisons, must not include past performance that implies future performance, and must include a clear statement that the value of investments can go down as well as up.
  • The FCA can require non-compliant promotions to be withdrawn, can impose financial penalties on FCA-authorised persons that approve non-compliant promotions, and can bring prosecution against non-FCA-authorised persons who communicate financial promotions unlawfully.

How ASA and FCA enforcement interact in practice

The ASA and FCA operate complementary but distinct enforcement tracks. The FCA focuses on whether a firm is authorised to communicate a financial promotion and whether the promotion complies with its prescribed disclosure requirements. The ASA focuses on the truthfulness and fairness of the advertising claims themselves — whether they are misleading, whether they are substantiated, whether they exploit vulnerable consumers.

In practice, a non-compliant crypto advertisement may simultaneously breach both regimes: it may fail to include the FCA's prescribed risk warning (an FCA breach) and contain unsubstantiated outcome claims (an ASA breach). Both regulators can act, and the FCA's actions — which can include injunctions, financial penalties, and criminal prosecution — tend to carry more immediate consequence for the advertiser than an ASA ruling alone.

For fraud victims, the FCA's financial promotions database of approved promotions — and its record of firms that have communicated unapproved promotions — is a useful supplementary check alongside the ASA rulings database. A firm that has communicated an unapproved financial promotion may appear in FCA enforcement records even if it has not been the subject of a specific ASA ruling.

Check before engaging: Before engaging any firm claiming to help with crypto losses, run the firm's name against the FCA Warning List at fca.org.uk/scamsmart and the FCA Financial Services Register at register.fca.org.uk. Absence from the Warning List does not confirm legitimacy — new fraudulent firms are added continuously — but presence on it is a definitive disqualifier.

What tighter advertising rules mean for fraud victims

For someone who has already lost money to a crypto scam, the ASA's enforcement activity and the FCA's financial promotions regime matter in several practical ways. Tighter advertising rules are not just an abstract regulatory development — they have direct consequences for the landscape of risk that fraud victims navigate when they begin researching their options.

Fewer misleading advertisements in circulation

The most direct consequence of sustained enforcement activity is that misleading advertisements are removed from circulation. Each ASA ruling that requires a non-compliant advertisement to be withdrawn, each FCA action that results in an unapproved promotion being pulled, and each digital platform enforcement action that removes a fraudulent paid advertisement represents one fewer channel through which scammers and secondary fraud operators can recruit new victims.

This matters particularly for secondary fraud — the category of scam targeting people who have already lost money and are actively searching for help. The search terms that fraud victims use ("how to recover lost crypto", "crypto tracing firm uk", "get money back after crypto scam") are precisely the terms that fraudulent recovery operators have historically targeted with misleading paid advertising. As ASA and FCA enforcement of this advertising category intensifies, the concentration of fraudulent operators in these paid search results should reduce.

A public due-diligence tool

The ASA's rulings database provides fraud victims with a searchable, public record of firms that have been found to have advertised misleadingly. This is a genuine due-diligence resource that costs nothing and takes a few minutes to use. Before engaging any firm claiming to help with crypto losses, searching that firm's name in the ASA database and filtering for upheld rulings takes approximately two minutes. A firm with multiple upheld rulings relating to recovery claims, outcome guarantees, or false regulatory status presents an evidence-based reason to look elsewhere.

A standard for what honest advertising looks like

ASA enforcement activity — by defining what non-compliant advertising looks like — simultaneously defines, by contrast, what compliant advertising looks like. A firm that does not make outcome guarantees, does not use fake celebrity endorsements, does not claim false regulatory status, does not use high-pressure urgency tactics, and substantiates any specific claims with verifiable evidence is operating within the code. Victims can use this standard as a positive indicator when assessing firms, not just as a way of flagging red flags.

Realism about what enforcement cannot do

Enforcement has limits that victims should understand. The ASA's rulings apply to advertisements it has investigated — not to all advertising in the market. A new fraudulent firm that has not yet been investigated will not appear in the database. The ASA investigates on the basis of complaints and its own proactive monitoring, but the volume of non-compliant advertising in the crypto space means that enforcement is selective. The absence of an ASA ruling against a firm is not, by itself, evidence that the firm is trustworthy.

Similarly, fraudulent operators frequently create new legal entities and new advertising accounts after enforcement action against a previous entity. The ASA's enforcement is against the specific advertiser name and advertisement — not automatically against all entities associated with the same individuals. This is why cross-referencing the ASA database with the FCA Warning List, the Companies House register, and an independent web search remains the appropriate multi-source due-diligence approach.

How to vet any firm claiming to help with crypto losses

The following checklist draws on the ASA's enforcement priorities, the FCA's guidance on verifying firms, and the practical red flags that distinguish fraudulent secondary operators from firms operating honestly in this space. Run through these checks before engaging any firm — including CountWize.

  1. Check the FCA Financial Services Register. Visit register.fca.org.uk and search the firm's name. On-chain tracing is not a regulated activity, so a tracing firm will not appear in the FCA Register as authorised for this activity — but any firm claiming to be FCA-authorised or FCA-registered for services it provides should appear in the Register. If a firm claims FCA authorisation but cannot provide a firm reference number that appears in the Register, the claim is false.
  2. Check the FCA Warning List. Visit fca.org.uk/scamsmart and search the firm's name. If the firm or its web address appears on the Warning List, do not engage under any circumstances and report it to Action Fraud.
  3. Search the ASA rulings database. Visit asa.org.uk/rulings and search the firm's name. Filter for "Upheld" results. Multiple upheld rulings, particularly relating to recovery claims, outcome guarantees, celebrity endorsements, or false regulatory status, are significant red flags.
  4. Verify the firm on Companies House. Every UK-registered company is searchable at find-and-update.company-information.service.gov.uk. Check that the company name and registration number match what the firm claims, that the company is active (not dissolved or in administration), and that the registered address is a real address rather than a virtual office or mailbox address with no physical presence.
  5. Verify any celebrity or authority endorsement independently. If an advertisement uses the name, photograph, or apparent endorsement of a public figure, verify through the public figure's own official channels — their verified social media accounts, official website, or press office. Do not accept the advertisement itself as evidence of the endorsement. A one-minute search will typically reveal whether an endorsement is genuine or fabricated.
  6. Look for honest limits disclosure. A firm operating honestly in this space will tell you clearly who it cannot help and what it does not guarantee. If a firm's website contains only positive framing — success stories, outcome promises, testimonials — with no clear statement of limitations, this is a warning signal. Fraudulent operators avoid honest limits disclosure because it conflicts with the outcome promises that attract victims.
  7. Assess whether the firm initiated contact or you found them. Unsolicited approaches — particularly those claiming that the firm has already identified where your funds are or has a specific opportunity to assist in your particular case — are a classic secondary fraud pattern. Legitimate tracing firms do not cold-contact potential clients with such claims.
  8. Check what fee structure is proposed before any work begins. A significant upfront fee before any tracing work has been described or initiated, combined with an outcome promise, is a high-risk combination. While tracing services are not free — they require skilled analyst time — the commercial structure should be explained clearly before you commit to any payment. Ask for this in writing.

Frequently asked questions

What is the ASA and what powers does it have over crypto advertising?

The Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) is the UK's independent advertising regulator. It enforces the CAP Code for online and print advertising and the BCAP Code for broadcast advertising. The ASA can require advertisers to withdraw non-compliant ads, publish rulings on its website (searchable by company name), and refer persistent offenders to Trading Standards or the FCA. It does not fine advertisers directly, but its public rulings create significant reputational consequences and persistent non-compliance can trigger prosecution. For fraud victims, the ASA rulings database is a free due-diligence tool to check whether a firm has a history of misleading advertising.

What types of crypto advertising has the ASA ruled against in 2025 and 2026?

The ASA's 2025–2026 enforcement activity has focused primarily on: fake celebrity endorsements using deepfakes or fabricated quotes; unsubstantiated asset recovery claims by firms targeting fraud victims; crypto investment advertisements missing or burying required FCA risk disclosures; high-pressure tactics including countdown timers and false scarcity claims; false regulatory status claims (particularly "FCA-regulated" by non-authorised firms); and misleading testimonials that imply specific monetary outcomes without verifiable basis. These categories map directly onto the tactics used by both primary crypto scammers and secondary fraud operators.

How do ASA enforcement actions help fraud victims?

ASA enforcement helps fraud victims by removing misleading advertisements from circulation, creating a public searchable record of firms that have breached advertising codes, establishing a standard for what honest compliant advertising looks like, and supporting FCA and Trading Standards referrals for persistent offenders. For victims specifically looking for help after a primary fraud, enforcement reduces the concentration of fraudulent "recovery" advertising in the search results where victims are most likely to encounter it.

How do I check whether a firm has an ASA ruling against it?

Visit asa.org.uk/rulings. Enter the firm's exact name (and variations of it) in the search field and filter by "Upheld" to see cases where the ASA found advertising breaches. Recent rulings about recovery claims, outcome guarantees, celebrity endorsements, or false regulatory status are the most significant red flags for firms claiming to help with crypto losses. This search costs nothing and takes under two minutes.

Does the ASA's jurisdiction cover all crypto advertising targeting UK consumers?

The ASA has jurisdiction over advertising appearing in the UK through its regulated channels — online advertising, paid search, social media, UK print, and broadcast. It does not automatically have jurisdiction over advertising placed by offshore firms on non-UK platforms, though it works with digital platforms to have non-compliant paid advertising removed. The FCA's financial promotions regime — which applies to any crypto financial promotion communicated to UK consumers regardless of the advertiser's location — may be the more relevant enforcement pathway for offshore advertising targeting UK consumers. Both regulators have active programmes in this area.
