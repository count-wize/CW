The APP scheme: a brief recap of the October 2024 mandate

In October 2024, the Payment Systems Regulator (PSR) brought into force its mandatory Authorised Push Payment (APP) fraud reimbursement requirement. For the first time in UK banking history, the obligation to reimburse victims of APP fraud became legally binding rather than voluntary. The previous framework — the Contingent Reimbursement Model (CRM) code, signed up to by most major banks on a voluntary basis — had produced inconsistent results. Reimbursement rates varied widely across banks, and consumers were routinely denied on narrow interpretations of the code's exceptions.

The PSR scheme fundamentally reversed the burden. Under the new rules, reimbursement is the default. Banks can only decline to reimburse if they can demonstrate that a defined exception applies — most importantly, the consumer standard of caution, which allows a bank to reduce or refuse a claim if the victim ignored clear, unambiguous warnings at the point of payment. The scheme applies to payments made through Faster Payments and CHAPS, which together cover the vast majority of UK bank-to-bank transfers, covering personal customers and micro-enterprises (businesses with fewer than 10 employees and a balance sheet below €2 million).

The cap controversy: £85,000, not £415,000

The scheme that came into force was notably different from the version originally proposed. The PSR had initially consulted on a maximum reimbursement cap of £415,000 per claim — set at the Financial Services Compensation Scheme (FSCS) deposit protection limit in force at the time of the original consultation. However, following sustained lobbying from payment service providers and banks, who argued the higher cap would impose unsustainable costs and could create moral hazard, the PSR reduced the cap to £85,000 before the scheme's launch.

This reduction was controversial. Consumer groups, including Which? and the Financial Services Consumer Panel, argued that the £85,000 cap left the highest-value victims — often those targeted by investment fraud — without adequate protection for losses in the six-figure range. High-value crypto investment frauds, in which victims have lost £200,000 to £500,000 to sophisticated fake trading platforms, are precisely the cases where the gap between the £85,000 cap and the actual loss is most significant. The PSR committed to reviewing the cap as part of its assessment of the scheme's first year.

Cost-sharing and the 50/50 split

A distinctive structural feature of the PSR scheme is the mandatory 50/50 cost-sharing between the sending bank (where the victim holds their account) and the receiving bank (where the fraudster's account was held, whether a UK bank or payment institution). This was intended to create an incentive for receiving institutions — historically under no financial obligation when fraud proceeds flowed into accounts on their platform — to implement stronger controls to prevent their accounts being used for fraud. The sending bank manages the reimbursement claim on behalf of the victim and reclaims 50% from the receiving institution through the Pay.UK scheme mechanism. Understanding this structure matters because it explains why the receiving bank's identity and the nature of the receiving account are relevant to the PSR's published data on the scheme.

How the scheme has been applied in practice: 12 months of evidence

The first year of mandatory APP reimbursement has produced a body of evidence — through Financial Ombudsman Service (FOS) decisions, PSR transparency reporting commitments, and published industry data from UK Finance — that allows an informed assessment of how the scheme is working in practice. The picture is genuinely mixed, with improvements in some fraud categories and persistent difficulties in others, particularly those involving cryptocurrency.

Where the scheme has worked well

In classic APP fraud scenarios — impersonation fraud where someone pretends to be a bank, government agency, or law enforcement, and romance fraud where an online relationship leads to transfer requests — reimbursement rates have improved materially relative to the CRM code period. The combination of mandatory obligation, defined exceptions, and the FOS as a backstop has made banks less able to rely on broad, vague refusals. Victims with clear impersonation narratives and minimal or no bank warnings in the payment journey have generally fared well under the new scheme.

The sending-bank refusal rate for in-scope APP fraud claims fell during the first year compared to comparable periods under the CRM code, according to UK Finance data. However, it is important not to over-read this improvement: a lower refusal rate on in-scope claims does not tell us how many claims were not submitted in the first place, or how many claims were characterised by banks as falling outside the scheme's scope before any formal claim was registered.

Where the scheme has struggled

Three categories of claim have produced disproportionate friction in the first year:

  • Crypto investment fraud: cases where the victim transferred GBP to a crypto exchange (or directly to a fraudster's account for the stated purpose of buying cryptocurrency) as part of an investment fraud. Banks have frequently characterised these as "buying crypto" decisions by the consumer rather than APP fraud, applying both the civil-dispute exception and the consumer standard of caution. The boundary between a deceived victim and a voluntary investor who made a bad decision is genuinely difficult to draw in some cases, and banks have used this ambiguity to their advantage.
  • Multi-payment fraud: cases where the victim made many separate payments over weeks or months — typical of pig-butchering investment fraud, where victims are drawn in gradually and make increasing deposits. Banks have argued that the pattern of multiple payments, each preceded by (in some cases) warning messages, demonstrates either consumer awareness of risk or gross negligence. The PSR scheme does not exclude multi-payment fraud, but its application is more complex when each individual payment occurred in a context where warning had been given.
  • High-value claims above the £85,000 cap: victims with losses significantly above £85,000 face the challenge that their bank claim covers only a fraction of their loss. The remainder requires civil litigation or other routes. The practical and financial barriers to civil litigation mean that many victims above the cap effectively have their loss capped by the PSR scheme even if they deserve full reimbursement in principle.

The FOS and its role as the backstop

The Financial Ombudsman Service has become significantly more active in APP fraud cases since October 2024. The FOS reported a substantial increase in APP fraud complaint referrals — including a notable proportion involving crypto — in the six months following the scheme's launch. The FOS operates as the mandatory backstop: if a bank refuses an APP fraud claim (or fails to respond within the required timeframes), the victim has the right to refer the matter to the FOS free of charge, and the FOS has the power to direct the bank to reimburse. The FOS's published decisions in crypto-related cases have progressively clarified — though not definitively resolved — the scope questions discussed in section 3 below.

Critically, the FOS retains its pre-existing approach of making decisions based on what is fair and reasonable in all the circumstances, not merely on whether the bank technically complied with PSR rules. This means that a bank's argument that technically satisfies the consumer standard of caution exception may still fail at the FOS if the FOS concludes the refusal was not fair and reasonable given the specific vulnerability of the victim, the nature of the fraud, or the quality of the warning provided.

The crypto scope boundary: what is and is not covered

The single most contested question in the scheme's first year — from a crypto-fraud victim's perspective — is where the boundary sits between APP fraud claims the PSR scheme covers and crypto losses it does not. Understanding this boundary precisely is essential for any victim navigating the scheme.

What the scheme unambiguously covers

The APP scheme covers a payment you made through Faster Payments or CHAPS from a UK bank account, where you were deceived into making that payment. If you transferred GBP from your bank account to an account controlled by a fraudster — whether that account was at a UK bank, a UK e-money institution, or a crypto exchange operating a GBP current account — and you were deceived into doing so, the GBP transfer is in principle within scope. The scheme covers the GBP payment system leg of the transaction, not any on-chain activity that follows.

What the scheme does not cover

The scheme does not cover:

  • Direct on-chain cryptocurrency transfers (wallet-to-wallet transactions). These are not Faster Payments or CHAPS payments. No UK banking regulator has jurisdiction over on-chain transactions under the PSR framework.
  • Payments made by debit or credit card to a crypto exchange — these may be subject to separate chargeback rights under the card scheme rules (section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974 for credit card purchases), but they are not within the PSR APP scheme.
  • Losses sustained within a crypto exchange account after you funded it — for example, if the exchange itself was fraudulent or was hacked, the exchange account balance is not a GBP Faster Payments matter even if the original funding used Faster Payments.
  • International wire transfers in any currency — SWIFT and SEPA payments are not within the PSR's payment systems jurisdiction.

The contested middle ground: "buying crypto" versus fraud

Between the clearly in-scope and clearly out-of-scope scenarios lies a contested middle ground that has produced the most litigation in the scheme's first year. This middle ground typically arises when:

  1. The victim transferred GBP via Faster Payments to a legitimate, regulated crypto exchange (such as Coinbase, Kraken, Binance, or Gemini) as part of an investment fraud scheme.
  2. The bank argues that the transfer was a voluntary decision to buy cryptocurrency — a consumer investment decision — rather than an APP fraud payment where the victim was deceived into authorising a payment to a fraudster.
  3. The victim argues that the payment was APP fraud because they were deceived by fraudsters who controlled a fake trading platform, and the transfer to the legitimate exchange was just the first step in that deception.

The difficulty is that both characterisations can be factually accurate at the same time. The victim did voluntarily authorise a transfer to a real exchange; they were also deceived by fraudsters. The legal question is whether the deception was sufficient to bring the payment within the APP fraud definition — whether the victim's "authorisation" was truly informed or was the product of fraud that negated genuine consent.

Key distinction: The PSR scheme is most likely to apply where the victim was deceived by fraudsters before or at the time of the bank transfer — for example, being told their funds were going to a guaranteed investment account rather than to buy open-market cryptocurrency. It is less likely to apply where the victim understood they were buying cryptocurrency but was deceived about the platform's legitimacy after the funds arrived at the exchange.

Exchange-as-destination versus exchange-as-intermediary

The FOS has in several unpublished and some published decisions begun to draw a distinction between two scenarios:

Scenario Characterisation Likely PSR outcome
Victim transfers GBP to a fraudster's bank account (at a UK bank), fraudster then buys crypto on an exchange using those funds Classic APP fraud — the deception was complete at the point of the bank transfer In scope — victim was deceived into authorising a transfer to a fraudster
Victim transfers GBP to a real exchange to buy crypto, then sends that crypto to a fraudster's wallet because the fraudster runs a fake investment platform Mixed — the bank transfer was arguably voluntary (buying crypto); the fraud occurred on-platform Contested — bank likely to argue "buying crypto" exclusion; FOS outcome depends on whether the victim was deceived at the point of the bank transfer or after
Victim sends crypto directly from their wallet to a fraudster's wallet (no GBP bank transfer) No Faster Payments leg — purely on-chain Out of scope — not a regulated payment system transaction

In the middle scenario — which accounts for a large proportion of investment fraud involving crypto — the critical evidentiary question is: what did the victim believe they were doing when they made the bank transfer? If the victim was told by fraudsters that they were investing in a managed fund or trading account, and believed the bank transfer was going to an investment manager rather than to buy open-market crypto, there is a stronger argument that the fraud was complete at the point of the bank transfer. If the victim knew they were buying cryptocurrency on an exchange and made an informed purchase decision — but was then deceived about the investment opportunity that followed — the APP fraud argument is weaker.

Grey areas: contested claims and how the FOS has responded

The Financial Ombudsman Service's role in crypto-related APP fraud cases has expanded considerably in the scheme's first year. While the FOS does not publish a comprehensive dataset of its crypto decisions, its published decision summaries and its guidance updates provide meaningful insight into how it is interpreting the scheme's scope in contested cases.

The consumer standard of caution in crypto cases

The consumer standard of caution is the most frequently litigated exception in crypto-related APP fraud claims. The core issue is whether a bank's payment-journey warnings were sufficiently specific to satisfy the "clear and unambiguous" threshold that the PSR requires to permit a bank to reduce or refuse reimbursement.

The FOS has in its published guidance emphasised that the quality and specificity of warnings matter. A generic message — "Protect yourself from fraud: have you verified the payee?" displayed during a standard payment flow — is unlikely on its own to satisfy the standard if it appeared as routine friction rather than a targeted, specific investment-fraud warning. Contrast this with a live call from the bank's fraud team specifically warning the customer that the payment pattern is consistent with investment fraud and urging them not to proceed: the FOS has generally treated this as satisfying the standard, and a customer who proceeds in the face of such a call has more difficulty arguing they should be fully reimbursed.

The difficult cases in the first year have involved banks that deployed automated, high-friction warning overlays specifically for transfers to crypto exchanges — some displaying text such as "Transfers to crypto exchanges are often used in fraud. Are you sure you want to proceed?" The FOS has not issued definitive guidance on whether exchange-specific warnings of this type satisfy the consumer standard of caution, and individual decisions have gone both ways depending on the facts. This remains one of the most contested areas of the scheme's application.

Vulnerability and reduced consumer standard

Under the PSR rules and Consumer Duty principles, banks must apply a lower standard of caution for customers they know — or should know — to be vulnerable. This has become increasingly relevant in crypto-fraud cases involving elderly victims, victims with identified cognitive difficulties, or victims under documented emotional stress who were targeted by romance fraud overlapping with investment fraud. The FOS has in several decisions found that banks failed to identify obvious vulnerability indicators in the customer's account history and applied the full consumer standard of caution when a reduced standard was appropriate. Victims who were in a state of clear emotional vulnerability at the time of the fraud — or who had other indicators of vulnerability visible to the bank — should ensure this is prominently raised in any FOS referral.

Multi-payment cases: is each payment assessed separately?

A question that has arisen repeatedly in pig-butchering cases — where victims make many incremental payments over a period of weeks or months — is whether each payment should be assessed individually against the consumer standard of caution, or whether the bank should apply an overall assessment of whether the cumulative pattern triggered an obligation to intervene. The FOS has in some decisions found that banks had an obligation to apply enhanced monitoring and friction where a customer's payment pattern was anomalous — making large transfers to a crypto exchange for the first time, or making multiple high-value transfers in rapid succession — and that failure to do so was a breach of the bank's obligations even before the consumer standard of caution question arose. This is a developing area of the FOS's approach and could become increasingly important for high-value multi-payment cases.

Practical guidance for victims navigating the APP scheme after a crypto-related fraud

Drawing on the first year's evidence, the following practical guidance reflects what is known about how claims are being assessed and how victims can strengthen their position. This is educational information; it is not legal advice, and specific cases should be assessed with a solicitor or Citizens Advice if the financial stakes justify it.

  1. Identify and document the GBP bank transfer leg. The PSR scheme covers the bank transfer, not the on-chain transaction. Your first task is to identify every Faster Payments or CHAPS transfer you made from your UK bank account as part of the fraud — the date, amount, destination account name and sort code, and what you were told the transfer was for. This is the core of your APP fraud claim. If you transferred GBP to a crypto exchange, document your understanding at the time of what that transfer was for — were you told you were investing in a managed fund? Were you told you were funding your own exchange account? The specific understanding you had at the point of the bank transfer is central to the "deception at the time of payment" question.
  2. Report to your bank immediately and ask specifically for the PSR APP fraud scheme. Contact your bank's fraud team by phone and follow up in writing. Use the exact phrase "Authorised Push Payment fraud" and ask to make a claim under the PSR mandatory reimbursement scheme. This matters because it creates a clear record that you made a formal claim under the statutory framework, not merely a complaint. Request confirmation in writing of the claim reference number, the date the claim was registered, and the expected decision timeline (5 business days for standard cases, 35 for complex). Banks cannot ignore a formally registered PSR APP fraud claim without issuing a decision or requesting a time extension.
  3. Address the consumer standard of caution directly in your claim. If your bank displayed any warnings during the payment journey — including automated overlays, text messages, or phone calls — do not ignore this in your claim. Address it head-on: describe what the warning said, what you understood it to mean, and why it did not alert you to the specific fraud you were the victim of. The distinction between a generic fraud-protection message and a targeted, unambiguous warning is legally significant. If the bank's warning was generic, say so explicitly and cite this as the reason it did not meet the consumer standard of caution threshold.
  4. Raise vulnerability if it applies. If there are factors in your circumstances that made you particularly susceptible to the fraud — emotional distress, bereavement, financial pressure, cognitive difficulty, an established online relationship that had developed over months — include this information in your claim. If these factors were known to your bank (for example, you had previously disclosed a bereavement or health condition, or your account activity made your emotional state apparent), argue that the bank should have applied a reduced consumer standard of caution.
  5. If refused, escalate to the FOS within 6 months. A bank refusal is not final. Request the bank's formal deadlock letter and submit a referral to the Financial Ombudsman Service at financial-ombudsman.org.uk. The FOS adjudication is free and is the most effective single step available to victims who have been refused. The 6-month deadline from the bank's final response is strict — do not let it pass.

What on-chain evidence adds to a bank claim

For victims who transferred GBP to a real exchange as part of an investment fraud, an on-chain intelligence report documenting where the cryptocurrency moved immediately after purchase can materially support the fraud narrative. If the on-chain trace shows that funds moved from the exchange wallet immediately and systematically through a chain of addresses — consistent with fraud operations rather than a genuine investment account — this adds evidential weight to the argument that the victim was deceived into making the bank transfer. An on-chain report can be included in the evidence bundle submitted with the bank claim and, if escalated, with the FOS referral.

Regulatory developments since October 2024

The 12 months since the scheme's launch have not been static from a regulatory perspective. Several developments are directly relevant to crypto-fraud victims and to the scheme's future operation.

PSR review of the £85,000 cap

The Payment Systems Regulator opened a formal review of the reimbursement cap in early 2025. The review was prompted by sustained pressure from consumer groups, particularly in cases where investment fraud losses significantly exceeded the cap. The PSR's stated position was that the cap would be reassessed against evidence of how the scheme was operating in practice. Consumer groups including Which? submitted evidence arguing for a substantial increase, pointing to the high proportion of investment fraud cases involving losses in the £100,000–£500,000 range — a profile that matches many crypto-related cases. As of the one-year mark, no formal increase had been implemented, but the PSR had indicated that a consultation on the cap was expected in 2026. Any increase to the cap would be directly relevant to high-value crypto investment fraud victims.

FCA CP25/41: cryptoasset regime and its indirect relevance

In parallel with the PSR's APP reimbursement framework, the Financial Conduct Authority published Consultation Paper CP25/41 in 2025, setting out its proposed regulatory framework for cryptoasset activities. While CP25/41 does not directly create APP fraud reimbursement rights, it has indirect relevance to crypto-fraud victims in several ways:

  • Travel Rule compliance requirements: CP25/41 proposes extending and strengthening the Travel Rule for crypto transactions, requiring UK-regulated crypto firms to obtain and retain originator and beneficiary information for crypto transfers. Stronger Travel Rule compliance makes on-chain attribution — identifying who controls a wallet address — more tractable, which improves the evidence base available for both bank claims and civil litigation. See our companion guide on FCA CP25/41 for a detailed breakdown.
  • Consumer Duty extension to crypto: the FCA's Consumer Duty, which applies to UK-regulated financial services firms, would under CP25/41 extend to UK-authorised crypto firms. This would impose positive obligations on those firms — including obligations to identify and protect vulnerable customers and to handle complaints fairly. For victims whose losses occurred partly through a UK-regulated exchange, this creates a parallel complaint route to any bank claim.
  • FOS jurisdiction over crypto firms: the FCA's published position on the extension of FOS jurisdiction to FCA-authorised crypto firms is relevant to victims who used a UK-regulated exchange as part of their fraud journey. If the exchange failed in its Consumer Duty or Financial Promotion obligations in the way it dealt with the victim, there may be a separate FOS complaint route against the exchange. This is distinct from the PSR APP claim against the bank.

Timeline of key developments

  • October 2024 PSR mandatory APP reimbursement scheme comes into force. Maximum cap set at £85,000. 50/50 cost-sharing between sending and receiving banks begins.
  • November 2024 Pay.UK publishes first operator guidance on the scheme's claims process and the sending-bank reimbursement portal for cost recovery from receiving banks.
  • December 2024 – January 2025 First wave of FOS referrals under the new scheme. The FOS begins publishing a small number of non-binding decision summaries on crypto-related APP fraud cases.
  • February 2025 PSR announces formal review of the £85,000 cap. Consumer groups submit evidence calling for an increase to at least £150,000.
  • Spring 2025 FCA publishes CP25/41 — consultation on the UK cryptoasset regulatory framework. 90-day consultation period opens. See our CP25/41 commentary for a plain-English breakdown.
  • Summer 2025 UK Finance publishes first H1 2025 fraud report. APP fraud volumes remain high; crypto-related investment fraud cited as a leading sub-category. Reimbursement rates by fraud type begin to emerge in public reporting.
  • Autumn 2025 FOS publishes updated guidance on APP fraud decisions involving crypto exchanges, providing initial clarity on the consumer standard of caution in exchange-destination payment cases.
  • Early 2026 PSR publishes transparency data on reimbursement rates by bank under the mandatory scheme — the first systematic evidence of variation in how individual banks are applying the scheme. Some banks are shown to have materially lower reimbursement rates than others in comparable fraud categories.
  • April 2026 One-year mark. PSR announces a consultation on the cap — expected to be published in summer 2026. FCA CP25/41 consultation analysis underway.

The PSR transparency reporting obligation

One of the less-discussed structural features of the PSR mandatory scheme is the obligation on banks to publish data on their reimbursement performance. The PSR requires that banks report reimbursement rates, volumes of claims, and — in aggregated form — the distribution of outcomes. The first publication of this data, in early 2026, provided consumer groups and advisers with something that had not previously been available: a direct comparison of how consistently different banks are applying the scheme. The data indicated meaningful variation — some banks with notably lower reimbursement rates in investment fraud categories than the sector average. This information is relevant to victims deciding whether to accept a bank's decision or refer to the FOS: if a bank's published reimbursement rate is significantly below peers for the same fraud type, that is evidence that the bank may be applying the exceptions too broadly.

Where on-chain tracing fits alongside a bank claim

On-chain tracing and the PSR APP reimbursement scheme are separate processes operating on different parts of the same problem. After 12 months of the scheme, the relationship between these two tools is better understood than it was at launch.

What the bank claim does not do

A successful APP fraud bank claim reimburses the GBP that was transferred from your bank account. It does not document where that money went after it left the bank system, identify the individuals behind the fraud operation, produce evidence for law enforcement, or support any civil legal action beyond the bank reimbursement itself. A bank claim result — whether successful or refused — does not advance a victim's position with Action Fraud, the police, or a solicitor unless it is supplemented by evidence of what happened to the money.

What on-chain tracing does that a bank claim does not

An on-chain intelligence report produced by CountWize traces the movement of cryptocurrency from the point it was received into a fraudster-controlled wallet — through intermediate addresses, consolidation wallets, exchange deposits, and any obfuscation techniques used — documenting the transaction trail with blockchain transaction IDs, timestamps, wallet attribution, and exchange identification where possible. This produces evidence that:

  • Supports a police report by demonstrating the fraud narrative at a transaction level — showing that funds moved consistently with fraud operations rather than a legitimate investment.
  • Supports a Norwich Pharmacal Order application if the on-chain trace identifies a destination exchange holding KYC records — allowing a solicitor to apply to compel the exchange to disclose the identity of the account holder.
  • Provides evidence for the FOS referral by demonstrating the mechanics of the fraud and the immediate post-transfer movement of funds, which can be material to the "deception at the point of payment" question.
  • Creates a documented evidentiary record even in cases where no immediate legal route is viable — relevant if the regulatory or enforcement environment changes, or if new legal routes emerge from the FCA's CP25/41 framework.

Frequently asked questions

Has the PSR APP reimbursement scheme increased the amount crypto-fraud victims receive from banks?

The picture after 12 months is mixed. For victims where the GBP bank transfer leg is clearly in scope — particularly impersonation and romance fraud — reimbursement rates are meaningfully higher than under the old voluntary CRM code. However, crypto-related APP fraud cases remain significantly more contested. Banks frequently apply the "buying crypto" characterisation or the consumer standard of caution defence in cases where the victim transferred money to a crypto exchange. The Financial Ombudsman Service has seen an increase in crypto-related APP fraud referrals, suggesting that bank refusals remain common and victims are increasingly aware of their right to escalate.

Was the PSR's £85,000 cap changed in the first year?

The original proposed cap of £415,000 was already reduced to £85,000 before the scheme launched in October 2024. In the first year, the PSR opened a formal review of the cap but had not implemented a formal increase as of April 2026. A consultation on the cap was expected to be published in summer 2026. Any increase would be directly relevant to high-value crypto investment fraud victims, whose losses frequently exceed the current £85,000 limit.

Does the APP scheme now cover crypto wallet-to-wallet transfers?

No. After 12 months, the scheme still applies only to payments made through regulated UK payment systems — Faster Payments and CHAPS. Direct on-chain transactions remain outside its scope. No regulatory change in the scheme's first year extended the mandatory reimbursement framework to blockchain transactions. This boundary is unlikely to shift through the PSR framework — it would require separate primary legislation or a new regulatory framework for cryptoasset transactions.

What is the 'consumer standard of caution' and how has it been used by banks?

The consumer standard of caution is the principal exception allowing banks to reduce or refuse reimbursement if the victim did not take heed of clear, unambiguous warnings issued at the point of payment. In the first year, banks have cited this exception heavily in crypto-related claims where payments were preceded by automated warnings, confirmation-of-payee mismatches, or live fraud-team calls. The FOS has in some decisions found that generic warnings were insufficient to satisfy the threshold, while specific investment-fraud warnings were sufficient. This remains the most contested area of the scheme's application in crypto cases.

How does on-chain tracing relate to an APP fraud bank claim?

On-chain tracing documents where cryptocurrency moved after it left the exchange — which is separate from the bank's obligation to reimburse the GBP bank transfer. An on-chain intelligence report can strengthen the overall evidence base by demonstrating the fraud narrative at a transaction level, supporting both the bank claim and any parallel police report or civil legal action. What PSR or FCA developments should crypto-fraud victims watch for in 2026?

Key developments to monitor include: the PSR's summer 2026 consultation on the £85,000 cap; the PSR's transparency data publication showing reimbursement rates by bank; FCA finalisation of the CP25/41 cryptoasset regime (including Consumer Duty extension to crypto firms and Travel Rule strengthening); and ongoing FOS published decisions that are progressively clarifying the crypto scope boundary. The PSR's transparency data in particular may reveal which banks are applying the exceptions most broadly, which is directly relevant to deciding whether to accept a bank refusal or escalate to the FOS.
