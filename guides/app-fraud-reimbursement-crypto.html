APP fraud and crypto — what the October 2024 reimbursement rules mean for UK victims

What is APP fraud?

Authorised Push Payment fraud — universally abbreviated to APP fraud — is the term used when a victim is tricked or coerced into authorising a bank transfer to an account controlled by a fraudster. The word "authorised" is the critical distinction: unlike card fraud or account takeover, where a criminal accesses a victim's account and moves money without permission, in APP fraud the victim genuinely approves the payment themselves. The bank's systems see a legitimate instruction from a legitimate customer and process it accordingly.

This distinction had significant legal consequences before October 2024. Under traditional banking law, if a customer authorises a payment, the bank has discharged its obligation by executing the instruction correctly — it was not liable for the customer being deceived into giving that instruction. Victims were often left with nothing, or with the slim prospect of a goodwill payment under the industry's voluntary Contingent Reimbursement Model (CRM) code, which banks applied inconsistently and interpreted narrowly.

How APP fraud happens in practice

APP fraud encompasses a wide range of deception scenarios. The common thread is that the victim believes they are sending money to a legitimate recipient. Common scenarios in the UK include:

  • Investment fraud: the victim is persuaded to transfer funds to what appears to be a legitimate investment platform — often a fake cryptocurrency trading platform or a fraudulent forex broker.
  • Impersonation fraud: the victim receives a call or message from someone purporting to be their bank, HMRC, the police, or a government agency. The fraudster warns of compromised accounts or outstanding tax debts and directs the victim to move money to a "safe account."
  • Romance fraud: an online relationship is developed over weeks or months, after which the fraudster requests money transfers for emergencies, travel costs, or investment opportunities.
  • Purchase fraud: the victim pays for goods or services — often through an online marketplace or social media listing — that are never delivered.
  • CEO or invoice fraud: a business is deceived into paying an invoice to a fraudulent account, often after receiving a spoofed email from a supplier or senior employee.

In all of these scenarios, the victim genuinely pressed "confirm" on a legitimate payment. That is the heart of why APP fraud protection required specific regulatory intervention rather than relying on existing fraud protections designed for unauthorised transactions.

The scale of the problem in the UK

UK Finance data, cited by the Payment Systems Regulator in its consultation materials for the 2024 scheme, documented hundreds of millions of pounds lost to APP fraud by UK consumers every year between 2020 and 2024. The figure includes both personal and business victims. Crypto-related APP fraud — where the victim was induced to transfer GBP to purchase cryptocurrency as part of a fraudulent investment scheme — has been one of the fastest-growing sub-categories, tracking the growth of retail cryptocurrency adoption and the proliferation of fake crypto trading platforms documented by the FCA and Action Fraud.

Importantly, UK Finance's published figures are likely to be underestimates. Many victims do not report APP fraud because they feel ashamed, believe nothing can be done, or are unaware of the reporting mechanisms available to them. If you have been a victim of APP fraud, reporting to Action Fraud and to your bank as quickly as possible remains the single most important step regardless of any subsequent reimbursement application.

The October 2024 PSR reimbursement scheme — what changed

The Payment Systems Regulator (PSR) is the UK's regulator for payment systems — the infrastructure that underpins bank transfers, including Faster Payments and CHAPS. In October 2024, the PSR's mandatory APP fraud reimbursement requirement came into force, fundamentally changing the legal position of UK bank customers who fall victim to APP fraud.

The old position: the voluntary CRM code

Before October 2024, consumer protection against APP fraud was governed by the Contingent Reimbursement Model (CRM) code — a voluntary industry code to which most major UK banks were signatories. The CRM code required banks to reimburse victims in certain circumstances, but its application was widely criticised as inconsistent. Banks interpreted the code narrowly, frequently relying on exceptions — particularly the "consumer standard of caution" — to deny claims. Reimbursement rates varied significantly between banks, and many victims received nothing even in cases where the fraud appeared clear-cut.

What the PSR scheme introduced

The PSR's mandatory scheme replaced the CRM code with a legally binding reimbursement obligation. The key features are:

Feature Detail
Maximum reimbursement £85,000 per claim (set at the FSCS deposit protection limit level)
Payment systems covered Faster Payments and CHAPS only — these are the regulated systems over which the PSR has jurisdiction
Who pays Cost is shared 50/50 between the sending bank (where the victim holds their account) and the receiving bank (where the fraudster's account is held)
Mandatory basis Reimbursement is required unless a specific exception applies — this is a reversal of the CRM code, under which refusal was common
Timeframe Banks must resolve claims within 5 business days in most cases (35 business days for complex cases)
Excess Banks can apply a maximum excess of £100 — they cannot charge more than this even for large claims

The £85,000 cap means that claims above this level are only partially covered under the scheme — the victim would need to pursue the excess through other legal routes. The 50/50 cost-sharing between banks creates an incentive for receiving banks (those that hold fraudsters' accounts) to implement better controls to prevent their accounts being used for fraud, since they now bear direct financial responsibility when they are.

Who the scheme covers

The scheme covers personal customers and micro-enterprises (businesses with fewer than 10 employees and an annual turnover or balance sheet below €2 million). Larger businesses are not covered. The scheme applies to Faster Payments and CHAPS transactions — which together cover the vast majority of UK bank-to-bank transfers. It does not apply to card payments, international wire transfers, or, crucially for many crypto-related cases, direct cryptocurrency transfers.

The PSR published detailed guidance on the scheme requirements at psr.org.uk. The scheme is administered by Pay.UK, the operator of the Faster Payments system.

Does the scheme cover crypto losses?

This is the question that matters most to many victims reading this page, and the honest answer is: it depends on the specific facts of your case, and particularly on which part of the transaction chain you are seeking reimbursement for.

The critical distinction: GBP bank transfer versus crypto transfer

The APP reimbursement scheme applies to payments made through regulated UK payment systems — Faster Payments and CHAPS. It does not apply to transactions on the blockchain. This means:

  • If you transferred GBP from your UK bank account to a crypto exchange via Faster Payments — and that exchange was fraudulent, or if you were deceived into making that payment — the GBP bank transfer may fall within the scheme's scope. You would be claiming for the loss of the GBP, not the cryptocurrency. The transfer of GBP from your bank to the exchange (or directly to a fraudster's UK bank account) via Faster Payments is the regulated payment system transaction that the PSR can cover.
  • If you sent cryptocurrency directly from your wallet to another wallet address — whether you were deceived or not — the scheme does not apply. Blockchain transactions are not Faster Payments or CHAPS. No UK banking regulator has jurisdiction over on-chain crypto transfers, and no mandatory reimbursement framework currently applies to direct wallet-to-wallet transfers.

The "buying crypto" problem

Even in cases where the initial loss involved a GBP Faster Payments transfer to a crypto exchange, banks frequently refuse APP fraud claims on the basis that the customer was "buying crypto" — a voluntary consumer purchase decision — rather than being defrauded into making a payment. This argument is most commonly applied when:

  • The victim made multiple transfers to the same exchange over several weeks or months, suggesting an ongoing investment decision rather than a single induced payment.
  • The exchange receiving the funds is a real, regulated exchange (such as Coinbase, Kraken, or Binance) rather than a fraudulent platform — the fraud occurred within the exchange account, not at the point of the bank transfer.
  • The victim received a warning from their bank at the point of transfer (a "confirmation of payee" mismatch notice, a scam warning overlay, or a customer service call) and proceeded regardless.

Whether the "buying crypto" argument succeeds depends on the specific facts and on how the bank and, if escalated, the Financial Ombudsman Service characterise the transaction. Some FOS decisions have found in favour of victims in crypto investment fraud cases; others have upheld bank refusals. The FOS publishes redacted case decisions on its website at financial-ombudsman.org.uk, which can provide useful context for similar situations.

Where the scheme clearly does not apply

To be direct about the cases the scheme cannot help with:

  • You sent crypto directly to a fake exchange wallet — you never made a GBP bank transfer, only an on-chain transaction.
  • You used a non-UK payment method (international wire, foreign debit card) to fund the fraudulent account.
  • Your losses occurred entirely within a crypto exchange account you held — for example, the exchange itself was hacked or was fraudulent, but you funded the account via a card payment rather than a bank transfer.
Important: Even if the PSR scheme does not apply to your situation, other routes may be relevant — including FCA complaints, Financial Ombudsman referrals for card chargebacks, civil litigation, or on-chain tracing to support a police report. This guide covers the PSR scheme specifically; other routes require separate assessment.

When banks can legitimately refuse

The PSR scheme is mandatory — but it includes defined exceptions under which banks can legitimately decline to reimburse. Understanding these exceptions is important both because it helps victims anticipate bank arguments and because it demonstrates why the facts of each case matter. The principal exception is the consumer standard of caution.

The consumer standard of caution

Under the PSR's rules, a bank can reduce or refuse reimbursement if the victim did not meet the consumer standard of caution. This standard requires the consumer to have:

  • Taken heed of warnings issued by their payment service provider at or before the time of the payment — including automated scam warnings shown during the payment journey, text message warnings, or live calls from the bank's fraud team.
  • Reported the fraud to their bank promptly after becoming aware of it, without unreasonable delay.
  • Cooperated with the bank and, where relevant, the police in any investigation following the fraud.

The "took heed of warnings" requirement is the most commonly invoked ground for refusal in crypto fraud cases. Many UK banks now display prominent scam warnings when customers attempt to transfer money to crypto exchanges, particularly for new payees or high-value transfers. If the bank can demonstrate that it warned the customer that the payment may be part of a scam, and the customer clicked through and proceeded regardless, the bank has a viable ground for refusing or reducing the reimbursement.

Critically, however, the PSR standard requires that warnings must have been clear and unambiguous. A generic "protect yourself from fraud" message at the bottom of a payment screen is very different from a targeted, specific warning that this particular payment pattern is consistent with investment fraud. The Financial Ombudsman has made decisions where generic warnings were not sufficient to deny a claim; the evidence and quality of the warning matters.

Statutory exceptions: when reimbursement is never required

Beyond the consumer standard of caution, the PSR scheme specifies categories of payment that are excluded from mandatory reimbursement:

Exception What this means in practice
Civil disputes A payment for goods or services where the seller has failed to deliver — but there was a genuine commercial relationship. The bank will argue this is a consumer matter for the seller or small claims court, not APP fraud.
Private purchase transactions Payments to a private individual for something you were buying — such as a second-hand car, concert tickets, or private rental deposit — are excluded from mandatory reimbursement even if the seller turned out to be fraudulent.
First-party fraud If the bank has reasonable grounds to believe the customer was complicit in the fraud — for example, if the customer was a "money mule" knowingly moving fraud proceeds — no reimbursement is required.
Gross negligence Banks retain the ability to argue gross negligence in extreme circumstances — distinct from the consumer standard of caution. This is a higher bar and should be rare in practice.

Partial reimbursement

In some cases, a bank may offer partial reimbursement — for example, if it concludes the victim acted on clear warnings for some payments but not others, or if the victim contributed to the loss through delayed reporting. Partial reimbursement is better than nothing, but if you believe the partial offer is unfair, you retain the right to escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service, which will review the bank's assessment independently.

How to make a claim — 5 steps

If you believe the GBP Faster Payments or CHAPS leg of a crypto-related fraud falls within the PSR scheme, the following five steps describe the claims process. Working through these steps systematically and keeping clear records at each stage significantly improves the outcome of any subsequent escalation.

  1. Report to your bank immediately. Contact your bank's fraud team as soon as you realise you have been defrauded. The faster you report, the better. In some cases — particularly where the transfer was very recent — the bank may be able to attempt a recall of the funds before they are moved onward. Reporting quickly also removes one of the bank's potential refusal arguments (delayed reporting). Ask specifically to make an APP fraud claim under the Payment Systems Regulator reimbursement scheme. Get the name of the agent you speak to, the time and date of the call, and request confirmation in writing. Many banks have dedicated fraud claim forms — ask for these.
  2. Report to Action Fraud. Report the fraud to the UK's national fraud reporting centre at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. You will receive a crime reference number (CRN). This number is important — your bank will ask for it during the claims process, and you will need it if you escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service. When reporting, include: the bank account details of the destination account, wallet addresses if crypto was involved, transaction IDs, screenshots of any fraudulent platform or communications, and the full timeline of events.
  3. Submit your APP fraud claim in writing to the bank. Do not rely solely on the initial phone call. Follow up in writing — by email or through the bank's secure message centre — clearly stating: the date and amount of each payment, why you believe it constitutes APP fraud, the Action Fraud crime reference number, and a request for reimbursement under the PSR mandatory scheme. Keeping a written record of your claim means you have evidence of what you submitted and when, which is essential if the bank later disputes the timeline or the basis of your claim.
  4. Gather your evidence. In parallel with the bank claim, compile all available evidence: bank statements showing the transfers, all communications with the fraudster (screenshot everything, including any calls or voice messages if possible), URLs and screenshots of the fake platform, any documents or identification the fraudster provided, the timeline of when you made payments and when you first suspected fraud, and any warnings the bank displayed at the time of payment (screenshot or note these even if you proceeded through them — you will need to address them in your claim). If crypto was involved, gather transaction IDs and wallet addresses for all on-chain activity.
  5. If refused, escalate to the Financial Ombudsman Service. If your bank refuses your claim — whether in full or in part — do not accept that as final. Request the bank's formal final response (deadlock letter). You then have the right to refer the matter to the Financial Ombudsman Service free of charge at financial-ombudsman.org.uk or by calling 0800 023 4567. The FOS is an independent body that reviews disputes between consumers and financial services firms. It has the power to direct the bank to reimburse you. The FOS reviews each case on its specific facts; an FOS referral is not a guarantee of success, but many victims who were refused by their bank have obtained reimbursement through the FOS.
Time limit: You must refer a case to the Financial Ombudsman Service within 6 months of receiving the bank's final response letter. Do not let this deadline pass without acting if you intend to escalate.

For cases where crypto is involved and the on-chain transaction trail is an important part of the evidence, producing a clear, documented record of where funds went after leaving the exchange can strengthen the overall picture of the fraud. This is separate from the bank claim itself — see section 6 below.

Where on-chain tracing fits in

On-chain tracing and the PSR APP reimbursement claim are separate processes that address different parts of the same problem. It is important to understand what each one does and does not do.

What the bank claim addresses

The APP fraud claim to your bank is about the GBP Faster Payments or CHAPS transfer from your bank account. The PSR scheme gives you a right to reimbursement from the sending and receiving bank if that transfer was made because you were deceived. The bank's internal investigation looks at: was this payment an APP fraud payment, did the bank comply with its obligations, and do any exceptions apply? The bank is looking at its own records, the payee account details, and potentially the receiving bank's records — it is not conducting an on-chain blockchain investigation.

What on-chain tracing does

Once GBP leaves your bank account and is used to purchase cryptocurrency on an exchange, the bank claim can potentially cover the GBP leg. But separately, on-chain tracing addresses what happened to the cryptocurrency after it was purchased. An on-chain intelligence report produced by CountWize traces the movement of the cryptocurrency from the point of loss — the wallet address where it was first sent or held after the exchange — through subsequent on-chain hops, following the funds to wherever they currently reside on the blockchain.

This is relevant to the overall picture in several ways:

  • Supporting evidence for the bank claim: an intelligence report documenting the on-chain movement of funds can help establish the fraud narrative — showing that the funds moved immediately through a chain of addresses to a known fraudulent operation, rather than sitting in a genuine investment account.
  • Supporting a police report: Action Fraud intelligence is passed to the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau (NFIB), which uses it to build operational cases. An on-chain report with exchange attribution — identifying that the funds reached a regulated exchange — adds to the intelligence picture and may contribute to a broader law enforcement operation.
  • Supporting civil litigation: if the on-chain trace identifies a destination exchange that holds KYC identity records for the account that received the funds, a solicitor can apply for a Norwich Pharmacal Order (NPO) to compel the exchange to disclose those records. This is a civil legal mechanism available to fraud victims who can identify where their funds went.

Common mistakes victims make

Fraud victims frequently make understandable but consequential errors in the hours, days, and weeks after discovering what has happened. The following are the most common mistakes — and what to do instead.

  1. Waiting too long to report. Many victims take days or weeks to report because they are in shock, because they hope the situation will resolve, or because they are too embarrassed to tell their bank. Every day of delay reduces the prospect of a funds recall, reduces the value of the intelligence to law enforcement, and gives the bank a potential refusal argument. Report to your bank and to Action Fraud as soon as you suspect fraud — even if you are not yet certain. You can update your report as you learn more.
  2. Accepting the first bank refusal. Many victims receive a refusal letter from their bank, assume this is final, and take no further action. This is one of the most consequential mistakes. The Financial Ombudsman Service exists precisely to provide a second, independent review when banks refuse claims. The FOS overturns bank decisions in a significant proportion of cases. A bank refusal letter is the starting point for an FOS referral, not the end of the road. You have 6 months from the bank's final response to refer to the FOS.
  3. Paying a "recovery fee" to a secondary scammer. After a fraud, some victims are approached by firms or individuals claiming to be specialist recovery companies, solicitors, or government agencies who can retrieve their funds — for an upfront fee. This is a recovery scam. It is a second fraud targeting the same victim. The FCA has published extensive warnings about recovery scams at fca.org.uk/scamsmart. No legitimate firm charges an upfront fee to recover fraud losses. If you are approached in this way, do not pay anything; report the approach to Action Fraud and to the FCA.
  4. Deleting evidence. In the aftermath of discovering a fraud, victims sometimes delete conversation histories, uninstall apps, or close accounts associated with the fraud — often because seeing the material is distressing. All of this material is evidence. Preserve everything: screenshots, transaction records, conversation logs, platform URLs, any documents or photographs shared by the fraudster. Evidence that has been deleted may be irretrievable and may significantly weaken a bank claim, FOS referral, or police report.
  5. Not making a formal police report. Reporting to Action Fraud is not the same as making a formal police report, but it does enter the national fraud intelligence system and generates a crime reference number. Some victims do not report at all because they believe nothing will come of it. While individual APP fraud cases rarely result in a police prosecution, the intelligence contributes to broader operations, and some exchange disclosure requests and civil legal actions do produce results. The crime reference number is also required for your bank claim and FOS referral.
