What is a crypto recovery scam?

A crypto recovery scam is a secondary fraud that targets people who have already lost cryptocurrency to an initial scam. Having already experienced one loss, victims are understandably desperate to recoup their funds — and fraudsters exploit that desperation with precision. The fraudster poses as a specialist recovery firm, a law enforcement-linked agency, a blockchain forensics company, or even a legal professional, and promises to trace and retrieve the victim's cryptocurrency. They typically charge an upfront fee, and then disappear without providing any service.

Recovery scams are not marginal. The FCA has published multiple warnings about them, and Action Fraud receives a substantial and growing volume of reports each year. UK reporting of recovery scam fraud has increased dramatically alongside the rise in the underlying crypto fraud epidemic — by some estimates, reports have surged by more than 1,400% year-on-year as the total pool of crypto fraud victims has grown. Every new wave of investment scam, romance fraud, and pig-butchering operation generates a corresponding wave of recovery scam targeting of those same victims, sometimes within days of the original loss.

How victims are targeted

Recovery scam operators use several methods to identify and contact victims. Some purchase or steal contact lists from other fraudulent operations — a victim's contact details may be sold between criminal networks after the original fraud. Others scrape victim reports from public forums, Reddit threads, consumer review sites, and social media discussions where people share their experiences of losing cryptocurrency. In some cases, the same criminal group that ran the original investment scam operates the recovery scam, re-targeting their own previous victims once the initial operation has concluded.

Contact is typically unsolicited. The victim receives an email, a WhatsApp message, a direct message on social media, or a phone call from someone claiming to specialise in crypto fraud investigation. The message often references the victim's specific loss in plausible detail — which adds an air of legitimacy and increases the likelihood the victim will engage. In reality, this detail was obtained from the original fraud, from public victim reports, or was simply guessed based on common fraud patterns.

The UK regulatory context

The FCA has made clear that firms offering cryptocurrency recovery services in the UK are not regulated under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 in the same way as mainstream financial services. However, if a firm claims FCA authorisation, claims to work alongside the FCA or law enforcement in an official capacity, or charges fees for services it does not provide, it may be operating fraudulently and potentially criminally. The FCA maintains a Warning List of unauthorised firms at fca.org.uk/scamsmart, and many fake recovery firms appear on it once enough victims have reported them.

Five warning signs of a fake recovery firm

Fake recovery firms are often professional-looking and persistent. They may have a well-designed website, an official-sounding name, and convincing testimonials (which are fabricated). The following five warning signs are present in the large majority of recovery scam cases reported in the UK. If you encounter any one of them, treat the contact with extreme caution. If you encounter more than one, stop all contact immediately and report to Action Fraud.

  1. Advance fees demanded upfront before any work begins. This is the single most reliable indicator that a firm is operating a recovery scam. A legitimate on-chain tracing firm does not require payment before conducting even a basic case review. If a firm demands payment — in any form, including cryptocurrency, bank transfer, gift cards, or wire transfer — before it has provided you with a specific, verifiable assessment of your case, that is a scam. The fee is commonly framed as a "registration fee," a "government processing charge," an "anti-money laundering compliance deposit," or an "unlock code for your frozen funds." None of these descriptions corresponds to any real regulatory requirement.
  2. Guaranteed recovery promised. No legitimate firm can guarantee the recovery of cryptocurrency. Blockchain transactions are irreversible. Whether funds can be traced, and whether a trace leads to any action, depends on where the funds went, the jurisdiction of any destination exchange, law enforcement priorities, and many other factors outside any firm's control. A firm that promises a specific percentage of your funds will be returned, or that guarantees you will get your money back, is making a claim that is impossible to underwrite honestly. This language is a hallmark of recovery scams — it is designed to appeal to the victim's desperation, not to describe a realistic outcome.
  3. Unsolicited contact made to you after your original loss. You did not seek this firm out — they found you. They may claim to have discovered your details through a "victim compensation scheme," an "FCA enforcement action," a "class action lawsuit," or a cryptocurrency monitoring service. None of these explanations is credible. Legitimate tracing firms do not cold-contact victims. If a firm contacts you out of the blue claiming to have information about your specific case, be extremely sceptical — the most likely explanation is that your contact details were obtained from the original fraudster or from a public victim forum.
  4. Asks for your seed phrase, private key, remote access, or two-factor codes. No legitimate firm involved in on-chain tracing needs access to your wallet's private credentials. On-chain analysis works entirely from publicly available blockchain data — your wallet address, your transaction IDs, and the public ledger are all that is required. Any firm that requests your seed phrase, private key, exchange login password, two-factor authentication codes, or remote access to your computer or phone is not conducting forensic analysis — it is attempting to steal the remainder of your assets. Providing these details to any third party permanently compromises any wallet or account associated with them.
  5. Not verifiable on the FCA Register. If a firm claims FCA authorisation or regulation, you can verify this at register.fca.org.uk. Search for the firm's exact name. If the firm does not appear, or if the name in the Register is different from the firm that contacted you, do not proceed. Even if the firm does not claim FCA authorisation, check the FCA Warning List at fca.org.uk/scamsmart to see whether the firm or its website has already been flagged. Note that absence from the Warning List does not confirm a firm is legitimate — the list is reactive and many scam firms are not yet listed.

How to check the FCA Warning List

The FCA provides two tools that are relevant when evaluating any firm claiming to offer crypto-related services in the UK: the Financial Services Register and the Warning List of Unauthorised Firms. Using both takes only a few minutes and can prevent significant additional loss.

Step 1: Check the FCA Financial Services Register

Go to register.fca.org.uk. Use the search box to enter the exact name of the firm that contacted you, as it appears on their website or in their communications. Search results will show you whether the firm is listed as an FCA-authorised or FCA-registered firm, and if so, exactly what activities it is authorised to conduct. If no results appear, the firm is not FCA-authorised. If results appear under a similar but different name, the firm may be impersonating a legitimate authorised firm — this is a tactic known as clone fraud, and the FCA publishes specific warnings about it.

Step 2: Check the FCA Warning List

Go to fca.org.uk/scamsmart and select "Check a firm" or navigate directly to the Warning List of unauthorised firms. Enter the firm's name and, if available, its website URL. If the firm appears on the Warning List, the FCA has already received reports about it and has assessed it as operating without proper authorisation. Any firm on this list should be avoided entirely.

Step 3: Search independently

In addition to the FCA's own tools, search the firm's name on independent consumer review platforms and fraud reporting sites. Search "[firm name] scam," "[firm name] reviews," and "[firm name] FCA" in a standard search engine. Check the Companies House register at find-and-update.company-information.service.gov.uk to see whether the firm is a registered UK company with a trading history. A firm that cannot be found on Companies House and does not appear on the FCA Register should be treated as highly suspicious.

What to do if a firm is on the Warning List or the Register shows no match

Stop all contact immediately. Do not transfer any money. Report the firm to the FCA Consumer Helpline on 0800 111 6768 or using the ScamSmart reporting tool. Also report to Action Fraud on 0300 123 2040 or at actionfraud.police.uk. If you have already made a payment to this firm, do not send further money — see section 4 of this guide for immediate steps.

Note on clone fraud: Some recovery scam firms operate by cloning the identity of a legitimate FCA-authorised firm — using the same name, registration number, and even copying a real firm's website. Always verify the contact details independently by calling the number listed directly on the FCA Register, not the number provided to you by the firm that contacted you.

What to do if you have already paid a fake recovery firm

If you have discovered that a firm you paid for "recovery" services was itself fraudulent, the most important immediate step is to stop sending money. Many recovery scam operators, once they have secured an initial payment, will return with demands for further fees — framed as additional compliance charges, legal costs, or taxes on the "recovered" funds. Paying these additional demands does not result in any funds being released; it only increases your total loss.

Step 1: Stop all contact and do not send more money

Cease communication with the firm. Block the number, email address, and any messaging app account used to contact you. Do not respond to further messages, however urgent or official-sounding they may appear. If the firm claims that your funds are about to be released and you must pay one final fee to receive them, this is a final extraction attempt — not a genuine payment condition.

Step 2: Report to Action Fraud

Report both the original cryptocurrency scam and the recovery scam to Action Fraud separately if possible. Call 0300 123 2040 or report online at actionfraud.police.uk. You will receive a crime reference number (CRN) for each report — keep these for all subsequent steps. When reporting, include: the name of the recovery firm, its website, all contact details, every communication you received, dates and amounts of all payments made, and the method of payment used.

Step 3: Report the firm to the FCA

Report the unauthorised firm to the FCA Consumer Helpline on 0800 111 6768. The FCA uses these reports to update its Warning List and to assist law enforcement with intelligence on active fraud operations. Even if you believe the firm may already be known to the FCA, reporting ensures your case is on record and contributes to any ongoing investigation.

Step 4: Contact your bank and enquire about a potential chargeback

If you paid by credit card, you may be entitled to a chargeback under Section 75 of the Consumer Credit Act 1974, which provides protection for purchases between £100 and £30,000 where the supplier fails to deliver the service. Contact your credit card provider and explain that you paid for a service that was not delivered. If you paid by bank transfer, contact your bank as soon as possible to report the payment as fraudulent. Under the Payment Systems Regulator's mandatory reimbursement scheme — which came into force in October 2024 — UK banks may be required to reimburse victims of authorised push payment (APP) fraud in certain circumstances. The outcome of any chargeback or reimbursement claim will depend on your specific bank and the payment method used.

Step 5: Keep all communications and payment records

Do not delete any emails, text messages, WhatsApp conversations, or other communications from the fraudulent recovery firm. Do not delete any payment receipts, bank statements, or transaction records. These records are evidence for any subsequent investigation, bank dispute, or legal proceeding. Save copies to a secure location that is separate from any accounts or devices the firm may have had access to.

How on-chain tracing differs from recovery scams

Legitimate on-chain tracing is a forensic analysis service, not a recovery service. The distinction is both practical and legal, and understanding it is essential to evaluating any firm that offers to help with cryptocurrency fraud.

What on-chain tracing is

On-chain tracing analyses the public blockchain ledger to follow the movement of specific cryptocurrency funds from a known starting point — typically the wallet address you sent funds to — through subsequent transactions to wherever those funds currently reside or last appeared on-chain. Every transaction on a public blockchain such as Bitcoin or Ethereum is permanently recorded and is readable by anyone. A tracing exercise can identify address clusters controlled by the same actor, destination addresses at centralised exchanges that hold Know Your Customer (KYC) identity records, and patterns that may be relevant to law enforcement or civil proceedings.

The report is produced by analysis of publicly available blockchain data — no access to your wallet, your private keys, or your exchange account is required or requested.

How to tell the difference in practice

A legitimate firm will: give you a realistic assessment of whether on-chain tracing is likely to produce useful intelligence in your specific case, explain clearly what the report will and will not show, and be transparent about the limits of what tracing can achieve. A fraudulent firm will: make promises about outcomes, demand payment before assessment, and often use urgency tactics to pressure you into paying quickly. If a firm claiming to offer tracing services asks you for any credential — seed phrase, private key, exchange password, two-factor codes, or remote access to your device — it is not conducting forensic analysis. It is attempting to steal from you a second time.

Three realistic outcome scenarios

The following scenarios represent an honest breakdown of what on-chain tracing can produce in practice following a crypto fraud loss. No outcome can be guaranteed — the scenarios below depend on where your funds went and what happened to them after leaving your wallet.

Scenario A: Trace identifies a regulated exchange deposit address

This is the most favourable scenario for a victim pursuing civil or legal action. If on-chain tracing establishes that your funds arrived at a deposit address held at a regulated, KYC-compliant exchange — particularly one operating in the UK, EU, or another jurisdiction with mutual legal assistance arrangements — there is a legal mechanism to pursue identification of the account holder. CountWize produces an intelligence report documenting the exchange attribution; you or your solicitor can then consider a Norwich Pharmacal Order application to compel the exchange to disclose account holder details. Even in this scenario, recovery depends on many further steps: the account holder's identity, their location, available assets, and law enforcement cooperation. CountWize cannot guarantee any of these steps will produce a recovery.

Scenario B: Trace reaches a cold wallet with no exchange link yet identified

In this scenario, tracing establishes the movement of funds to a series of wallet addresses but does not identify a deposit address at a regulated exchange where KYC records would exist. The intelligence report documents the full transaction chain and the current known holding addresses. This report preserves evidence in a format suitable for law enforcement — particularly if other victims of the same operation are reporting, creating a combined intelligence picture that may support a broader investigation. The prospects of any direct civil action are more limited without exchange attribution, but the report has ongoing value as the investigation develops.

Scenario C: Funds dispersed across many wallets or passed through a mixer

This is the most challenging scenario. Some fraudulent operations route funds through many intermediate addresses in rapid succession, pass funds through cryptocurrency mixing services designed to obscure the transaction trail, or convert funds into privacy coins. Tracing can establish the movement up to the point of obfuscation and document the evidence that exists, but the chain beyond that point becomes substantially harder to follow. A written intelligence report is still produced and may be useful to law enforcement as part of a broader investigation, but an honest assessment at the outset of any engagement in this scenario would note that exchange attribution is less likely and the prospects of any direct action are reduced. An honest firm tells you this before, not after, taking any fee.
