What CountWize impersonation fraud looks like

Cryptocurrency recovery firms are a primary target for impersonation fraud. The reason is straightforward: people who have already lost money to one scam are identifiable targets for a second fraud. Impersonators exploit the name of a known, UK-registered firm — such as CountWize — to lend credibility to a fake "recovery" approach. The victim, already distressed, may be more likely to trust a firm they can search online and find to be real.

CountWize Ltd is a UK-registered company (Companies House number 16198508) that provides on-chain tracing as an investigative service. We have a public website, a registered business address, and a published phone number. Fraudsters exploit these real details to make fake approaches more convincing. They may:

  • Contact you by phone, WhatsApp, Telegram, email, or social media, claiming to be a CountWize case handler
  • Reference your previous crypto loss — often obtained from data that was leaked or sold following the original scam
  • Use our real company name, Companies House number, or website address to appear legitimate
  • Create spoofed email addresses that resemble but are not our actual domain (countwize.co)
  • Present documents, case numbers, or "evidence" that appear professional but are fabricated
  • Tell you that a recovery has already been traced and requires only a small upfront fee to release

None of these approaches represents genuine CountWize contact. The real CountWize does not contact people who have not first submitted an enquiry through our official website or called us at the number published here.

Key fact: The original scam you experienced and any subsequent approach claiming to help you recover it are often run by the same criminal network or by affiliated networks that buy victim data from each other. This second-fraud pattern — commonly called a "reload scam" — is documented by the FCA and Action Fraud. Do not assume that an approach claiming to help you is independent of the original fraud.

Warning signs: how to spot a fake

Impersonators use pressure, urgency, and authority signals to push victims into acting before thinking. The following patterns are consistent with impersonation fraud and inconsistent with how the real CountWize operates.

Red flags that indicate impersonation, not real CountWize contact

  • You did not initiate the contact. The real CountWize never makes unsolicited outreach. If we contact you, it is always in response to an enquiry you submitted through countwize.co or a call you made to our published number.
  • Pressure to act quickly or pay before the window closes. Fraudsters create artificial deadlines — "your funds will be moved in 24 hours", "the freeze order expires today" — to stop you from taking time to verify. The real CountWize does not use deadline pressure tactics.
  • A request for an upfront fee before any verified work has begun. The real CountWize does not ask for payment in advance of a confirmed and documented engagement. We do not charge to release funds that are "already traced."
  • A request for payment in cryptocurrency. Legitimate UK service firms accept payment by bank transfer or card. A demand for crypto payment — particularly to a wallet address — is a strong indicator of fraud.
  • A request for your private keys, seed phrase, or wallet access credentials. No legitimate tracing firm needs your private keys. Anyone requesting these is seeking to steal your remaining crypto assets.
  • A request for remote access to your devices. Impersonators may ask you to install software such as AnyDesk or TeamViewer to "verify your wallet." This provides them access to your device and all accounts on it.
  • Contact via WhatsApp, Telegram, or an unofficial email. Official CountWize contact is via support@countwize.co or our published phone number. Contact through unofficial messaging apps or email addresses that do not match this domain is not from us.
  • Claims that your case is "almost closed" or that funds are "ready to release." These claims are designed to make the fraud feel like a near-certain outcome. On-chain tracing does not work this way — there are no funds "held" by us pending a fee payment.
  • Unverifiable credentials or vague answers about the firm. The upfront-fee trap: a common fraud pattern

The most common and financially damaging form of recovery fraud is the upfront-fee scam. Understanding how it works helps you recognise it before any money is transferred.

The fraud operates in a predictable sequence. A fraudster contacts you claiming to represent a recovery firm — sometimes using the name of a legitimate UK firm such as CountWize. They tell you that your stolen cryptocurrency has been traced and is being held at an exchange or in an interim account. To release the funds, they explain, a small fee is required: this might be framed as a tax, a transfer fee, a legal clearance fee, an anti-money-laundering verification charge, or an insurance bond. The amount is typically a fraction of the claimed recovery — designed to seem reasonable relative to the "funds waiting to be released."

Once you pay the initial fee, the fraudster does not disappear. They return with additional fees — each justified by a new bureaucratic obstacle. Many victims pay multiple rounds of fees before the pattern becomes clear. The claimed recovery never arrives. The fees paid cannot be retrieved.

This pattern has the following consistent characteristics that distinguish it from any legitimate engagement:

  • Payment is demanded before any verified work has been agreed or a written engagement letter provided
  • The fee is framed as a necessary precondition for releasing money that is "already there"
  • Urgency is used to prevent the victim from taking time to verify or seek advice
  • The contact was unsolicited — the victim did not approach the firm first
  • Payment is requested in cryptocurrency, bank transfer to a personal account, or gift cards

The FCA's ScamSmart database and Action Fraud both document this pattern extensively. If the contact you received matches any part of this description, do not pay and report immediately using the steps in the section below.

Sources: FCA ScamSmart; Action Fraud UK; Citizens Advice.

If you have already paid someone claiming to be CountWize

If you have paid money to a person or organisation claiming to be CountWize, and you are now concerned that it may have been fraudulent, take the following steps immediately. Speed matters: the faster you act, the greater the chance of limiting further loss or preventing additional payment attempts.

  1. Stop all contact with the fraudster. Do not respond to further messages, calls, or requests for additional payment. Do not explain your reasoning — simply cease responding.
  2. Contact your bank immediately. If you made payment by bank transfer, call your bank's fraud team and ask them to attempt a recall. Under the Payment Systems Regulator's mandatory reimbursement rules, banks are required to reimburse many victims of authorised push payment (APP) fraud. Speed is critical — the longer you wait, the less likely a recall is to succeed. If you paid by debit or credit card, contact your card issuer to dispute the transaction.
  3. Report to Action Fraud. File a report at actionfraud.police.uk or call 0300 123 2040. You will receive a Crime Reference Number, which is required for any bank dispute, legal proceedings, or FCA complaint. Include all contact details, messages, payment confirmations, and any "documentation" the fraudster sent you.
  4. Report the impersonation to us. Contact the real CountWize via the official form at countwize.co/contact-us.html or call +44 7533 598854. We maintain records of known impersonation attempts and can alert other potential victims. We can also confirm to Action Fraud that the contact was not from us.
  5. Report to the FCA. If the impersonator claimed to be FCA-regulated or used this claim as part of their pitch, report it to the FCA at fca.org.uk/scamsmart. The FCA maintains a warning list of known unauthorised firms and updates it based on public reports.
  6. Secure your accounts. If you shared any login credentials, wallet details, seed phrases, or device access with the fraudster, change your passwords and enable two-factor authentication on all relevant accounts immediately. If you installed remote access software, uninstall it and seek IT advice before using the device further for financial transactions.
Important: Having been defrauded by a CountWize impersonator does not mean the real CountWize can assist you in recovering those fees. Recovery of payments made to fraudsters is a matter for your bank and Action Fraud, not for on-chain tracing. We cannot help recover money lost to a fake recovery service. What we can do is confirm to the relevant authorities that the contact was not from CountWize, and provide any information we hold about known impersonation patterns.

How to report a CountWize impersonator

If you receive a contact claiming to be from CountWize that you believe to be fraudulent — even if you have not paid anything — please report it. Doing so helps us track impersonation patterns and alert other potential victims before harm occurs.

Report to CountWize directly

Contact us via the official form at countwize.co/contact-us.html or call +44 7533 598854. Provide as much detail as you can: the phone number, email address, Telegram or WhatsApp username, and any documentation the impersonator sent you. Screenshots are particularly helpful.

Report to Action Fraud

File a report at actionfraud.police.uk or by calling 0300 123 2040. Even if you did not pay anything, a report creates an intelligence record that the National Fraud Intelligence Bureau uses to identify and pursue fraud networks. Most victim data sold between criminal networks comes from prior scam cases — your report contributes to pattern intelligence that may protect others.

Report to the FCA

If the impersonator claimed FCA authorisation or used regulatory language to appear legitimate, report at fca.org.uk/consumers/report-scam-we-are-not-authorised. The FCA ScamSmart warning list is publicly searchable and is updated based on reports received.

Report to the platform where the contact occurred

If the impersonator contacted you via WhatsApp, Telegram, Facebook, Instagram, or LinkedIn, use the platform's built-in reporting tools to report the account. Platforms are increasingly responsive to fraud reporting and can act to suspend accounts before they reach other potential victims.

Frequently asked questions

Someone contacted me claiming to be CountWize. Is this real?

Almost certainly not, if the contact was unsolicited. The real CountWize does not make unsolicited outreach of any kind. If you did not first submit an enquiry through countwize.co or call us at our published number (+44 7533 598854), then any contact claiming to be from CountWize was not initiated by us. Verify by calling us directly on the number published at countwize.co — do not call back the number the contact gave you.

The person knew details about my previous crypto loss. Does that mean they are genuine?

No. Victim data from crypto fraud cases is frequently sold between criminal networks. Fraudsters often contact prior victims with specific details about their previous loss precisely because this knowledge makes the approach seem credible. Knowing the details of your case does not prove the contact is from CountWize or any other legitimate firm. Verify the identity of any contact independently using the steps on this page before taking any action.

I was told CountWize has already traced my funds and needs a small fee to release them. Is this true?

No. On-chain tracing does not work this way. CountWize does not hold your funds in any account or wallet. There are no funds "ready to release" pending a fee payment. Any claim to this effect is a fraud. The real CountWize produces investigative intelligence reports — we do not hold, freeze, or release cryptocurrency assets. If someone is telling you that your funds are ready to release pending a fee, that contact is fraudulent.

What does a genuine CountWize engagement cost?

We do not publish pricing on our guide pages, as fees depend on the complexity of each case and are discussed and agreed in writing before any engagement begins. What we can confirm is that no payment is required before a written engagement letter has been issued, no payment is taken in cryptocurrency, and no fee is contingent on a recovery outcome. If a contact told you a specific fee is required right now before a time-limited window closes, that is not a description of how CountWize operates.

Can CountWize help me recover money I paid to a fraudster claiming to be CountWize?

No — that falls outside the scope of what we do. On-chain tracing is an investigative service for tracing cryptocurrency that was lost in a scam involving blockchain transactions. Recovering bank transfer payments made to fraud impersonators is a matter for your bank (who may be able to recall the payment or apply PSR mandatory reimbursement rules) and Action Fraud. We can confirm to the authorities that the contact was not from CountWize, which may assist your bank dispute, but we cannot directly help recover fees paid to impersonators.

How can I tell if an email is really from CountWize?

Our only official email domain is countwize.co. All genuine CountWize emails come from an address ending in @countwize.co. Any email from a different domain — countwize.com, countwize.net, countwize-uk.com, countwize.org, or any other variation — did not come from us. If you received an email that you believe may be a spoofed or impersonating CountWize address, forward it to support@countwize.co and report it to Action Fraud.

CountWize will never ask you for:
  • Upfront payment before starting a trace
  • Your private keys or seed phrase
  • Remote access to your devices or wallets
  • Payment in cryptocurrency as a condition of service
  • Your exchange login credentials or two-factor authentication codes

Any message claiming to be from CountWize and requesting these is a scam. Report it to us on the number at countwize.co/contact-us.html and to actionfraud.police.uk. Verify our identity before sharing any details.

Who we cannot help

  • Your loss involved cash or bank transfers with no blockchain component — on-chain tracing requires on-chain data to work from.
  • The total crypto lost is under £500 in current value — the cost and effort of a trace is unlikely to be proportionate.
  • The transaction occurred more than 5 years ago with no traceable on-chain activity — older transactions with no recent movement are significantly harder to pursue through legal channels.
  • Your funds were moved exclusively through Monero or other privacy coins with no exchange conversion points — privacy coin transactions are not fully traceable using current methods.
  • You have already paid a third-party firm claiming to be a recovery service, and wish to recover those fees — this is a separate matter outside our scope.
